Honda's India Partnership Hunt Is An Admission, Not A Strategy

By Rajat Dewasi · 25 May 2026

Honda CEO Takashi Nakajima saying the company is actively hunting for an India partner is not a confident growth move. It is an admission that a three-car line-up leaning on one compact sedan cannot deliver the scale Honda wants from this market. The Amaze did 2,856 units in April. The City managed 177. That tells you everything about where Honda actually stands.

The Car Jury verdict

Honda's problem in India is not product quality. It is product breadth and pricing discipline. The City remains a TCJ BUY on merit, and the Elevate is a competent mid-size SUV, but 177 City units in a month is the harsh reality of a sedan-shaped portfolio in an SUV market. The Amaze doing 70 percent of the work is not a strategy, it is a single point of failure.

The creator community is still rooting for the brand. Rachit Hirani of MotorOctane opened a recent video with, "Today we have with us, finally, the Honda City for which we had been waiting for a long time." Biturbo Media called Honda "the best brand for CVT." Goodwill exists. Honda is not converting it because there is no sub-10 lakh hatch, no proper 7-seater, no serious EV, and no aggressive hybrid play despite Faisal Khan of FasBeam noting buyer awareness of the "Honda City Hybrid."

A partnership, likely for platforms or EVs, is the right call. But it is a defensive call. Honda needed this conversation three years ago. Buyers waiting for an Elevate EV or a CNG Amaze should not hold their breath; whatever the partner brings will take 24 to 30 months to show up in showrooms. Verdict: necessary move, late move.

What was announced

Honda Cars India President and CEO Takashi Nakajima has publicly confirmed that the company is open to and actively pursuing partnerships in the Indian market. This is a notable shift for a brand that has historically operated independently in India for over two decades. The statement reflects a clear gap between Honda's stated ambition, treating India as one of its three key global growth markets alongside the US and Japan, and its current on-ground scale.

The April 2026 numbers explain the urgency. Honda sold 4,069 cars in the month, a 21 percent year-on-year jump that looks healthy in percentage terms but modest in absolute volume. The Amaze compact sedan contributed 2,856 units, accounting for more than 70 percent of domestic sales. The Elevate SUV added 1,036 units. The City, once Honda's flagship volume product in India and a category-defining sedan in its prime, managed just 177 units for the month.

Honda's current India portfolio rests on three nameplates: Amaze, City and Elevate. There is no sub-4-metre SUV, no entry hatchback, no 7-seater, no CNG variant of consequence, and no EV on sale. Rivals operate across multiple body styles, fuel types and price bands. Maruti and Hyundai each retail more cars in a week than Honda does in a month. The partnership signal, therefore, is less about ambition and more about a structural need for shared platforms, powertrains or distribution to make the India business viable at the scale Honda's Tokyo leadership wants.
