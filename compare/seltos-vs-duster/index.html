Kia Seltos vs

Choose the Seltos for feature-rich comfort; choose the Duster if the road itself is the destination.

For: This comparison is for buyers spending Rs 15-22 lakh on a primary family SUV who want more than a Creta but do not need a seven-seater. Buyers prioritising diesel, AWD, or maximum rear-seat space should look at the Hyundai Alcazar or Skoda Kushaq instead.

Choose the
Kia Seltos
  • You have a family of four and your spouse insists on rear-seat comfort during 300-km highway runs.
  • You work from the car occasionally and want a triple-screen cockpit with physical AC controls rather than a menu-heavy touchscreen.
  • You want a diesel option because you clock 2,000-plus km a month and long-term fuel costs matter more than upfront price.
  • You live in a city where kerb presence and a premium cabin impress clients or colleagues.
  • You plan to sell in three to four years and want the stronger resale trajectory of an established nameplate.
  • You want one car that covers school runs, highway trips, and mild off-road detours without any single compromise standing out.
Choose the
Renault Duster
  • You drive mountain or coastal roads regularly and want suspension that genuinely absorbs broken tarmac rather than merely tolerating it.
  • You are a solo commuter or couple who finds rear-seat roominess irrelevant compared to how the car feels from the driver's seat.
  • You want the mechanical confidence of a Mercedes-Benz co-developed 1.3L turbo and are happy with petrol-only ownership.
  • You prefer a taller, more upright SUV stance and the 212 mm ground clearance for seasonal waterlogging or village roads.
  • You want 480-litre boot space for luggage-heavy road trips without folding the rear bench.
  • You value a driver-tilted cockpit and Google Built-in infotainment over the widest possible feature list.
Where They Diverge
Four situations that tip the decision

Both score 7.8/10. In real life, they are built for different people.

Long highway drive with family

The Seltos seats four adults without the rear-seat knee-room complaints that follow the Duster on extended journeys. Faisal Khan notes the Seltos wheelbase is 33 mm longer, and rear passengers feel that difference on trips beyond 200 km. The Duster's ride quality is exceptional but its tighter rear bench limits how long passengers stay comfortable.

Edge: Kia Seltos
Twisty state highway or hill road

Faisal Khan calls the Duster 'the new segment benchmark' for ride and handling, and every reviewer who drove it on broken tarmac agrees. The Seltos rides well for a feature-focused SUV but its suspension tuning prioritises comfort over body control. Drivers who enjoy cornering will find the Duster noticeably more rewarding.

Edge: Renault Duster
Resale value in three to four years

The Seltos has held strong residual values through two generations and carries Kia's established service network as a safety net. The new Duster is returning after a four-year absence, and its resale trajectory in this generation is unproven. For buyers who treat resale as part of ownership cost, the Seltos carries lower uncertainty.

Edge: Kia Seltos
Rough village roads or seasonal flooding

The Duster's 212 mm ground clearance beats the Seltos by 12 mm, and its taller body means better water wading on flooded urban roads. V3Cars highlights the thick underbody cladding front and rear as genuinely functional rather than decorative. The Seltos manages most rough roads well but sits closer to the ground and is more cautious territory.

Edge: Renault Duster
Dimension by Dimension
What the jury said, head-to-head

Axis Kia Seltos Renault Duster Best for
Design
The Seltos wears a digital tiger-nose grille, square LED clusters, and a connected LED tail-lamp bar that reads as premium and contemporary. Auto-flush door handles and a hidden rear wiper add polish to the silhouette. At 4,545 mm it is the longest car in the segment and looks proportionate from every angle.
7.8 / 10
The Duster leads with raw SUV stance: muscular cladding, 18-inch alloys, and a 212 mm ride height that commands attention. Faisal Khan notes the Renault badge is absent at the front; the Duster name sits directly on the grille, projecting confident identity. V3Cars calls it muscular rather than sleek, and that distinction matters.
8.0 / 10
Premium urban buyersSeltos reads more polished and contemporary on city streets
Interior
The Seltos cabin is anchored by a 30-inch panoramic trinity display with twin 12.3-inch screens and a 5-inch climate panel. Physical buttons for AC and drive modes make daily use genuinely faster. Soft-touch materials on the upper dashboard and door tops set a quality benchmark for this segment.
8.0 / 10
V3Cars rates the Duster cabin second only to the Seltos in this segment, citing soft leather trim, 48-colour ambient lighting, and a panoramic sunroof. The 10.1-inch touchscreen runs Google Built-in with Chrome, Assistant, and Maps natively. The slightly driver-tilted layout creates a cockpit feel, but screen size and material quality fall just short of the Seltos.
7.5 / 10
Tech-forward familiesSeltos offers more screen real estate and better ergonomic shortcuts
Performance
Faisal Khan clocks the Seltos turbo petrol at 0-100 km/h in 10.6 seconds via the 7-speed DCT. The 160 PS engine is smooth and refined, and the diesel variant adds a 116 PS option for high-mileage users. NA petrol buyers accept a slower 13.7-second run but gain the lowest ownership cost.
7.5 / 10
The Duster's 1.3L turbo produces 160 PS and 280 Nm, co-developed with Mercedes-Benz according to Gagan Choudhary. The extra 27 Nm over the Seltos turbo translates to stronger mid-range pull on overtakes. No diesel is offered, which limits the Duster to petrol-only ownership regardless of buyer need.
7.5 / 10
Diesel or petrol buyersSeltos covers both fuels; Duster wins on petrol torque alone
Ride Quality
The K3 platform brings meaningfully improved ride comfort over the previous Seltos generation. It handles city potholes and highway undulations without harshness, though it does not excite drivers on winding roads. For most family use cases, the ride is more than adequate.
7.8 / 10
Faisal Khan calls the Duster 'the new segment benchmark' for ride and handling, a verdict echoed across every head-to-head review. The suspension absorbs broken tarmac at speeds that expose the Seltos's limits. Buyers who regularly use state highways or hill roads will notice the gap immediately.
9.0 / 10
Road-trip driversDuster's suspension tuning is class-leading on imperfect tarmac
Build Quality
The Seltos scores the segment's highest build quality rating in this comparison. Panel gaps are tight, the K3 platform adds structural rigidity, and all four wheels get disc brakes from the base variant. Interior plastics are appropriately graded, with premium materials where hands and eyes land most.
8.5 / 10
The Duster impresses with its exterior robustness: thick cladding, solid door feel, and a chassis tuned for durability. The manual variant uses drum rear brakes, which is a notable omission at this price point flagged by ASY Cardrive. Interior build is strong but the Seltos edges it on fit-and-finish consistency.
8.0 / 10
Detail-conscious buyersSeltos shows tighter assembly and better brake specification across the range
Value for Money
The Seltos packs the most features per rupee at mid-spec, with the triple screen, physical controls, and segment-leading interior space in one package. Diesel and NA petrol options extend its reach to buyers at both ends of the budget. Resale confidence adds implicit value over a four-year ownership cycle.
7.0 / 10
The Duster justifies its price with a 480-litre boot, 212 mm ground clearance, Google Built-in infotainment, and a Mercedes-developed engine. Dropping diesel and AWD removes two options the original Duster was valued for, narrowing its audience. For buyers who want those items, the Duster's value case weakens noticeably.
7.5 / 10
Feature-maximising buyersSeltos delivers more usable features across more buyer types
Practicality
At 4,545 mm long with a 2,690 mm wheelbase, the Seltos offers genuine rear-seat legroom for taller adults. Boot space is 433 litres, sufficient for a family of four on a weekend trip. Three engine options and a wide variant ladder mean most buyers find a practical fit within the range.
The Duster's 480-litre boot beats the Seltos by 47 litres and handles bulky luggage more comfortably. Its rear seat is tighter, with a shorter wheelbase of 2,657 mm, making it better suited to couples or small families. The taller body and higher ground clearance add practical confidence on roads that challenge lower-slung SUVs.
