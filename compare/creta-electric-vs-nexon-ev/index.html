Hyundai Creta Electric vs
Tata Nexon EV

Hyundai's ownership confidence versus Tata's value-first boldness: pick your priority.

The Car Jury
7 independent creators
April 2026
For: This comparison is built for first-time EV buyers in the 17-23 lakh bracket choosing between a proven brand ecosystem and aggressive value. If you are considering a petrol Creta or a Punch EV, look elsewhere.
Find Your Car
Same price. Different life.

Most buyers decide here. Read this before anything else.

Choose the
Hyundai Creta Electric
  • You live in a city with patchy public charging and want one app to handle payments across 10,000+ stations without carrying multiple cards.
  • You clock 60-plus kilometres daily and need the 473 km long-range pack to eliminate Sunday-night charging anxiety before Monday's commute.
  • You are upgrading from a petrol Creta and want the same service network, familiar cabin logic, and zero relearning curve.
  • You carry elderly parents or young children and value Level 2 ADAS with lane-keep and adaptive cruise for long expressway runs.
  • You run a home business and need V2L to power a laptop, projector, or small appliance at a client site or campsite.
  • You prioritise 7.9-second acceleration for confident highway overtakes and are willing to pay a small premium over the Nexon EV for that margin.
Choose the
Tata Nexon EV
  • You are buying your household's first EV on a tight budget and the roughly 3-lakh price difference lets you install a home charger and still save money.
  • You want Netflix and YouTube on a 12.3-inch screen during charging stops and treat the car's infotainment as a genuine entertainment hub.
  • You frequently road-trip with friends and want V2V capability to top up a fellow EV owner's battery in a pinch.
  • You value a composed, planted ride on broken state highways more than outright sport, and the Nexon EV's balanced suspension suits your daily terrain.
  • You prioritise safety certification and the standard six-airbag setup with reinforced side structure gives you peace of mind without paying for a higher variant.
  • You want a bold, head-turning design with the full-width LED light bar that doubles as a charging-status indicator, a feature that genuinely draws attention on the street.
Where They Diverge
Four situations that tip the decision

Both score 7.8/10. In real life, they are built for different people.

Long highway run, one charge stop

The Creta Electric's 51.4 kWh pack claims 473 km and Hyundai's in-car payment integrates with the largest charging network in India, making unplanned stops far less stressful. The Nexon EV's 465 km claim is competitive, but Tata's public charging footprint remains smaller in tier-2 corridors. For a Mumbai-Pune-Nashik loop without pre-planning, the Creta Electric's ecosystem edge is real.

Edge: Creta
Weekend family budget, total cost of ownership

The Nexon EV starts near 14.49 lakh, a meaningful gap below the Creta Electric's entry point. V3Cars notes the bundled 7.2 kW AC charger on the Nexon EV adds tangible value that buyers would otherwise pay extra for. Over a five-year ownership window, that upfront delta plus lower variant pricing makes the Nexon EV the sharper financial choice for cost-conscious families.

Edge: Duster
Spirited driving, quick urban overtakes

Faisal Khan clocked the Creta Electric's long-range variant at 7.5 seconds in ideal conditions, making it the fastest production Creta ever built. The Nexon EV hits 0-100 km/h in 8.9 seconds with its Gen-2 motor, which is quicker than its predecessor but still a full second behind. Drivers who enjoy a surge of acceleration when merging onto expressways will feel the difference immediately.

Edge: Creta
Resale value, three-year horizon

The Creta nameplate is India's best-selling mid-size SUV and carries strong residual values in the used-car market. Hyundai's service network density and brand trust consistently support higher resale prices for Creta variants versus Tata's electric lineup, which is still establishing long-term residual patterns. Buyers who plan to upgrade in three years should factor this into their effective ownership cost.

Edge: Creta
Dimension by Dimension
What the jury said, head-to-head

Design
Hyundai made 32 visual changes to separate the Electric from the petrol Creta: a closed grille with active air flaps, pixel-pattern bumper, and a front-mounted charging port. Nikhil Rana notes the connected tail lamps and DRL signature read as premium and cohesive from any angle. The overall effect is evolutionary and broadly appealing rather than polarising.
7.5 / 10
The Nexon EV's full-width connected LED light bar front and rear is its defining statement, glowing progressively as the battery charges, a functional showpiece. The EV-specific alloys and air curtain add purpose-built credibility. Car Blogger called it the bolder, more street-forward design of the two.
8.0 / 10
Design-forward buyersNexon EV's light bar creates a stronger visual identity on Indian streets
Interior
The Creta Electric's cabin centres on a free-floating console that frees up genuine floor storage, paired with NFC tap-to-start, cooled under-armrest storage, and a flat-bottom steering wheel with Ioniq 5-inspired detailing. Mudit Bhambri highlighted the shift-by-wire selector and wireless charger placement as thoughtfully integrated rather than bolted on. Material quality across touch points feels consistent.
8.0 / 10
The Empowered variant's blue-and-black cabin with soft-touch upper dashboard and Range Rover-inspired steering wheel punches above its price. The 12.3-inch Arcade.EV screen running Netflix, YouTube, and games via a bundled Jio dongle is genuinely useful during charging stops. Ergonomic quirks noted by Gagan, including switch placement and minor inconsistencies, prevent it from matching the Creta Electric's overall cabin refinement.
7.5 / 10
Refinement seekersCreta Electric's interior execution is more consistently premium across all touch points
Performance
The 171 PS long-range motor delivers 255 Nm and a claimed 7.9-second 0-100 sprint. Faisal Khan recorded 7.5 seconds with optimal conditions, though front-wheel-drive wheelspin limits launches in wet weather. This is the strongest powertrain in the comparison and provides a genuine confidence margin on fast highways.
8.0 / 10
The Gen-2 motor produces 145 PS and 250 Nm, with the 20 kg weight reduction helping the Nexon EV reach 8.9 seconds despite lower peak torque than its predecessor. Top speed rises to 150 km/h. Paddle-shifted regen adds driver engagement, but the performance gap versus the Creta Electric is consistent and measurable.
7.5 / 10
Performance-focused driversCreta Electric's 171 PS motor is in a different tier for highway acceleration
Ride Quality
V3 Cars rated the Creta Electric's ride as composed on good surfaces but noted it transmits more road harshness at low speeds than expected for its price point. Hyundai's suspension tuning prioritises stability over cushioning, which works well on highways but feels firm in slow city traffic over sharp bumps.
7.0 / 10
The Nexon EV's suspension absorbs broken surfaces with more compliance than the Creta Electric, particularly on the potholed state roads common outside metro areas. Gagan noted the low-speed ride is stiffer than the outgoing model but settles into a more rounded character at highway speeds. For buyers whose daily route includes poorly maintained roads, the Nexon EV edges ahead.
7.5 / 10
Mixed-terrain commutersNexon EV's tune handles broken road surfaces with more composure
Build Quality
Hyundai's build consistency is one of the Creta Electric's clearest advantages. Panel gaps are tight, shut lines are even, and the interior feels assembled with precision. Faisal Khan and Mudit Bhambri both noted the absence of squeaks or rattles even on rough surfaces, reinforcing Hyundai's reputation for production-line discipline.
8.0 / 10
Tata has improved the Nexon EV's build with reinforced side structure and upgraded materials, but panel-gap niggles flagged by multiple reviewers including V3Cars remain a known weakness. The car feels solid in motion, but static inspection reveals inconsistencies that buyers at this price point should be aware of before visiting a showroom.
7.0 / 10
Quality-conscious buyersCreta Electric's panel consistency and fit-finish lead the segment
Value for Money
The Creta Electric justifies its premium with ADAS, V2L, 360-camera, in-car charging payments, and a larger battery in the long-range variant. V3 Cars rates the feature-to-price ratio as competitive but notes buyers do pay a clear premium for the Hyundai badge and network. The value is real but requires a higher initial outlay.
7.5 / 10
Starting at 14.49 lakh with a bundled 7.2 kW AC charger, six airbags standard, V2L, V2V, and Arcade.EV infotainment, the Nexon EV delivers a longer feature list per rupee at its entry variants. Gagan pointed out that the charging-equipment inclusion alone saves buyers 30,000 to 50,000 rupees at delivery. For budget-conscious buyers, this is the clearer value proposition.
8.0 / 10
Budget-conscious first buyersNexon EV's pricing and bundled charger create a stronger rupee-for-rupee case
Real-World Range
The 51.4 kWh long-range pack claims 473 km ARAI with Hyundai's integrated charging network covering 10,000-plus stations accessible from the driver's seat. In mixed city and highway use, reviewers estimate 350-380 km of real-world range, which covers most weekly usage patterns without mid-week charging. The 42 kWh standard pack is adequate for urban-only buyers.
The 40.5 kWh pack claims 465 km ARAI, and the air curtain is claimed to add 2% to real-world efficiency. Tata's Tata.ev charging network has grown but remains behind Hyundai's footprint in tier-2 cities. Real-world range lands near 340-360 km in mixed conditions, competitive for its battery size but slightly behind the Creta Electric's larger pack.
Long-distance commutersCreta Electric's larger battery and wider charging network reduce range anxiety on inter-city runs
