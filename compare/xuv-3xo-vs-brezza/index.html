Mahindra XUV 3XO vs
Maruti Brezza

A driver's turbocharged machine versus India's most trusted family hauler.

Choose the
Mahindra XUV 3XO
  • You commute on highways regularly and want a punchy turbo engine that responds when you need to overtake.
  • You have a young family and want the reassurance of the highest safety rating in the segment, including six airbags across most variants.
  • You enjoy driving and want a car that rewards you with a communicative chassis, not just comfortable transport.
  • You prefer a feature-rich cabin and specifically want a dual-screen setup, Harman Kardon audio, or a 65W USB-C port for work.
  • You want a diesel option because your annual mileage makes fuel costs a real concern.
  • You are willing to live with a compact boot and a polarising front face in exchange for more performance and more kit per rupee.
Choose the
Maruti Brezza
  • You are buying your family's primary car and resale value in three to five years matters as much as the purchase price today.
  • You live in a Tier-2 or Tier-3 city where Maruti's service network means a workshop is always within easy reach.
  • You want a relaxed, unfussy daily driver that never demands attention from the engine or gearbox on slow city roads.
  • You are a first-time SUV buyer who wants a simple, dependable ownership experience without worrying about turbo maintenance.
  • You regularly carry four or five adults and need a wider, more comfortable rear bench for longer trips.
  • You plan to keep the car for seven or more years and want the confidence of Maruti's parts availability and low service costs.
Where They Diverge
Four situations that tip the decision

Both score 7.6/10. In real life, they are built for different people.

Weekend highway run with family

The 3XO's 130 hp turbo-GDI petrol makes overtaking effortless and the 6-speed Aisin automatic shifts quickly on demand. The Brezza's 103 PS naturally aspirated engine is refined but requires more planning at highway speeds. Gagan Choudhary noted the 3XO feels genuinely quick where the Brezza feels adequate.

Edge: Mahindra XUV 3XO
Unexpected breakdown in a small town

Maruti's service network is simply unmatched in India, with authorised workshops in towns where Mahindra dealers do not exist. MotorOctane consistently flags this as a decisive real-world advantage for Brezza buyers outside metro areas. For anyone outside a major city, this peace of mind is worth more than any spec-sheet advantage.

Edge: Maruti Brezza
Selling the car after four years

Maruti Brezza holds its resale value better than almost any competitor in this segment, a pattern Namaste Car and MotorBeam both highlight repeatedly. The 3XO carries the uncertainty of a freshly facelifted nameplate, and Mahindra's resale track record in this class remains weaker. Buyers who finance their car and plan to upgrade in a few years absorb a real cost difference at the time of sale.

Edge: Maruti Brezza
Daily city commute in stop-and-go traffic

Both cars use torque converter automatics, but the Brezza's K15C smart hybrid system adds a mild-electric assist that eases stop-start fatigue and improves city fuel efficiency. MotorBeam reports 3XO city figures of 8 to 10 km/l, while the Brezza consistently returns better numbers in identical conditions. The 3XO's cabin is wider and better equipped, which matters on a long daily commute even if the powertrain edge goes to the Brezza.

Edge: Maruti Brezza

Design
Mahindra went for a shock-value facelift with connected DRLs, a wide grille and high-mounted fog lamps that split opinion sharply. Faisal Khan finds the front divisive but agrees the rear and three-quarter view look resolved and planted. If you warm to it, it stands out; if you do not, nothing else changes that.
7.0 / 10
The Brezza facelift sharpens the original without abandoning its boxy SUV identity. LED projector headlamps with dual DRL strips and precision-cut 16-inch alloys give it a genuinely modern look that ages well. MotorOctane called it a clean, cohesive update that reads confidently from every angle.

Interior
The 3XO's dual 10.25-inch screens, dual-zone climate, Harman Kardon audio and 65W USB-C port mark a clear generational leap in this segment. The widest cabin in class means front passengers sit without compromise. MotorBeam notes the stitched leatherette and white-black dual-tone dashboard lift perceived quality noticeably.
7.5 / 10
The Brezza's facelift adds brown inserts, a flat-bottom steering wheel, a colour head-up display and wireless charging, making this its most improved area. The 9-inch SmartPlay Pro+ screen is responsive and easy to use daily. Namaste Car points out rear space is class-competitive, though the overall ambience falls short of the 3XO's dual-screen setup.

Performance
The 1.2-litre turbo-GDI produces 130 hp and 230 Nm, making it the most powerful engine in the sub-4m SUV segment. The 6-speed Aisin automatic is smooth and well-calibrated, and MotorBeam confirms the overboost function delivers a 250 Nm surge on demand. The 1.5 diesel variant adds 300 Nm for buyers who prioritise torque.
8.0 / 10
The 1.5-litre K15C with 12V mild hybrid assistance produces 103 PS and 136 Nm, refined and unstressed at city speeds. The new 6-speed automatic is a genuine improvement over the old 4-speed unit. Gagan Choudhary notes the engine feels confident within city limits but runs out of enthusiasm at triple-digit highway speeds.
6.5 / 10
Driving enthusiasts3XO's turbo gap over the Brezza is the widest in any scored dimension
Ride Quality
The 3XO rides on a well-sorted suspension tune that absorbs broken city roads confidently while staying composed on highways. MotorInc highlighted the ride as one of the most impressive aspects of the facelift, noting it handles sharp bumps without unsettling passengers. The driver-focused setup does firm up slightly at low speeds on very bad roads.
8.0 / 10
The Brezza returns the same class-leading ride comfort score, with a tuning focused entirely on passenger compliance. Namaste Car found the suspension soaks up rough roads with minimal cabin intrusion, making long family trips genuinely fatigue-free. Neither car wins clearly here; both earn the same score for good reason.
8.0 / 10
All passenger typesBoth cars ride equally well across real Indian road conditions
Build Quality
Panel gaps on the 3XO are tight and the structural rigidity reflects its five-star Global NCAP foundation. The interior plastics mix hard and soft surfaces, but the overall feel is a step above the outgoing XUV 300. MotorBeam noted a few interior trim pieces that feel average for the price, keeping the score from going higher.
7.5 / 10
Maruti's build quality on the Brezza is consistent and dependable rather than impressive. Panel gaps are acceptable and the cabin materials are functional, though hard plastics dominate at this price point. The strength of Maruti's build reputation comes from long-term durability data, not first-impression luxury.
7.5 / 10
Buyers valuing longevityBoth score equally; Maruti's track record adds long-term confidence
Value for Money
Starting at Rs 7.49 lakh and offering 130 hp, dual screens, Harman Kardon audio and class-leading safety at mid-variant prices, the 3XO packs an exceptional feature-to-rupee ratio. MotorInc and Faisal Khan both called the AX5 and AX7 variants the sweet-spot buys in the segment. The diesel option adds further versatility for high-mileage buyers.
8.0 / 10
The Brezza is not the cheapest on paper, but its lower insurance costs, higher resale value and cheaper service intervals reduce the total ownership cost meaningfully over five years. MotorBeam factored in these running costs and found the Brezza more economical to live with than its sticker price suggests. Buyers who calculate cost per kilometre over five years often find the Brezza more competitive than it first appears.
7.0 / 10
Feature-first buyers3XO delivers more kit per rupee at purchase; Brezza wins on total cost of ownership
Practicality
The 3XO's widest-in-class cabin benefits front occupants most, but the small boot remains its most cited limitation. Reviewers consistently note it struggles with airport luggage for four adults. The lack of a CNG option and the compact load bay make it a tighter fit for large-family use.
The Brezza offers a more balanced practical package with a usable boot, a comfortable rear bench for three adults and a CNG option in lower variants for budget-conscious buyers. Gagan Choudhary noted the rear seat width and floor space make it the more natural choice for families of four or five. For buyers who prioritise everyday usability, this gap matters.
