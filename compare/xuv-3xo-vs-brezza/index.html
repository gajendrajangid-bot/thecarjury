Mahindra XUV 3XO vs
Maruti Brezza

Choose the 3XO for driver thrills; choose the Brezza for family peace of mind.

For: This comparison is for first-time SUV buyers spending Rs 10-14 lakh who want a practical daily car and cannot decide between performance and reliability. If you need seven seats or a diesel automatic, look at the Nexon or Creta instead.

Most buyers decide here. Read this before anything else.

Choose the
Mahindra XUV 3XO
  • You commute solo or with one passenger and want a car that feels genuinely quick when you need to overtake on a two-lane highway.
  • You prioritise safety above all else and want the highest crash-test scores in the segment for your family.
  • You are buying a mid-spec variant and want the most feature-per-rupee return, including dual 10.25-inch screens and a Harman Kardon system.
  • You want a diesel option because your monthly driving crosses 2,500 km and fuel costs matter more than badge value.
  • You enjoy driving and want an SUV that responds when you push it, not one that merely gets you there.
  • You park in tight urban spots daily and want the widest cabin in the segment without stepping up to a longer car.
Choose the
Maruti Brezza
  • You are buying your family's primary car and a five-year ownership cost lower than almost any rival matters more than outright performance.
  • You live in a smaller city or town where Maruti's service network means a workshop is never more than a few kilometres away.
  • You plan to sell the car in three to four years and want the segment's strongest resale value working in your favour.
  • You regularly carry rear passengers on long trips and want a car that does not punish them with a stiff ride or a cramped footwell.
  • You or a family member is a nervous driver who wants a smooth, predictable automatic with no turbo surge and a car that feels unthreatening in traffic.
  • You want a simple, one-fuel, low-maintenance ownership story with no DPF or turbo complexity to think about.
Where They Diverge
Four situations that tip the decision

Both score 7.6/10. In real life, they are built for different people.

Weekend highway run with family

The 3XO's 130 hp turbo-GDI pulls confidently at triple-digit speeds, making overtakes feel effortless. The Brezza's 103 PS naturally aspirated engine runs out of breath above 100 kmph and needs planning ahead of trucks. For a family covering 300 km every other Sunday, the difference in effort is real.

Edge: Mahindra XUV 3XO
Five-year total cost of ownership

Maruti's service network, spare part prices and fuel efficiency keep the Brezza's running costs among the lowest in the segment. MotorBeam notes the 3XO's turbo-GDI returns 8-10 km/l in city use, while the Brezza's mild-hybrid system stretches that figure further in stop-and-go traffic. Resale rates on the Brezza consistently outperform most rivals at the three-year mark.

Edge: Maruti Brezza
Rear-seat comfort on long drives

The Brezza offers more rear legroom and a better-shaped bench for three adults on long journeys. The 3XO's cabin is the widest in the segment but rear legroom is tighter, and the recline angle is steeper than ideal for multi-hour stretches. Namaste Car flagged the 3XO's rear seat as comfortable for two but cramped for three adults.

Edge: Maruti Brezza
Spirited driving through ghats or hill roads

The 3XO's chassis traces its lineage to the SsangYong-derived platform that earned the XUV 300 its driver-focused reputation, and the steering weights up predictably on winding roads. The Brezza steers with less feedback and the naturally aspirated engine lacks the punch needed to carry speed out of tight corners. Faisal Khan called the 3XO's driving dynamics the segment's best-kept secret.

Edge: Mahindra XUV 3XO

What the jury said, head-to-head

Design
Mahindra replaced the forgettable XUV 300 face with connected DRLs, a wide grille and high-mounted fog lamps that provoke strong reactions. The rear LED light bar and planted stance read well in traffic. Faisal Khan finds the front design grows on you, though several reviewers admit it polarises.
7.0 / 10
The Brezza facelift sharpens the car without abandoning its upright, boxy identity. LED projector headlamps with dual DRL strips and precision-cut 16-inch alloys look genuinely modern. Gagan Choudhary notes it is the safer, broader-audience design of the two.
7.5 / 10
Style-conscious urban buyersBrezza plays it safe and pleases more crowds from the kerb
Interior
The 3XO's dual 10.25-inch screens, dual-zone climate control, Harman Kardon audio and 65W USB-C charging represent the richest cabin in the segment at this price. The white-and-black stitched dashboard feels a full class above the outgoing XUV 300. MotorInc called it the most pleasant sub-4m interior to sit in.
7.5 / 10
The Brezza's updated cabin gains a 9-inch SmartPlay Pro+ screen, a head-up display, a flat-bottom steering wheel and ambient lighting, which lift it meaningfully over the previous generation. The brown inserts add warmth, though the overall material quality and screen size still trail the 3XO. Namaste Car rates the layout practical and intuitive for everyday use.
7.0 / 10
Tech-first buyers3XO's dual screens and premium audio set a higher bar
Performance
The 1.2-litre turbo-GDI's 130 hp and 250 Nm overboost make it the segment's most powerful engine, paired with a smooth 6-speed Aisin automatic. MotorBeam records 8-10 km/l city and 12-14 km/l highway returns. The 1.5 diesel adds 300 Nm of torque for buyers who cover big distances.
8.0 / 10
The K15C's 103 PS and 136 Nm are adequate in the city but require patience on highways. The new 6-speed automatic is a clear improvement over the old 4-speed unit and shifts smoothly in traffic. MotorOctane notes the engine is refined and stress-free but uninspiring when pushed hard.
6.5 / 10
Enthusiast commuters3XO's turbo-GDI is in a different league for outright pace
Ride Quality
The 3XO absorbs broken city roads and highway undulations with composure, a trait the XUV 300 platform was always respected for. High-speed stability is strong and the suspension filters most sharp edges without crashing through. Gagan Choudhary rated the ride among the best in the sub-4m class.
8.0 / 10
The Brezza rides with a similar suppleness and is equally composed over bad roads, with a slightly more planted feel at lower speeds that suits cautious drivers. MotorBeam notes rear-passenger comfort is particularly good on potholed city roads, thanks to a longer wheelbase than several rivals.
8.0 / 10
All road conditionsBoth are class leaders; the Brezza's rear comfort edges it for families
Build Quality
Panel gaps and fit-and-finish on the 3XO are noticeably tighter than the XUV 300 it replaced. The reinforced safety cell supports five-star safety ratings and the doors shut with a solid thud. MotorBeam found no major quality concerns across the range.
7.5 / 10
The Brezza carries Maruti's reputation for consistent, dependable build quality across tens of thousands of units. Panels are even and the cabin materials wear well over time, even if they do not impress on first touch. Arun Panwar notes long-term reliability data on the platform is extensive and reassuring.
7.5 / 10
Long-term ownershipBrezza's proven reliability record tips the balance for cautious buyers
Value for Money
Starting at Rs 7.49 lakh, the 3XO's mid-spec variants deliver dual screens, premium audio and a powerful turbo engine at prices that undercut feature-equivalent rivals. The AX7 trim in particular bundles safety and technology that competes with cars costing two lakh more. Namaste Car called the mid-range 3XO the segment's best-value proposition.
8.0 / 10
The Brezza's value case rests on total ownership cost rather than showroom spec sheets. Lower service costs, better fuel economy, stronger resale value and near-zero downtime across a nationwide network make it cheaper to own over four to five years. MotorOctane notes that rational buyers consistently land on the Brezza when they calculate the full five-year picture.
7.0 / 10
Upfront feature seekers3XO wins at launch price; Brezza wins over the ownership period
Practicality
The 3XO's boot is a known limitation at 256 litres, which fills up quickly on a family trip. The cabin width compensates for rear-seat adults, but families with luggage will feel the squeeze. The sunroof, USB-C charging and wireless charger add daily convenience that matters in urban use.
The Brezza offers more boot space and a better-shaped rear bench that genuinely accommodates three adults. MotorOctane highlighted the practical loading lip and flexible rear seat as everyday advantages for family buyers. If you regularly carry bulky items or travel with in-laws, the Brezza's practicality is the easier choice.
