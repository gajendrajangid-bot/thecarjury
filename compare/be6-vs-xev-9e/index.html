Mahindra BE6 vs
Mahindra XEV 9e

Driver's EV or family tech flagship: two very different reasons to go electric.

Same price. Different life.

Most buyers decide here. Read this before anything else.

Choose the
Mahindra BE6
  • You commute solo or with one passenger most of the week and the drive itself is part of the reward.
  • You want your car to stop traffic and are happy explaining to strangers that yes, it is a Mahindra.
  • You live in a metro with a home charger and rarely need more than 400 km between charges.
  • You are buying this as your primary car and rear-seat passengers are occasional, not daily.
  • You prioritise sharp steering and sporty dynamics over a pillowy, isolated ride.
  • You want maximum performance and drama at the lowest possible entry price in the segment.
Choose the
Mahindra XEV 9e
  • You regularly carry three adults in the rear and need a genuinely comfortable back bench.
  • You want the most feature-rich cabin in the segment and treat the car as a rolling lounge.
  • You take long highway trips and want a smoother, more relaxed cruising character.
  • You are buying a second family car and comfort consensus across all occupants matters more than driver thrill.
  • You are willing to wait 6-12 months for software to mature before taking delivery.
  • You want the larger 79 kWh pack and the bragging rights of triple screens without moving to a premium European brand.
Where They Diverge
Four situations that tip the decision

Both score 8.0/10. In real life, they are built for different people.

Long highway run with the family

The XEV 9e's longer wheelbase and more upright roofline translate to a noticeably more relaxed rear-seat experience on a six-hour drive. The BE6's firm semi-active dampers, praised by MotorOctane for canyon carving, begin to wear on passengers over broken highway sections. For a family of four on a weekend trip, the 9e simply causes less fatigue.

Edge: Mahindra XEV 9e
Spirited driving on a winding ghat road

The BE6's rear-wheel-drive layout, low centre of gravity and sharp steering give it a genuinely playful edge through corners. Faisal Khan called the design 'straight out of the year 2050' and the dynamics match that ambition. The 9e's powertrain is smooth and capable, but it is tuned for linear confidence rather than driver involvement.

Edge: Mahindra BE6
Living with software and ownership day to day

Both cars run Mahindra's new INGLO-era software, but reviewers including V3Cars and Namaste Car flag more persistent UI niggles on the 9e at launch. The BE6 is not without its own software quirks, but the 9e's three-screen ecosystem adds more variables that need time to stabilise. Early 9e buyers are, in effect, beta testers.

Edge: Mahindra BE6
Fitting the car into a parking-constrained urban life

The XEV 9e stretches to 4.8 metres and nearly 1.9 metres wide, which makes tight city parking bays and narrow lanes a genuine daily consideration. The BE6 is more compact and, despite its visual drama, easier to place in urban traffic. City-first buyers should factor this size difference in before signing.

Edge: Mahindra BE6

What the jury said, head-to-head

Design
MotorOctane notes the BE6 attracts supercar-level attention at a local market. Manish Bhardwaj's owner reports people in Rajasthan mistook it for a supercar and were shocked to learn it was a Mahindra. The coupe-SUV silhouette is singular and deeply intentional.
9.0 / 10
The 9e has genuine road presence with a full-width LED light bar, flush handles and connected tail lamps that look premium after dark. But the sloping coupe roof divides opinion, and several reviewers consider it the less resolved design of the two.
7.0 / 10
Statement seekersBE6 draws the stronger, more unanimous crowd reaction
Interior
The BE6's cockpit is built around the driver: twin 12.3-inch screens, a thrust-lever selector and a halo surround create genuine theatre. RushLane praises the sports-car seats and the AR Rahman-tuned Infinity roof. The 16-speaker 1400W Harman Kardon system is a legitimate highlight.
7.5 / 10
Three 12.3-inch screens span the 9e's dashboard, including a dedicated passenger display for streaming. The Harman Kardon setup with Dolby Atmos is outstanding, and the cabin feels more democratic in its comfort than the driver-biased BE6. Passengers in every seat feel catered for.
8.0 / 10
Passenger-first families9e's three-screen layout and rear comfort serve all occupants equally
Performance
282 BHP, 0-100 in 6.7 seconds and rear-wheel drive make the BE6 the more exciting machine on paper and in practice. The semi-active dampers and sharp steering give it a dynamic identity that feels genuinely different from other EVs in this price band.
8.5 / 10
The 9e's 281 BHP Pack 3 matches the BE6 nearly on numbers, clocking 0-100 in 6.8 seconds. Mahindra has tuned it for linear, confidence-inspiring delivery across Range, Everyday and Race modes rather than outright drama. It is quick, just not theatrical.
8.0 / 10
Enthusiast driversBE6's RWD layout and sharper tune reward drivers who seek feel, not just speed
Ride Quality
The BE6's semi-active dampers earn praise on smooth roads and mountain passes, but reviewers consistently flag a firm character on broken city surfaces. It is a deliberate performance trade-off, but it limits comfort for daily rear-seat passengers.
7.0 / 10
The 9e's longer wheelbase and softer suspension tune produce a more settled, isolated ride. At 218 mm ground clearance and with a more upright stance, it absorbs urban potholes without the firmness that defines the BE6. It is the more comfortable daily cruiser.
7.5 / 10
Comfort-first commuters9e's tune suits mixed Indian road conditions without compromising composure
Build Quality
Panel gaps and fit-finish are acceptable for the price, and RushLane notes the generous use of soft-touch materials inside. The usual Mahindra question marks around long-term service quality remain valid until the ownership base grows and real-world data accumulates.
7.5 / 10
The 9e's build quality carries similar caveats. V3Cars and Namaste Car both note that software and feature execution feel less polished at launch than the hardware itself deserves. The INGLO platform is structurally sound, but the finish is not yet European-benchmark tight.
7.0 / 10
Pragmatic early adoptersTie: both carry the same Mahindra new-platform caveats
Value for Money
Starting at Rs 18.90 lakh ex-showroom, the BE6 delivers 282 BHP, semi-active dampers and a 16-speaker audio system at a price that undercuts most rivals with half its performance. Faisal Khan and MotorOctane both describe the pricing as disruptive. The entry point alone makes this a difficult car to argue against on value.
8.5 / 10
The 9e's three screens, Dolby Atmos audio and 79 kWh battery pack into a package that humbles competitors at twice the price, as multiple reviewers note. It costs more than the BE6 but delivers more cabin and more range. The value case is strong if the software matures quickly.
8.0 / 10
Entry-point EV buyersBE6's starting price is the segment's most aggressive proposition
Range and Charging
Real-world range of 450-500 km from the BE6 is confirmed across MotorOctane and RevLimits testing. Fast charging brings the battery to usable levels quickly enough for highway pit stops. For most urban and mixed-use buyers, the range comfortably covers a week of driving.
The 79 kWh Pack 3 gives the 9e a comparable or marginally larger buffer on long highway runs, and the larger battery suits buyers who do regular intercity travel without charging confidence. The 59 kWh variant trades some range for a lower entry cost into the 9e lineup.
