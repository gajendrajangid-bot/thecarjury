Choose the

  • You carry three adults on the rear seat regularly and cannot compromise on legroom or shoulder room.
  • You work from cafes or co-working spaces and appreciate a near-flat floor and reclining seats that double as a lounge between meetings.

Where They Diverge
Four situations that tip the decision

Both score 7.8/10. In real life, they are built for different people.

Weekend highway run of 250 to 350 km

The Windsor Pro's 52.9 kWh pack delivers real-world range of 350 to 380 km, and King Indian confirmed 280 to 290 km of easy city range on a charge. The Punch EV's 40 kWh long-range pack returns roughly 300 to 350 km in practice. For a round trip without a midpoint charge, the Windsor gives a larger buffer.

Edge: Mg
Daily parking in tight urban societies

At 3.8 metres long and 1.7 metres wide, the Punch EV slots into gaps that would make a Windsor driver nervous. Namaste Car highlighted the near-90-degree door opening as a genuine quality-of-life feature in basement parking. The Windsor's 4.3-metre length and 1.85-metre width are real constraints in older urban layouts.

Edge: Tata Punch EV
Total cost of ownership over five years with BaaS

AllAboutEVs broke down the BaaS math clearly: Windsor's per-kilometre battery fee sits at 3.9 to 4.5 rupees on top of charging costs, while Punch EV's BaaS rate is approximately 2.6 rupees per kilometre. Buyers who own the full battery upfront on either car avoid this entirely. Long-term, BaaS erodes the EV running-cost advantage more on the Windsor than on the Punch.

Edge: Tata Punch EV
Family road trip with luggage and three rear passengers

The Windsor's 604-litre boot, flat floor, and 135-degree reclining rear seats make it the obvious choice for a family of four or five on a long drive. The Punch EV's boot and narrower rear bench mean a third adult in the rear is genuinely cramped. MotorOctane noted that no rival at this price band matches the Windsor's cabin volume.

Edge: Mg

Design
The Windsor's stepped bonnet and MPV-crossover silhouette is polarising by any measure. MotorOctane acknowledges it is not conventionally pretty, but the near-flush handles and fixed panoramic roof give it a distinctive street presence. Buyers who want a crowd-pleaser should know it provokes strong opinions.
7.0 / 10
The Punch EV wears a clean closed-off grille, sequential DRLs, and diamond-cut 16-inch alloys that read as a proper compact SUV. Its proportions are tidy and inoffensive, which Faisal Khan noted makes it an easy family consensus choice. It looks exactly like the segment expects.

Interior
The Windsor's 15.6-inch touchscreen, 8.8-inch driver display, flat floor, and 135-degree reclining rear seats set the interior benchmark for this segment. V3Cars rates the rear space as class-leading, and the lounge-like layout is genuinely different from any other car at this price. Hard plastics on lower panels are the main caveat.
8.5 / 10
The Punch EV's dual 10.25-inch screens, ventilated front seats, 65W USB-C fast charger, and wireless charging pad make it feel punching above its segment. The dual-tone beige and black dashboard looks genuinely upmarket. However, Gagan Choudhary and others flagged patchy execution: the seat ventilation switch placement and some panel gaps undercut the premium first impression.

Performance
The Windsor's 136 PS and 200 Nm motor is smooth and well-suited to highway cruising. It is not a quick car by EV standards, and Team Car Delight noted it feels more comfort-tuned than sporty. Adequate for city use; unremarkable when pressed on a ramp.
7.5 / 10
The Punch EV long-range makes 122 PS and 190 Nm, hitting 100 kmph in around 9.5 seconds. Arun Panwar noted Sport mode sharpens throttle response noticeably. The base 82 PS variant is genuinely underpowered and best avoided.
7.5 / 10

Ride Quality
The Windsor rides on a comfortable, long-wheelbase setup that absorbs urban potholes and highway expansion joints smoothly. MotorByte noted it feels planted at highway speeds. At 7.0 on ride, it is not the sharpest handler but serves its comfort brief well.
7.0 / 10
The Punch EV scores 8.0 on ride, the highest in this comparison, and its 190 mm ground clearance means it takes broken roads and speed breakers in stride. Namaste Car specifically praised its ability to handle rough urban terrain without cabin disturbance. It is the more capable urban tool on imperfect roads.
8.0 / 10

Build Quality
The Windsor scores 7.0 on build, with soft-touch materials on the upper dashboard but mixed quality lower down. Shut lines and panel gaps are acceptable but not class-leading. Long-term MG reliability data in India is thinner than Tata's, which is a consideration for five-year ownership.
7.0 / 10
The Punch EV scores 6.5 on build. Gagan Choudhary and Faisal Khan both called out inconsistent panel gaps and a few interior cost-cuts that feel out of place given the feature list. Tata's service network and parts availability are strengths, but the physical product quality needs improvement.
6.5 / 10

Value for Money
The Windsor scores 8.5 on value, delivering genuine MPV space, a 15.6-inch screen, panoramic roof, and class-leading rear comfort from approximately 15 lakh on-road. MotorOctane considers it hard to beat on a per-feature and per-square-metre basis. BaaS buyers should do long-term cost math before committing.
8.5 / 10
The Punch EV scores 7.5 on value. At 9.99 lakh entry, it democratises EV ownership more than any rival. The long-range variant at around 13 to 14 lakh stacks up features that embarrass cars costing far more. Per-kilometre running costs favour outright battery ownership over BaaS.
7.5 / 10
Feature-per-rupee huntersWindsor packs more cabin into each rupee at mid-spec
Real-World Range
The Windsor's 38 kWh pack delivers 240 to 280 km in city conditions. The 52.9 kWh Pro pack stretches to 350 to 380 km in real-world use, King Indian confirmed 280 to 290 km from a typical daily cycle. For users who spend significant time on highways, the Pro pack is the only sensible choice.
The Punch EV's 40 kWh long-range pack returns 300 to 350 km in practice against a claimed 421 km. AllAboutEVs put real-world expectations at 300 to 350 km. The 30 kWh version is better suited to pure city loops. Both packs use LFP chemistry, which holds charge better over years of use.
