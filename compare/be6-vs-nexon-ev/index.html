Most buyers decide here. Read this before anything else.

Choose the
Mahindra BE6
  • You are the sole daily driver and rear-seat passengers are occasional, not permanent fixtures in your commute.
  • You live in a metro with a home charger and treat highway runs as events, not weekly obligations.
  • You want strangers to stop and stare at your car the way Manish Bhardwaj's owner Harry experienced in Rajasthan.
  • You value a sharp, connected steering feel and sports-car acceleration more than a cosseting, quiet ride.
  • You are comfortable being an early adopter with a newer service network, in exchange for segment-leading technology.
  • You stretch to the higher variants and want the 282 BHP, 1400W Harman Kardon system and semi-active dampers as a package.
Choose the
Tata Nexon EV
  • You regularly carry four adults and need a rear seat where knees and heads both clear without negotiation.
  • You want a car that has accumulated years of owner data, a dense service network and predictable long-term costs.
  • You charge mostly at public DC stations and appreciate the faster 56-minute 10-100% time for real-world top-ups.
  • You need V2L capability to power appliances during trips or outages, a practical feature your family will actually use.
  • Your driving involves a mix of city crawls and state-highway runs where a compliant, forgiving ride matters most.
  • You are buying your first EV and want the reassurance of India's best-selling electric car behind your decision.
Where They Diverge
Four situations that tip the decision

Both score 8.0/10. In real life, they are built for different people.

Weekend highway run, solo or with one passenger

The BE6's 282 BHP rear-wheel-drive platform and semi-active dampers reward a driver who wants to feel the road. MotorOctane noted the car feels planted and alive at triple-digit speeds in a way few mass-market EVs attempt. The Nexon EV is capable and composed, but its 145 PS motor is tuned for efficiency, not theatre.

Edge: Mahindra BE6
Family road trip with two adults and two children

Rear knee room in the BE6 is a genuine compromise; reviewers consistently flag the coupe roofline as the cost of that silhouette. The Nexon EV's upright SUV body gives rear occupants usable headroom and shoulder space across long distances. V3Cars rates the Nexon's rear habitability as one of its strongest practical arguments.

Edge: Tata Nexon EV
Charging stop on a toll-highway with no home charger

The BE6 accepts 175 kW DC charging and reaches 80% in approximately 20 minutes, making pit stops shorter on longer corridors. The Nexon EV's DC capability is more modest, taking around 56 minutes for a full charge from 10%. For users dependent on public infrastructure, the BE6's charging speed is a tangible time advantage.

Edge: Mahindra BE6
Three-year resale and total cost of ownership

The Nexon EV has the largest installed base of any Indian electric car, which means a deeper used-car market, more independent service options and better-understood depreciation curves. The BE6 is newer and its resale trajectory is unproven. Buyers who sell at the three-year mark carry more uncertainty with the Mahindra.

Edge: Tata Nexon EV

Design
Faisal Khan called the BE6 'straight out of the year 2050', and no reviewer disagreed. MotorOctane observed that owners cannot park at a local market without drawing a crowd. The aerodynamic coupe-SUV silhouette is unlike anything else in the Indian mass market.
9.0 / 10
The 2025 facelift gives the Nexon EV a full-width connected LED light bar that doubles as a live charging-status indicator, a genuinely clever and distinctive detail. The overall shape is a sharper version of a familiar compact SUV, attractive but recognisably mainstream. Car Blogger notes the design is confident without being polarising.
8.0 / 10
Statement-seekersBE6 commands attention the Nexon EV simply cannot match
Interior
RushLane praises the sports-car-like seats, twin 12.3-inch free-standing screens and the AR Rahman-tuned Infinity roof with interactive ambient lighting. The 1400W Harman Kardon system and augmented-reality HUD complete a cabin that feels theatrical by design. Rear space is the single honest caveat.
7.5 / 10
The Empowered variant's blue-and-black leather-like upper dashboard and flat-bottom steering wheel lift the Nexon EV's interior well above its price point. Arcade.EV brings Netflix, YouTube and games to the 12.3-inch screen via a bundled Jio dongle. Gagan notes the ergonomic layout still has minor quirks, but day-to-day usability is strong.

Performance
282 BHP, rear-wheel drive and a 0-100 time of 6.7 seconds place the BE6 in a different performance class than any rival near its price. The low centre of gravity and sharp steering mean the numbers translate into a genuinely engaging drive, not just a spec-sheet flex. RevLimits confirms the chassis backs up the power.
8.5 / 10
The Gen-2 motor produces 145 PS and reaches 100 km/h in 8.9 seconds, quicker than the outgoing model despite 38 Nm less torque, thanks to a 20 kg weight reduction. Three drive modes and paddle-shifted regeneration give the driver meaningful control. V3Cars notes it feels brisk in city conditions, which covers most of its use cases.
7.5 / 10
Driver-focused buyersBE6's RWD platform is in a separate league for driving engagement
Ride Quality
The semi-active dampers help, but multiple reviewers flag the BE6's ride as firm on broken urban surfaces. RushLane notes the setup is clearly tuned for handling composure rather than passenger comfort. At highway speeds it settles, but city potholes are felt by all occupants.
7.0 / 10
The Nexon EV's low-speed ride is described as stiff by V3Cars, a recurring theme across the platform. At city speeds over sharp bumps it is not plush, but it is more forgiving than the BE6 across a wider range of road conditions. Gagan notes it absorbs typical Indian B-road surfaces with greater compliance.
7.5 / 10
Mixed-road commutersNexon EV absorbs real-world Indian roads with less complaint
Build Quality
The BE6 uses soft-touch materials generously and the INGLO platform feels structurally solid. RushLane is positive on tactile quality inside. The open question is long-term durability and service consistency, an area where Mahindra's newer platforms carry less of a track record than its critics would like.
7.5 / 10
Panel-gap niggles appear in Car Blogger's review and are a known talking point among Nexon EV owners. Tata's assembly quality has improved across generations but has not yet reached the point where reviewers stop mentioning it. The reinforced side structure added for 2025 is a meaningful safety upgrade.
7.0 / 10

Value for Money
Starting at Rs 18.90 lakh ex-showroom for 282 BHP, semi-active dampers, a 1400W audio system and 450-500 km real-world range is disruptive pricing by any measure. RushLane and MotorOctane both flag the BE6 as redefining what is achievable at its price point. The value case weakens if you frequently carry rear passengers.
8.5 / 10
At Rs 14.49 lakh entry, the Nexon EV includes a 7.2 kW AC charger as standard, six airbags, V2L and V2V capability, and Arcade.EV. V3Cars rates this bundling as excellent for the segment. The four-lakh price gap versus the BE6 is real money that buys school fees, not just bragging rights.
8.0 / 10
Budget-conscious familiesNexon EV's lower entry point and standard features suit tighter budgets
Real-World Range and Charging
MotorOctane and RevLimits confirm 450-500 km of real-world range from the larger battery pack, backed by 175 kW DC charging that hits 80% in roughly 20 minutes. For buyers doing intercity travel, this combination meaningfully reduces range anxiety. The charging network for high-power DC remains thinner outside major corridors.
The 40.5 kWh battery claims 465 km ARAI; real-world figures typically land lower, around 350-380 km in mixed use. DC charging to full takes around 56 minutes, and the standard 7.2 kW AC charger makes overnight home charging straightforward. V2L adds genuine utility for camping or power cuts, something the BE6 does not offer.
