Tata Harrier EV vs
Tata Curvv EV

Choose between a serious electric performance SUV and a value-first coupe for city buyers.

For: This comparison is for buyers with Rs 18-29 lakh to spend on an EV who want more than a Nexon but are not sure whether to stretch for a bigger battery and a proper body-on-frame platform. If you need a seven-seater or petrol fallback, look elsewhere.

Most buyers decide here. Read this before anything else.

Choose the
Tata Harrier EV
  • You live 80-plus kilometres from the nearest highway charger and need a larger battery to commute without range anxiety.
  • You drive on broken state highways or occasionally take trails and want genuine SUV clearance and a 600 mm wading depth.
  • You have a family that values rear-seat comfort over a stylish roofline and cannot live with a compromised headroom.
  • You want the fastest electric SUV in this price band and enjoy the 390 hp surge when overtaking on the expressway.
  • You are buying a second car for the household and want a lifetime battery warranty to lock in long-term ownership costs.
  • You travel frequently on Mumbai-Pune or Delhi-Jaipur corridors and want the Harman JBL Dolby Atmos setup for long drives.
Choose the
Tata Curvv EV
  • You live in a metro, charge overnight at home and a genuine real-world 450 km is more than enough for your week.
  • You are 32 and care about how your car looks parked outside a restaurant; the raked roofline genuinely turns heads.
  • You are buying your first EV and want a lower entry price with the flexibility of the smaller 45 kWh pack.
  • You drive mostly solo or with one passenger and do not need the rear headroom that the coupe roofline sacrifices.
  • Your monthly instalment ceiling is tight and the Curvv's stronger value score means you get more feature content per rupee.
  • You want a car that feels light and flickable in traffic rather than a 2,300 kg SUV that telegraphs its mass.
Where They Diverge
Four situations that tip the decision

Both score 7.5/10. In real life, they are built for different people.

Long expressway run with DC charging stops

The Harrier EV's 75 kWh battery and 120 kW DC charging rate mean a longer buffer before you need a charger, and shorter stops when you do find one. The Curvv EV tops out at 70 kW, and its 55 kWh pack means more frequent stops on a Delhi-to-Jaipur run. For buyers who treat the expressway as a weekly commute, the Harrier EV's charging ceiling is a meaningful practical edge.

Edge: Tata Harrier EV
Weekend trail or flooded road crossing

Namaste Car highlighted the Harrier EV's 25.3-degree approach angle and MotorBeam confirmed it completed Rajmachi's off-road trail without drama. The Curvv EV is a front-wheel-drive coupe-SUV on the acti.ev platform; it has no off-road pretension and Tata does not position it for anything beyond a muddy parking lot. If your drive occasionally includes broken mountain roads, this is not a close comparison.

Edge: Tata Harrier EV
First EV purchase on a monthly budget

The Curvv EV's starting price of Rs 17.49 lakh undercuts the Harrier EV by nearly Rs 4 lakh, and V3Cars rates its value for money at 8.0 versus 7.5 for the Harrier. Gagan Choudhary noted that the Curvv's lower range variant overlaps in price with the top Nexon EV, making the upgrade math easier. For a first-time EV buyer watching EMIs, the Curvv simply goes further per rupee.

Edge: Tata Curvv EV
Daily city driving and parking in tight spots

At 4.6 metres long and roughly 2,300 kg, the Harrier EV demands wider parking bays and more planning in dense urban areas. The Curvv EV is lighter, and its coupe silhouette reads more manageable in traffic. Faisal Khan found the Curvv genuinely brisk in Sport mode with sufficient punchiness for city use, without the weight penalty that makes the Harrier EV feel deliberate at low speeds.

Edge: Tata Curvv EV
Dimension by Dimension
What the jury said, head-to-head

Design
The Harrier EV wears a closed grille, connected LED DRLs with sequential indicators and 19-inch aero alloys, but Biturbo Media noted it shares its silhouette directly with the diesel car. The QWD badging and subtle tweaks signal EV identity without fully committing to one. It reads as authoritative rather than exciting.
7.5 / 10
The Curvv EV is India's first mainstream coupe-SUV and the rear three-quarter is genuinely distinctive, with a sharply raked roofline, connected LED tail lamps and an integrated spoiler. Gagan Choudhary called the front largely familiar from the Nexon, but the overall shape earns attention on the road.
8.0 / 10
Design-led younger buyersCurvv's coupe roofline is a deliberate style statement the Harrier cannot match
Interior
The Harrier EV delivers a 14.53-inch Samsung NeoQLED screen, a 10-speaker JBL Dolby Atmos system, ventilated memory front seats and a genuinely premium dashboard mix of soft-touch materials. V3Cars flagged rough edges in the software, but the material quality step over the Curvv is real and noticeable on first contact.
7.5 / 10
Step inside the Curvv EV and Namaste Car put it plainly: it feels like a Nexon EV in nicer clothes. The dashboard, AC controls and door pads are carried over wholesale, with only the steering wheel and upholstery shade as differentiators. The 12.3-inch Harman screen is crisp, but the interior does not feel like a new-generation product.
7.0 / 10
Buyers who spend hours insideHarrier EV's material quality and audio system justify the premium for long-haul users
Performance
The QWD Harrier EV pairs a front induction motor with a rear permanent-magnet motor for 390 hp and 504 Nm, with a claimed 0-100 km/h in 6.3 seconds. MotorBeam found the instant torque genuinely useful in Mumbai traffic and on the old Mumbai-Pune highway. This is the fastest mainstream electric SUV at this price in India.
8.5 / 10
The 55 kWh Curvv EV produces 167 PS and 215 Nm, claiming 0-100 km/h in 8.6 seconds. MotorOctane noted it feels brisk in Sport mode with traction control working hard off the line. It is quick enough for city use and comfortable highway cruising, but it is in a different performance category from the Harrier EV's dual-motor setup.
7.5 / 10
Performance-focused driversHarrier EV's 390 hp dual-motor system is in a different league at this price
Ride Quality
Land Rover-derived underpinnings give the Harrier EV a composed, mature ride that absorbs broken tarmac without drama. Gagan Choudhary and Namaste Car both found the suspension well-calibrated for Indian road conditions, managing the car's 2,300 kg weight without feeling floaty at speed.
8.0 / 10
The Curvv EV rides on the acti.ev platform and delivers a competent, comfortable city ride. V3Cars found it handles Mumbai's broken roads well at normal speeds, though the lighter platform does not isolate large impacts with the same authority as the Harrier's heavier underpinnings.
7.5 / 10
Mixed-road daily commutersHarrier EV's platform absorbs rough patches with noticeably more composure
Build Quality
The Harrier EV carries a 5-star Bharat NCAP rating and a lifetime battery warranty, and the body panels feel substantial. Namaste Car noted that panel gaps and material quality are a step ahead of Tata's older products, though some cabin trim pieces still feel below European benchmark expectations.
7.5 / 10
The Curvv EV scores 6.5 on build quality in the Jury's assessment, the lowest dimension score in this comparison. Faisal Khan and V3Cars both flagged Tata's familiar quality niggles: inconsistent panel fits, shared interior plastics with the Nexon and switchgear that does not feel generationally new.
6.5 / 10
Long-term ownership buyersHarrier EV's higher build score and lifetime battery warranty reduce long-run ownership uncertainty
Value for Money
At Rs 21.49-28.99 lakh, the Harrier EV packs 390 hp, a large battery, Land Rover platform origins and a lifetime battery warranty. V3Cars rates its value at 7.5, acknowledging the strong feature set but noting that buyers pay a clear premium for the performance and platform.
7.5 / 10
Starting at Rs 17.49 lakh, the Curvv EV scores 8.0 for value. Gagan Choudhary highlighted that the Curvv's pricing overlaps strategically with the Nexon EV's top variant, making it an easy upgrade decision. For buyers not needing dual motors or a 75 kWh battery, it delivers a stronger value equation.
8.0 / 10
Budget-conscious EV upgradersCurvv delivers more usable features per rupee for buyers who do not need the Harrier's performance tier
Real-World Range
The Harrier EV's 75 kWh battery returns 380-420 km in mixed real-world driving, per MotorBeam's testing. That buffer makes it a credible intercity car without obsessive charging planning. The 120 kW DC charging ceiling means a meaningful top-up takes roughly 40 minutes at a fast charger.
The Curvv EV's 55 kWh long-range pack claims 585 km but delivers a tested real-world figure closer to 450 km in mixed conditions. Gagan Choudhary's range test confirmed it outperforms the Nexon EV significantly. For city-primary buyers, 450 km is more than sufficient, though the 70 kW DC charging cap extends pit stops on long trips.
Intercity highway travellersHarrier EV's larger battery and faster DC charging rate give it the edge on routes where chargers are spread far apart
