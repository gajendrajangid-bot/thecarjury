Maruti Brezza vs
Tata Nexon

Maruti's reliability-first ownership case versus Tata's performance and safety credentials.

Choose the
Maruti Brezza
  • You live in a Tier-2 city where the nearest Tata service centre is 40 km away and a Maruti workshop is around the corner.
  • You are buying your first car and want the lowest total cost of ownership over five years, including resale.
  • You drive mostly in the city and value a light, easy automatic that does not demand rev-matching or anticipation.
  • You are a family of four prioritising a smooth, refined cabin on long highway runs over outright acceleration.
  • You want a well-rounded feature set without the anxiety of an untested powertrain or ergonomic surprises.
  • You plan to sell the car within four years and want the most predictable residual value in the segment.
Choose the
Tata Nexon
  • You have a long daily highway commute and want a diesel automatic that sips fuel and pulls cleanly at 100 kmph.
  • You drive in the hills occasionally and need torque on tap from a turbocharged motor, not a naturally aspirated unit.
  • Safety ratings are non-negotiable for you and a 5-star Global NCAP score settles the argument before you even test-drive.
  • You want the segment's strongest CNG option and refuse to sacrifice driveability for running costs.
  • You are a design-conscious buyer who wants a cabin that looks and feels a generation newer than the segment average.
  • You carry four adults regularly and want ventilated front seats and a panoramic sunroof to justify every long weekend.
Where They Diverge
Four situations that tip the decision

Both score 7.4/10. In real life, they are built for different people.

Daily city commute in stop-and-go traffic

The Brezza's 6-speed torque converter automatic is smoother and more forgiving in crawling traffic than the Nexon's AMT or even its DCA at low speeds. MotorOctane noted the Brezza's refined NVH at idle makes city driving genuinely relaxed. The Nexon's turbo-petrol AMT can feel jerky in slow queues, and Faisal Khan called the low-speed DCA response "slightly abrupt" in urban conditions.

Edge: Maruti Brezza
Weekend highway run with full passenger load

The Nexon's turbo-petrol produces 120 PS and 170 Nm, giving it a measurable overtaking advantage over the Brezza's 103 PS naturally aspirated unit. Arun Panwar noted the Nexon diesel feels especially effortless on expressways past 80 kmph. The Brezza keeps pace comfortably at highway speeds but requires more throttle to sustain momentum when fully loaded.

Edge: Tata Nexon
Resale and long-term ownership cost

Maruti's resale advantage in the sub-4m SUV segment is structural, not anecdotal. Namaste Car pointed out that Brezza owners consistently recover 60-65 percent of purchase price at the three-year mark. Nexon resale has improved since the 5-star NCAP rating, but Maruti's service density keeps the Brezza's running cost lower in most Indian cities.

Edge: Maruti Brezza
Mountain or ghat driving with passengers

The Nexon diesel's 260 Nm of torque handles sustained climbs without protest, and Biturbo Media rated it the segment's most capable powertrain in elevation. The turbo-petrol CNG also punches harder than any factory CNG rival. The Brezza's 1.5-litre NA motor manages the task but MotorBeam noted it requires downshifting earlier and more often on steep gradients.

Edge: Tata Nexon

What the jury said, head-to-head

Design
The facelift gives the Brezza a cleaner, more confident face with LED projector headlamps and dual DRL strips. Gagan Choudhary noted the reduced chrome makes it look more premium than before. The boxy silhouette is familiar but the precision-cut alloys and reworked C-pillar keep it from feeling dated.
7.5 / 10
The Nexon wears the sharpest Tata design language yet, with tri-arrow DRLs and a connected LED tail-lamp strip that makes it unmistakable in traffic. MotorBeam called it "the most resolved-looking Tata" in this segment. The Union Jack tail-lamp graphic divides opinion but the overall stance reads genuinely SUV-premium.

Design-first buyersNexon commands more presence and modernity at the kerb
Interior
Maruti's cabin update brings brown layered inserts, a flat-bottom leather steering and a 9-inch SmartPlay Pro+ with wireless CarPlay. Namaste Car appreciated the HUD and ambient lighting as genuine additions for the price. Plastics are acceptable but the overall interior does not feel class-leading.
7.0 / 10
The Nexon gets a dual 10.25-inch screen setup, a three-tone dashboard, a panoramic sunroof and ventilated front seats on top trims. Faisal Khan praised the cabin's premium feel relative to its price point. The touch climate panel draws criticism for usability while driving.
7.5 / 10
Tech-focused familiesNexon's larger screens and panoramic roof feel a step ahead
Performance
The 1.5-litre K15C with 12V mild hybrid produces 103 PS and 136 Nm, smooth and refined but lacking urgency. MotorOctane confirmed the new 6-speed automatic is a meaningful upgrade over the old 4-speed. Above 80 kmph with a full load, the engine sounds busy and progress slows noticeably.
6.5 / 10
The Nexon's 120 PS turbo-petrol and 260 Nm diesel give buyers two genuinely capable options. Arun Panwar rated the diesel automatic the most satisfying drivetrain in the segment for highway use. Even the turbo-petrol CNG at 100 PS outruns every other factory CNG available today.
7.5 / 10
Enthusiast driversNexon's turbo and diesel options simply pull harder
Ride Quality
The Brezza rides on a well-sorted suspension that absorbs broken urban roads confidently. MotorBeam rated it one of the smoothest sub-4m SUVs in its class over patchy surfaces. High-speed stability is composed and the cabin stays quiet on good tarmac.
8.0 / 10
The Nexon matches the Brezza in ride suppleness and handles bad roads with equal confidence. Biturbo Media noted the 209 mm ground clearance gives it a slight edge on broken village roads and waterlogged urban stretches. Both cars are genuinely well-tuned here.
8.0 / 10
Mixed road usersBoth cars handle Indian roads with equal competence
Build Quality
Brezza panel gaps are consistent and the doors shut with a solid feel. Gagan Choudhary noted Maruti has improved perceived quality noticeably over the pre-facelift car. The 5-star Global NCAP rating it once held has lapsed under newer protocols.
7.5 / 10
The Nexon holds the only active 5-star Global NCAP rating in its price band, and Tata's structural reputation has strengthened considerably since its first crash test. MotorBeam praised the door sills and panel consistency on the facelifted car. For buyers who see safety as part of build quality, this gap is decisive.
7.5 / 10
Safety-conscious buyersNexon's 5-star NCAP rating is the segment's strongest safety case
Value for Money
The Brezza's value is structural: low service costs, wide dealer coverage, high resale and a feature list that covers daily needs without excess. Namaste Car pointed out that the total 5-year cost of ownership is lower than most rivals at the same purchase price. It asks nothing of you beyond fuel and scheduled maintenance.
7.0 / 10
The Nexon's 36-variant range means a buyer can enter at Rs. 8 lakh or build to Rs. 15.26 lakh, matching almost any budget. The diesel and CNG options add genuine running-cost flexibility that the Brezza cannot match. MotorBeam noted that top-spec Nexon trims pack more hardware per rupee than the equivalent Brezza.
7.5 / 10
Value seekersBrezza wins on long-term cost; Nexon wins on features per rupee
Practicality
The Brezza offers a usable boot, comfortable rear knee-room for two adults and a simple, intuitive layout that requires no learning curve. MotorOctane found the flat rear floor and wide door apertures make it easier to load child seats than most rivals. The lack of diesel or CNG on top trims limits flexibility for high-mileage users.
The Nexon's 382-litre boot, rear AC vents and wide powertrain choice make it genuinely practical for varied buyer needs. Faisal Khan noted the turbo-CNG option alone opens the car to a buyer profile no other compact SUV currently serves. Rear headroom is adequate but taller passengers will find the sloping roofline tighter on long trips.
High-mileage familiesNexon's diesel and CNG options serve more real-world use cases
