Kia Sonet: The Jury's Verdict

The 2025 Sonet facelift fixes its biggest pain points with better rear space, a more usable turbo petrol and improved ride, making it one of the most complete sub-4m SUVs if you pick the right variant.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 6 May 2026 Synthesis of 7 independent sources 1,998 words · 8 min read

The 2025 Kia Sonet facelift takes an already style-led sub-4m SUV and gives it the substance it was missing: a roomier rear bench, a more tractable turbo petrol, retuned suspension and Level 1 ADAS. Prices stretch from around Rs 9.99 lakh ex-showroom for the HTX iMT petrol to nearly Rs 18 lakh on-road for a loaded diesel automatic. Pick the right variant and it is one of the most rounded packages in its segment.

What Works

  • Sharp, mature design with premium LED lighting front and rear
  • Improved rear thigh support (up 21 mm) and 30 mm more knee scoop space
  • Refined 1.0 turbo petrol; smooth diesel automatic for highway runs
  • Loaded with features including 360 camera, ventilated seats and ADAS
  • Physical AC and steering buttons retained, easier to use on the move

Watch Out For

  • Top-end diesel automatic crosses Rs 18 lakh on-road, dangerously close to Seltos territory
  • No wireless Android Auto/CarPlay on the larger 10.25-inch screen
  • Cabin still feels narrow; rear seat tight for two tall adults back-to-back
  • Variant matrix is confusing and waiting periods on some trims are long

Design

The facelifted Sonet doubles down on what the original did best, which is look expensive for the money. Up front, the connected DRL signature drops down dramatically into reshaped headlamps with three reflector LED chambers, while the slim slit-like fog lamps on the top trims add genuine drama. The X-Line goes further with matte Peter Olive paint, blacked-out detailing and red brake calipers, and as Biturbo Media points out the painted calipers can look aftermarket rather than factory-finished. At the rear, the new connected tail lamp bar is the cleanest update, paired with a subtly extended spoiler. Wheels are 16-inch crystal-cut alloys on top trims, wrapped in 215/60 R16 MRF Wanderer tyres, which itself is an upgrade over the older JK rubber. Even the rear parking sensors are angled inward to compensate for number plate placement, the kind of detail that signals a manufacturer paying attention. It is, in this segment, the design benchmark alongside the Nexon.

Interior & Features

Inside, the Sonet finally feels like it belongs at the top of the sub-4m class, but with caveats. The dual 10.25-inch screens dominate a redesigned dashboard that thankfully retains physical AC and steering buttons, a deliberate choice that makes daily use far less distracting than the touch-capacitive trend elsewhere. Material quality is consistent, the leatherette upholstery in beige-black or sage green looks rich, and the front seats are 4-way power adjustable with ventilation. The bigger story is at the back: Kia has added 21 mm of thigh support and 30 mm of knee scoop space, and a 5'8" passenger behind a similar-height driver now sits in genuine comfort. Push to two six-footers and it gets tight again, and the cabin still feels narrower than a Sierra or 3XO. Boot space is a healthy 385 litres, sun shades are standard at the rear, and the air purifier with PM 2.5 readout is a nice touch.

Performance & Powertrain

Three engines carry over but the tuning tweaks matter. The 1.0-litre turbo petrol making 118 hp now feels more usable in traffic, with reduced turbo lag in Normal mode and a more linear delivery; switch to Sport and the old urgency returns. The 7-speed DCT is smooth and the paddle shifts are quick. The 1.2 naturally aspirated petrol remains the low-maintenance, low-power workhorse for budget buyers. The 1.5 diesel makes 114 hp and 250 Nm and is the pick for highway-heavy users; with the 6-speed torque converter automatic it is genuinely refined, while the 6-speed iMT is the value sweet spot, quieter and more efficient. As MotorBeam notes, real-world fuel efficiency has dipped slightly post BS6 Phase 2, but the diesel still returns 18-20 kmpl on highways with a light foot. The turbo petrol returns roughly 14-16 kmpl on the highway and 9-11 kmpl in mixed driving. Buyers doing under 1,000 km a month should avoid the diesel due to DPF risks.

Ride Quality & Handling

The retuned suspension is the most underrated upgrade. The Sonet now rides with more compliance over broken patches and sharp expansion joints, and the switch to MRF Wanderer tyres has noticeably reduced the thumping that earlier JK-shod cars suffered from. Namaste Car finds the balance is now neither too stiff nor too soft, a sweet spot for Indian roads. At low city speeds it can still feel firm over really uneven surfaces, and the goldilocks pairing is the diesel automatic, where the heavier engine settles the chassis better. Handling stays composed at highway speeds without any vague steering, and there is a slight added weight in the steering even outside Sport mode. The Sonet is not an XUV 3XO in dynamics, but it now flows through corners with more confidence than before. Braking has improved with all-four disc brakes on the top trims, and the pedal feel is progressive without sudden bite. Wind and tyre noise are well contained up to 100 km/h.

Build Quality & Technology

Fit and finish are among the best in the segment. Panel gaps are tight, plastics feel consistent in grade and there are no rough edges in the cabin. The tactile feedback on switches, the click of the AC dials and the meaty action of the gear lever all suggest careful engineering. Kia's 15-feature robust safety package is now standard across variants and includes 6 airbags, ESC, vehicle stability management, hill-start assist, all-four-disc brakes on higher trims and a high-line TPMS. Level 1 ADAS adds forward collision warning and avoidance, lane-keep assist, lane-departure warning, driver attention warning and leading vehicle departure alert. As Gagan Choudhary cautions, structural crash safety is yet to be independently verified for this generation, though Kia says it goes to Bharat NCAP. Connected car tech via Kia Connect spans 70-plus features, the Bose 7-speaker setup sounds good once you switch to Original Sound mode, and the wireless charger has dedicated cooling vents. Wireless Android Auto and CarPlay are oddly missing on the larger screen.

Price & Value

Pricing is where buyers must do their homework. The HTX 1.0 iMT turbo petrol opens at Rs 9.99 lakh ex-showroom, with Arun Panwar reporting an on-road price of Rs 11.35 lakh in Gurgaon for a real owner. The HTX diesel manual is Rs 10.20 lakh ex-showroom. The fully loaded X-Line diesel automatic crosses Rs 18 lakh on-road, which is uncomfortably close to a base Seltos and worth pausing over. The HTX+ trim, especially in the Peter Olive non-GT colour, looks almost loaded and is the sensible value pick for most buyers. Compared to a Hyundai Venue it is more lavishly equipped, and against the Mahindra XUV 3XO it offers stronger powertrains and better cabin quality, though the 3XO is sharper to drive. The Sonet's segment-leading feature count, 3-year unlimited-km warranty extendable to 5, and 3-year roadside assistance soften the sticker shock. Pick the right variant and it is value-rich; chase the X-Line top trim and the math gets harder.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • Rear seat space and thigh support are meaningfully improved over the pre-facelift, though it is still not class-leading on knee room
  • The 1.0 turbo petrol with the DCT is now easier to drive in the city, with less turbo lag in normal mode
  • Ride quality has been retuned softer and feels more confident on bad roads, especially on the new MRF Wanderer tyres
  • The connected LED tail lamps, redesigned bumpers and X-Line matte paint make it one of the best-looking sub-4m SUVs
  • Feature list is segment-leading: 360 camera, ventilated seats, Bose audio, air purifier, Level 1 ADAS, 6 airbags and TPMS as standard

Points of Disagreement

  • Whether the cabin feels genuinely spacious: some find the rear bench transformed, others still call it tight for two six-footers
  • Whether the diesel automatic is worth its near 18 lakh on-road price when a Seltos becomes accessible at that money

TeamBHP's Take

TeamBHP

TeamBHP's owner forum repeatedly flags the Sonet's variant maze and long waiting periods on specific trim-colour combinations as the biggest practical hurdle, while ownership reports back the improved rear seat comfort and refined turbo petrol post-facelift. Long-term owners caution against the 1.0 DCT for high-stress city use without the extended warranty, and recommend the iMT for buyers who want the turbo petrol experience with lower long-term ownership risk.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Kia Sonet?
Yes, if you want a feature-rich, well-finished sub-4m SUV with strong powertrain choices. The HTX 1.0 iMT turbo petrol or HTX+ diesel iMT offer the best value. Avoid the diesel automatic top-end if a Seltos fits your budget.
What is the Kia Sonet price in India?
The HTX 1.0 iMT turbo petrol starts at Rs 9.99 lakh ex-showroom and the HTX 1.5 diesel manual is Rs 10.20 lakh ex-showroom. A real-world on-road price for the HTX iMT in Gurgaon is around Rs 11.35 lakh, while a fully loaded X-Line diesel automatic crosses Rs 18 lakh on-road.
What are the main problems with the Kia Sonet?
Cabin width still feels narrow, the variant matrix is confusing, wireless Android Auto and CarPlay are missing on the 10.25-inch screen, top-end pricing is steep, and the X-Line is locked to a single colour scheme.
How is the Kia Sonet mileage?
The 1.0 turbo petrol returns roughly 14-16 kmpl on the highway and 9-11 kmpl in the city. The 1.5 diesel manual or iMT can deliver 18-20 kmpl on highways with a light foot, while the diesel automatic returns around 14 kmpl in the city.
Is Kia Sonet good for highway driving?
Yes. The diesel automatic and the 1.0 turbo petrol DCT both feel composed at high speeds, with retuned suspension that flows confidently through corners. Wind noise is well controlled up to 100 km/h, and the cabin stays comfortable on long runs.
How does Kia Sonet compare to rivals?
Against the Hyundai Venue it is more lavishly equipped and better looking. Against the Mahindra XUV 3XO it has stronger powertrains and cabin quality, though the 3XO handles sharper. Against its own sibling the Kia Seltos, the Sonet is more compact and easier to park but the loaded Sonet pricing edges into base Seltos territory.
What is the boot space of Kia Sonet?
The Sonet offers 385 litres of boot space, which is larger than the Tata Nexon and Maruti Brezza. It comes with a 15-inch steel spare wheel and a tool kit underneath.
Is Kia Sonet safe?
It comes with 6 airbags, ESC, vehicle stability management, hill-start assist, all-four-disc brakes on higher trims, high-line TPMS and Level 1 ADAS as standard on top trims. Independent crash test ratings for this generation are pending Bharat NCAP results.
What is the waiting period for Kia Sonet?
Waiting periods vary by variant and colour combination, with some specific trims reporting longer waits. Buyers are advised to check current dealership stock as availability shifts month to month.
Which variant of Kia Sonet should I buy?
For most buyers, the HTX+ in either 1.0 turbo petrol iMT or 1.5 diesel iMT is the value sweet spot, offering nearly all the features without the X-Line price premium. Choose the diesel automatic only if you do high highway mileage and refinement is a priority.
