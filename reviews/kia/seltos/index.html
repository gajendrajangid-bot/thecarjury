Kia Seltos: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
7.8
Jury Score / 10

A comprehensively updated, feature-loaded urban SUV with proven powertrains and significantly improved ride quality, though it commands a premium price.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 2026-04-28 Synthesis of 8 independent sources 2,005 words · 8 min read

The 2025 Kia Seltos arrives on an all-new K3 platform with sharper styling, a triple-screen cockpit, and meaningfully improved ride comfort. Powertrains carry over unchanged: 1.5L NA petrol, 1.5L turbo petrol, and 1.5L diesel. It is the longest car in its segment at 4,545 mm, but the Sierra still beats it on wheelbase and interior space.

What Works

  • Outstanding interior quality with tactile buttons and intuitive ergonomics
  • Refined, punchy 1.5L turbo petrol with quick-shifting DCT
  • Notably improved ride comfort and NVH on the new K3 platform
  • Segment-first proximity auto-unlock, gas struts on bonnet, neon brake calipers
  • Proven Hyundai-Kia reliability with strong dealer network

Watch Out For

  • No power tailgate, no hybrid option, no wireless Android Auto on top variants
  • Rear seat lacks slide, recline beyond two steps, and ventilation, all of which the Sierra offers
  • Turbo petrol gets only IMT or DCT, no proper manual gearbox option
  • Top variant pricing pushes close to 25 lakh on-road, where bigger SUVs become viable

Design

The second-generation Seltos adopts a digital tiger-nose grille with vertical elements, square LED headlight clusters, and a connected LED tail-lamp bar that gives a slight retro-modern vibe up front. At 4,545 mm long, 1,800 mm wide, and on a 2,690 mm wheelbase, it is now the longest car in its segment, though it sits 10 mm lower than before. Auto-flush door handles, a hidden rear wiper-washer like a Range Rover, and gas struts for the bonnet are genuine segment-firsts. The neon-green brake calipers divide opinion: Biturbo Media compares them to an aftermarket paint job, while others see them as a sporty signature. The 215/55 R18 alloys on the GT Line draw criticism for their squarish design. Available in 10 solid and two dual-tone colours with four interior themes, the Seltos looks more cohesive in person than in launch images, though boxier rivals like the Sierra appear visually larger despite smaller dimensions on paper.

Interior & Features

The cabin is the Seltos's strongest suit. A 30-inch panoramic trinity display, comprising twin 12.3-inch screens flanking a 5-inch climate panel, dominates the dashboard and is supported by genuine tactile buttons for AC, drive modes, and shortcuts: a refreshing contrast to the touch-heavy interiors from Tata and Mahindra. Soft-touch materials cover the upper dashboard and door tops, while plastic quality throughout is consistently high with no rough edges. Pranay Kapoor's owner inputs confirm long-term fit and finish hold up well. The 10-way power driver seat with memory, ventilated front seats, dual-zone climate, head-up display on higher trims, and an 8-speaker Bose system are highlights. Rear seats get sunshades, twin Type-C ports, and AC vents, but no slide, no ventilation, and only a two-step recline. Headroom is adequate, though the dark theme and lower roofline make the cabin feel less voluminous than the Sierra's. Boot space is rated at 447 litres. Notable omissions include the air purifier, sound mood lamp, and puddle-lamp logo projection from the older Seltos.

Performance & Powertrain

Three carryover engines power the Seltos: a 1.5L NA petrol producing 115 PS and 144 Nm, a 1.5L turbo petrol with 160 PS and 253 Nm, and a 1.5L diesel making 116 PS and 250 Nm. Faisal Khan clocks the turbo petrol from 0-100 km/h in 10.6 seconds via the 7-speed DCT, with the diesel taking 14 seconds and the NA petrol around 13.7 seconds. The turbo petrol is the enthusiast pick: smooth, refined, with a strong mid-range, though the DCT's launch protection eats roughly a second on hard starts and it can feel jerky at crawl speeds. The diesel automatic, paired with a six-speed torque converter, feels slightly strained and adds steering wheel vibration that the manual avoids. Notable absences include a manual gearbox option for the turbo petrol (still IMT only) and any hybrid variant. Real-world fuel efficiency: turbo petrol returns 9-12 km/l in the city and 15-17 km/l on highways; diesel manual stretches to 17-18 km/l on highways.

Ride Quality & Handling

The new K3 platform is the biggest mechanical upgrade. Suspension tuning is now distinctly softer, transforming the Seltos from a stiff-riding SUV into a comfortable family cruiser. Bad roads and large potholes are absorbed without the loud clunks the previous Seltos was known for, and NVH levels have improved noticeably. AutoYogi notes that high-speed stability remains good, but Gagan Choudhary observes a slight floaty sensation on undulating roads, a side-effect of the sedan-derived platform underpinning a tall body. Body roll is more pronounced than before, and enthusiastic cornering reveals understeer, particularly on the lighter-steering petrol variants. The diesel's heavier nose gives it marginally better steering weight and composure. Brakes are progressive but the ABS can feel oversensitive. Drive modes (Eco, Normal, Sport) alter throttle response and steering weight but do not change suspension behaviour. Overall, the trade-off favours comfort over sportiness: a deliberate shift that aligns with what most family buyers in this segment actually want.

Build Quality & Technology

Quality is where Kia firmly outpaces the segment. Every touchpoint, from the chunky gear selector to the steering wheel borrowed from the EV6, feels properly engineered, with no loose panels, beading issues, or aftermarket-feeling fitments. Standard safety includes six airbags across all variants, ESC, hill-hold, and 24 standard safety features. The top-spec X-Line gets Level 2 ADAS with 28 features, while the GTX variants tested here run a 21-feature ADAS suite. A 360-degree camera, blind-view monitor on the cluster, six front and six rear parking sensors, and auto-dipping ORVMs that return to the saved position (unlike Tata's implementation) reflect attention to detail. The Hyundai-Kia group has not yet sent the new Seltos for Bharat NCAP or Global NCAP testing, which remains the one open question on safety credentials. Connected car features, OTA updates, valet mode, and a wireless charging pad are standard on higher trims. Notable feature deletions from the older Seltos include the air purifier, sound mood lamp, and ORVM logo projection.

Price & Value

Pricing has not been officially announced at the time of these reviews, but expectations point to a starting price near 13 lakh ex-showroom and a top-end of around 25 lakh on-road in Mumbai. The HTK(O) variant, as covered by DriveSpark, is positioned at 12.5 lakh ex-showroom and offers the 360-camera, panoramic sunroof, two screens, six airbags, and 16-inch alloys: arguably the value sweet spot. Pranay Kapoor's owner experience on the previous-gen GTX+ shows real-world negotiation can yield 2 lakh discounts at year-end, with insurance and accessory kits worth skipping in favour of third-party options. Service costs run 11,000 to 17,000 rupees annually, on the higher side for the segment. The concern at the top end is that 25 lakh on-road brings the XUV700, Scorpio-N, and Thar Roxx into consideration, all of which offer either more space, more presence, or more capability. The Seltos justifies its price through quality, refinement, and proven reliability rather than sheer value.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • Build quality, fit and finish, and cockpit design are best-in-class for the segment.
  • Ride quality on the new K3 platform is significantly more compliant than the outgoing Seltos.
  • The 1.5L turbo petrol with 7-speed DCT is the standout powertrain at 0-100 in 10.6 seconds.
  • Rear seat space is adequate but does not match the Tata Sierra despite larger exterior dimensions.
  • Feature set is loaded, with dual 12.3-inch screens, panoramic sunroof, ventilated seats, and Level 2 ADAS.

Points of Disagreement

  • Reviewers split on the new design: some find it muscular and aggressive, others call it bland or polarising versus the previous Seltos.
  • Handling verdict is divided: the diesel is rated the better handler by some, while others say the softer suspension reduces sportiness across the range.

TeamBHP's Take

TeamBHP

Long-term owner feedback echoes the editorial verdict: the Seltos delivers a refined, reliable ownership experience with strong resale, but service costs trend higher than rivals like Maruti and the wired-only Android Auto on top variants remains a baffling omission at this price. Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Kia Seltos?
Yes, if you prioritise interior quality, refinement, ride comfort, and proven reliability over outright space or off-road ability. The 1.5L turbo petrol DCT is the pick for enthusiasts; the NA petrol IVT suits city users; the diesel manual is the long-distance highway champ.
What is the Kia Seltos price in India?
Official pricing is yet to be announced at the time of these reviews. Expected starting price is around 13 lakh ex-showroom, with the top variant reaching approximately 25 lakh on-road in Mumbai. The HTK(O) variant is priced at 12.5 lakh ex-showroom.
What are the main problems with the Kia Seltos?
Key gripes include no wireless Android Auto on top variants, no power tailgate, no hybrid or proper manual for the turbo petrol, rear seat lacks slide and ventilation, removal of older features like the air purifier and sound mood lamp, and no published crash-test rating yet.
How is the Kia Seltos mileage?
Real-world figures: 1.5L turbo petrol returns 9-12 km/l in city and 15-17 km/l on highways; 1.5L NA petrol returns roughly 12-14 km/l city and 16-18 km/l highway; 1.5L diesel manual is the most efficient at 12-14 km/l city and 17-18 km/l highway.
Is Kia Seltos good for highway driving?
Yes. The new K3 platform delivers strong high-speed stability, the turbo petrol DCT offers effortless overtaking, ADAS Level 2 with adaptive cruise reduces fatigue, and ride comfort is significantly improved over the previous generation.
How does Kia Seltos compare to rivals?
It is the longest car in its segment but the Tata Sierra has a longer wheelbase, more rear space, and a faster turbo petrol. The Seltos beats rivals on cabin quality, build, and powertrain refinement, while the Hyundai Creta remains its closest twin.
What is the boot space of Kia Seltos?
447 litres, measured up to the parcel shelf level. This is marginally larger than the Tata Sierra's 440 litres at the same measurement point. The rear seat folds in a 60:40 split for additional cargo room.
