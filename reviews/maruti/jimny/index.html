Maruti Jimny: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
7.2
Jury Score / 10

A genuine, lightweight off-roader with global heritage that rewards lifestyle buyers, provided you accept its narrow cabin, modest features and premium pricing.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 6 May 2026 Synthesis of 3 independent sources 1,824 words · 8 min read

The 2025 Maruti Jimny is a five-door, four-seat ladder-frame off-roader powered by a 1.5-litre petrol engine, priced from Rs 12.74 lakh to around Rs 15 lakh ex-showroom. It is India's most accessible authentic 4x4, built around lightness, narrow dimensions and the AllGrip Pro system rather than urban comfort or feature overload. Buy it as a lifestyle statement or a weekend adventure tool, not as a one-car-does-all family SUV.

What Works

  • Authentic ladder-frame 4x4 with AllGrip Pro and excellent articulation
  • Compact, light and easy to place on trails where larger SUVs struggle
  • Five-door practicality unique to the India-spec Jimny globally
  • Reliable Maruti 1.5 petrol shared with Ciaz, Grand Vitara and Hyryder
  • Strong global cult appeal and Nexa lifestyle accessory ecosystem

Watch Out For

  • Cramped four-seat cabin with poor in-cabin storage and no cup holders
  • Mileage of around 16-17 kmpl is low for a sub-4-metre Maruti
  • Older 4-speed automatic and heavy manual clutch feel dated
  • Only 3-star Euro NCAP rating on the 3-door; pricing is steep for the size

Design

The Jimny wears its heritage proudly: round LED projector headlamps, a five-slot grille, clamshell bonnet, flared wheel arches and a tailgate-mounted spare wheel give it the unmistakable silhouette of a scaled-down G-Wagen. India gets a unique five-door body that is roughly 350 mm longer and around 110 kg heavier than the global three-door, yet still slips under four metres with the spare in place. Colour options include Kinetic Yellow, Nexa Blue, Bluish Black, Granite Grey and Sizzling Red, with optional dual-tone black roofs. The boxy proportions, drip rails and exposed door hinges are functional rather than decorative, and Namaste Car notes the design is unapologetically honest, prioritising visibility and approach angles over aerodynamic flourish. Compared with the more aggressive Mahindra Thar, the Jimny looks friendlier and almost toy-like, which polarises opinion: some find the face charmingly retro, others feel it leans too heavily on Mercedes cues. Either way, it is the most distinctive shape in Maruti's Nexa showroom.

Interior & Features

Step inside and the Jimny feels purposeful rather than premium. The dashboard is dominated by a 9-inch SmartPlay Pro+ touchscreen with wireless Android Auto and Apple CarPlay on the Alpha trim, while the Zeta variant gets a 7-inch unit. There is automatic climate control with a digital readout, a TFT multi-information display in the instrument cluster, cruise control and steering-mounted audio and phone controls. Beyond that, the cabin is deliberately basic: hard plastics, plain door cards, manual seat adjustment and only a handful of storage cubbies. The biggest gripe across reviewers is in-cabin storage, with no proper cup holders and very limited shelf space, which is why MotorInc bluntly suggests buyers should wear cargo trousers. The seats are 60:40 split at the rear and fold flat to liberate boot space, but the cabin is narrow by design, and adults above 5'8" will find rear headroom and shoulder room tight. It is honest, not luxurious.

Performance & Powertrain

Power comes from Maruti's 1.5-litre K15B petrol with mild-hybrid assistance, producing 105 PS and 134 Nm, and shared with the Ciaz, Grand Vitara, Brezza, Baleno and Toyota Hyryder. Buyers can choose a 5-speed manual or a 4-speed torque converter automatic, both driving all four wheels through the AllGrip Pro transfer case with low-range and a brake-based limited-slip function. The motor is tuned for low and mid-range tractability rather than outright pace, with strong torque across a wide rpm band that suits trails better than highway sprints. The manual's clutch and gear lever are deliberately heavier and more durable than typical Maruti fare, while the automatic, despite being an old four-speeder, is impressively peppy and the easier pick for daily duty. Claimed efficiency is just under 17 kmpl, and real-world numbers will sit around 12-14 kmpl, noticeably thirstier than a Brezza. It is brisk enough, never fast.

Ride Quality & Handling

The Jimny rides on a ladder-frame chassis with three-link rigid axles front and rear, coil springs all round and a generous wheel travel that lets it keep all four tyres planted on broken ground. On tarmac there is body roll and the steering feels lazy on turn-in, both expected traits of a body-on-frame off-roader, but the suspension is softer and less bouncy than the old Gypsy or the current Thar. Around town the compact 3,985 mm length and tight turning radius make it surprisingly easy to thread through traffic and park, and bad roads are simply ignored rather than transmitted. Off-road is where it shines: lightness, narrow width and excellent articulation let it glide over obstacles that would have a heavier Thar Roxx working hard, and the AllGrip Pro system with low-range and hill descent control is genuinely capable. Highway cruising at 100-plus is feasible but noisy and deliberate, not relaxing.

Build Quality & Technology

Build quality feels robust in the places that matter for an off-roader: the doors shut with a solid thunk, the gear lever and clutch are over-engineered for durability, and the ladder frame and panels resist flex during heavy articulation. Even after sustained off-road testing, no rattles or quality concerns surfaced. Feature count is modest by 2025 standards: six airbags, ESP, hill-hold and hill-descent control, rear parking sensors and camera, keyless entry with push-button start, auto headlamps and wipers, and connected-car telematics. There is no sunroof, no ventilated seats, no wireless charger and no 360-degree camera, omissions that buyers cross-shopping a Brezza or Grand Vitara will feel. Safety-wise, the three-door Jimny scored three stars at Euro NCAP, and the India-spec five-door has cleared the mandatory domestic crash norms but awaits a Bharat NCAP or Global NCAP rating. For the intended duty cycle, it feels built to last.

Price & Value

Pricing runs from Rs 12.74 lakh ex-showroom for the Zeta manual to roughly Rs 15 lakh for the Alpha automatic, with a subscription option from around Rs 33,000 a month. That positions it above the rear-wheel-drive Mahindra Thar and well clear of the Maruti Brezza, despite being smaller and less feature-rich than either. The premium exists because the 1.5-litre petrol misses sub-4-metre tax concessions and because Maruti is selling a globally cult-favourite nameplate through Nexa as a lifestyle product, with accessories like roof carriers, tents, portable speakers and graphics packs. As a single family car it is hard to justify against a Brezza, Tata Nexon or Tata Punch, all of which offer more space, features and efficiency for less. As a second car, weekend toy or genuine off-road tool, the Jimny is the cheapest authentic 4x4 you can buy new in India and holds its value well thanks to global demand.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • The Jimny is a purpose-built off-roader where lightness, narrow width and ground clearance matter more than outright power.
  • It is strictly a four-seater with limited cabin storage and tight space for taller adults in the rear.
  • The 1.5-litre K15B petrol engine delivers around 16-17 kmpl, well below typical Maruti benchmarks like the Brezza.
  • On-road manners are softer and more car-like than the Gypsy or Thar, but it still rolls and feels deliberate rather than quick.
  • It is priced at a premium for its size because the 1.5 petrol misses sub-4-metre tax breaks, making the Thar look better value on paper.

Points of Disagreement

  • Whether the iconic boxy face is a charming homage or too derivative of the Mercedes G-Wagen.
  • Whether the manual or the older 4-speed automatic is the better pick, given the heavy clutch versus the dated but surprisingly peppy torque converter.

TeamBHP's Take

TeamBHP

TeamBHP's owner community broadly endorses the Jimny as a brilliant trail machine that rewards enthusiasts, while flagging the same real-world frustrations: tight rear seat, sparse storage, average highway refinement and mileage that sits closer to 12 kmpl in city use. Long-term owners report rock-solid reliability and minimal niggles, with the strongest praise reserved for off-road composure and the Maruti service network advantage over the Thar.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Maruti Jimny?
Buy it if you want an authentic 4x4 with global heritage, plan weekend adventures or need a distinctive second car. Skip it if this has to be your only family car, in which case a Brezza, Nexon or Punch will serve better.
What is the Maruti Jimny price in India?
The Jimny is priced from Rs 12.74 lakh ex-showroom for the Zeta variant to around Rs 15 lakh for the top Alpha. Maruti also offers a subscription plan from roughly Rs 33,000 per month.
What are the main problems with the Maruti Jimny?
Tight rear seat space for taller adults, almost no in-cabin storage and no cup holders, an aging 4-speed automatic, mileage of just 16-17 kmpl claimed, and pricing that feels steep for a sub-4-metre vehicle.
How is the Maruti Jimny mileage?
Maruti claims just under 17 kmpl. Real-world figures hover around 12-14 kmpl in mixed driving, noticeably lower than a Brezza with the same 1.5 petrol because of the Jimny's extra weight, 4x4 hardware and chunky tyres.
Is Maruti Jimny good for highway driving?
It is competent but not relaxing. The 105 PS engine cruises comfortably at 100-110 kmph, but body roll, a lazy steering feel and wind noise mean long highway stints feel more deliberate than in a monocoque SUV like the Grand Vitara.
How does Maruti Jimny compare to rivals?
Versus the Mahindra Thar it is lighter, narrower and easier off-road but smaller and pricier. Against the Maruti Brezza, Tata Nexon or Tata Punch, it loses on space, features and efficiency but is the only true ladder-frame 4x4 in this price bracket.
What is the boot space of Maruti Jimny?
With the rear 60:40 split seats up, boot space is limited to small bags. Folding the rear bench flat liberates a usable, flat luggage area suitable for camping gear, which is how Maruti positions it through Nexa lifestyle accessories.
Is Maruti Jimny safe?
It comes with six airbags, ESP, hill-hold and hill-descent control, ISOFIX mounts and a reversing camera. The three-door Jimny scored three stars at Euro NCAP, and the India-spec five-door has cleared mandatory domestic crash tests but awaits a published Bharat NCAP or Global NCAP rating.
What is the waiting period for Maruti Jimny?
Waiting periods have eased from launch peaks and now vary by city and variant, typically a few weeks to a couple of months for the Alpha automatic. Globally the Jimny remains in high demand, so colour and trim availability fluctuate.
Which variant of Maruti Jimny should I buy?
The Alpha automatic is the sweet spot for most buyers, adding the 9-inch infotainment, alloy wheels, climate control and convenience of the 4-speed torque converter. The Zeta manual makes sense only if you want the cheapest entry into the range.
