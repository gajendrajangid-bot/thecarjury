Volvo XC60: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
7.6
Jury Score / 10

A genuinely safe, supremely comfortable luxury SUV that rewards understated buyers, even if it lacks the badge value and outright sportiness of its German rivals.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 13 May 2026 Synthesis of 5 independent sources 1,340 words · 6 min read

The 2025 Volvo XC60 is a Scandinavian luxury SUV that prioritises safety, comfort and understated design over outright performance or status. With a 2.0L turbo-petrol mild hybrid, AWD, a class-leading Bowers & Wilkins audio system and one of the most feature-loaded cabins in its segment, it is a refined alternative to the Mercedes GLC, BMW X3 and Audi Q5.

What Works

  • Outstanding safety package with full ADAS suite as standard
  • Bowers & Wilkins audio is the best in the segment
  • Extremely comfortable, orthopaedically designed front seats
  • Solid build, plush cabin and excellent NVH insulation
  • Air suspension with boot-lowering function and four-zone climate control

Watch Out For

  • Single 2.0L petrol option; no diesel, hybrid or higher powertrain choice
  • Touchscreen-heavy interface buries basic functions and can distract while driving
  • Boot space of 483-505 litres is modest for the segment
  • Lacks the badge value and road presence of Mercedes, BMW or Audi

Design

The XC60 wears Volvo's signature Scandinavian design with confidence: hammerhead Thor LED headlamps with active bending function, a vertical slatted grille, integrated headlight washers and 19-inch diamond-cut alloys shod with Michelin rubber specifically made for Volvo. At 4.7 metres long and 1.65 metres tall, it reads more crossover than imposing SUV, which Namaste Car notes splits opinion against the taller Fortuner-style rivals. The rear is an evolution of the previous generation, with the trademark vertical tail lamps preserved. Subtle chrome inscriptions, a roof spoiler and dual exhaust tips round off a design that prioritises elegance over aggression. It is a car designed to blend in tastefully rather than announce arrival, a deliberate Volvo philosophy.

Interior & Features

Inside, the XC60 feels genuinely premium with perforated Nappa leather, real wood inlays, brushed metal and a Crystal gearshift knob by Swedish glassmaker Orrefors. The 16-way power-adjustable front seats are the highlight: orthopaedically designed with massage, ventilation, heating, lumbar adjustment, extendable thigh support and memory. Four-zone climate control with an air purifier and PM 2.5 sensor is standard, and the panoramic sunroof lifts cabin ambience. The rear seat offers good headroom and dedicated AC vents, but thigh support is merely adequate and the prominent transmission tunnel makes the middle seat uncomfortable. Storage is generous, with one-litre door bins, a deep armrest cubby and twin Type-C ports for rear passengers. Heated rear seats feel misplaced for Indian conditions.

Performance & Powertrain

The XC60 uses a 2.0-litre four-cylinder turbo-petrol with 48V mild-hybrid assistance, producing 246 hp and 360 Nm, paired with an 8-speed Aisin automatic and AWD. Volvo's philosophy here is clear: brisk, not aggressive. 0-100 km/h takes 6.9 seconds and top speed is electronically limited to 180 km/h. There is no Sport mode and no paddle shifters; instead, drive modes span Eco, Comfort, Off-Road, Dynamic and Individual. As MotorOctane observes, the car is tuned for relaxed daily progress rather than outright performance. Throttle response is linear when driven smoothly, but a sudden stab produces a brief delay followed by a non-linear surge. Overtaking is effortless on highways. Buyers seeking the visceral pull of an X3 M40i will need to look elsewhere.

Ride Quality & Handling

Ride quality is a Volvo strength, helped by four-corner air suspension that can lower the car for easier boot loading. On highways the XC60 glides, absorbing expansion joints and broken patches with composed silence. At low city speeds the suspension can feel slightly busy or springy over sharp potholes, particularly with one or two occupants, but it firms up nicely with a fuller load. Steering is light, accurate and predictable, ideal for city use but lacking the connected feel of a BMW. Handling is secure rather than playful. Cabin insulation is outstanding: wind and tyre noise stay suppressed up to 120 km/h. ADAS calibration is generally mature, though MotorOctane flags that lane-keep can feel intrusive in dense Indian traffic and may need toggling off.

Build Quality & Technology

Build quality is where the XC60 quietly pulls ahead. Doors shut with a reassuring heft, panel gaps are tight and material choices feel considered rather than flashy. Faisal Khan repeatedly notes the cabin finish is phenomenal, on par with cars costing significantly more. The feature list is exhaustive: 360-degree camera with self-park, head-up display, heads-up adaptive cruise with Pilot Assist, blind-spot monitoring with steering correction, run-off-road mitigation, oncoming lane mitigation, six airbags, hill descent control, laminated side glass and a four-year free digital services subscription. Safety credentials include a five-star Euro NCAP rating, IIHS Top Safety Pick+ and an ultra-high-strength boron steel safety cage. The integrated dashcam and air quality sensor with PM 2.5 monitoring are thoughtful India-specific touches.

Price & Value

Priced around ₹75-84 lakh ex-showroom for the B5 Ultra petrol variant, the XC60 sits squarely against the Mercedes GLC, BMW X3 and Audi Q5. On features per rupee it arguably leads: ventilated and massaging seats, four-zone climate, air suspension, Bowers & Wilkins audio and full ADAS are either optional or unavailable on German rivals at this price. What you sacrifice is badge equity and resale value, both meaningfully stronger with the Germans. The service network is also thinner. For buyers who value substance over status and prioritise safety, comfort and audio quality, the XC60 represents strong value. For those buying primarily for road presence or driving thrills, the competition still makes a stronger case.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • Safety remains Volvo's strongest USP, with a full ADAS suite, six airbags and a five-star Euro NCAP rating.
  • The Bowers & Wilkins 15-speaker audio system is genuinely class-leading.
  • Front seats, designed with orthopaedic input, offer outstanding long-distance comfort with massage, ventilation, heating and lumbar adjustment.
  • Build quality, cabin insulation and fit-finish feel a clear notch above the German trio.
  • The four-cylinder petrol is smooth and adequate, but never sporty; there is no Sport mode or paddle shifters.

Points of Disagreement

  • Touchscreen experience is divided: some find the Google-powered system intuitive and feature-rich, others call it distracting with too many functions buried in menus.
  • Rear-seat comfort splits opinion, with praise for headroom and AC vents but criticism of thigh support and the large transmission tunnel.

TeamBHP's Take

TeamBHP

A genuinely loaded luxury SUV with class-leading comfort and safety, but expect a soft, springy ride and a fragile Google infotainment stack.

What owners praise
  • 16-way adjustable ventilated front seats with five massage modes proved a blessing during 45-degree summer test drives.
  • Bowers & Wilkins 1100W 14-speaker system rivals home theatres; convinced even a non-music-buff wife within five seconds.
  • Volvo India replaced the entire car within 30 days after a snag, without escalation or harsh communication needed.
  • Cabin stays silent up to 120 km/h with virtually no tyre or wind noise intruding.
What owners flag
  • Suspension stays busy with small up-down springy movements over highway undulations, unlike the stiffer BMW X1.
  • Google Automotive loses GPS signal or internet connection for maps frequently, still feels half-baked.
  • First delivered car developed a serious snag within a day, had to be towed back.
  • Service network is very thin compared to Germans; long-distance support remains a real concern.

Long-term reality: After 2500 km in 60 days, the owner rates Volvo India's customer support as exceptional, citing the no-questions replacement of a faulty car. He would buy the XC60 again for its comfort, safety and value over the Germans.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Volvo XC60?
Yes, if you prioritise safety, comfort and understated design over badge value. It is loaded with features that German rivals charge extra for.
What is the Volvo XC60 price in India?
The 2025 XC60 B5 Ultra petrol is priced around ₹75-84 lakh ex-showroom, with on-road prices expected slightly higher depending on state.
What are the main problems with the Volvo XC60?
A touchscreen-heavy interface, modest 483-505 litre boot, single petrol powertrain option, thin service network and weaker resale than German rivals are the key drawbacks.
How is the Volvo XC60 mileage?
Volvo claims around 12 km/l from the 71-litre tank for the 2.0L mild-hybrid petrol. Real-world city mileage is typically 8-10 km/l, highway 12-14 km/l.
Is Volvo XC60 good for highway driving?
Yes. It glides on air suspension, cabin stays silent up to 120 km/h, and Pilot Assist with adaptive cruise makes long-distance touring effortless and safe.
How does Volvo XC60 compare to rivals?
Versus Mercedes GLC, BMW X3 and Audi Q5, the XC60 offers more standard features and better safety but lacks badge value, sporty driving feel and resale strength.
What is the boot space of Volvo XC60?
The XC60 offers 483-505 litres of boot space, expandable to 1410 litres with the rear seats folded. Air suspension lowers the loading lip electronically.
Is Volvo XC60 safe?
Extremely. Five-star Euro NCAP, IIHS Top Safety Pick+, six airbags, full ADAS suite, boron steel safety cage and Volvo's pedestrian-safety bonnet design.
