Volvo XC40: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
7.4
Jury Score / 10

A safe, understated and well-built premium SUV that suits buyers who prize substance and safety over the badge value of the German trio.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 18 May 2026 Synthesis of 4 independent sources 1,223 words · 5 min read

The 2026 Volvo XC40 stays true to its understated Scandinavian brief: safety-first engineering, a minimalist cabin and a relaxed 197 hp mild-hybrid petrol drivetrain. It will not shout for attention on the road, but it offers genuine premium quality and one of the most complete ADAS suites in its segment.

What Works

  • Among the safest cars in its class with whiplash-protection seats and pedestrian-friendly bonnet
  • Genuinely premium fit-finish with cruelty-free leather and recycled materials
  • Panoramic sunroof with UV-blocking laminated glass and airy cabin feel
  • Comfortable, well-bolstered front seats with power adjust and thigh extension
  • Full ADAS suite, 360-degree camera and Google-powered infotainment

Watch Out For

  • Rear seat thigh support and headroom tight for taller adults
  • Infotenment-heavy interface buries basic functions behind multiple taps
  • Digital cluster is not customisable and feels dated for 2025
  • Road presence and badge value lag behind BMW, Mercedes and Audi

Design

The XC40 sticks to Volvo's restrained Scandinavian template: 4.4 metres long, 1.6 metres tall, Thor's Hammer LED DRLs, a closed-off grille and 18-inch five-spoke silver alloys on Continental rubber. The Fusion Red paint on the test car is one of five shades sold in India, with green reserved for other markets. A power-retractable shark-fin antenna, roof rails rated for 75 kg and a 2,100 kg towing capacity round out the exterior brief. Namaste Car notes the 11.4-metre turning circle and 1,700 kg kerb weight. It is deliberately understated: buyers who want their SUV to announce itself should look at the German trio, but those who want premium without theatre will find the proportions tidy and timeless.

Interior & Features

Inside, the XC40 is minimalist to a fault. The dashboard mixes soft-touch surfaces with a pale wood-effect insert, cruelty-free leather upholstery and a 12.3-inch digital cluster paired with a portrait touchscreen running Google built-in with Maps, Assistant and Play Store apps. Power-adjustable front seats include whiplash-protection headrests and extendable thigh support; the panoramic sunroof uses UV-blocking laminated glass. Storage is generous up front but the rear bench is the weak link: kneeroom is decent, thigh support is short and the transmission tunnel hump compromises the middle seat. MotorOctane flags that the touchscreen is smaller than rivals and that the 360-camera stitching is rough. Physical climate shortcuts and a wireless charger are welcome additions.

Performance & Powertrain

Power comes from a 2.0-litre four-cylinder petrol with a 48V mild-hybrid system, rated at 197 hp and 300 Nm, good for 0-100 km/h in 7.6 seconds and a 180 km/h top speed. Fuel economy hovers around 12 km/l from the 54-litre tank. Volvo's philosophy, as Gagan Choudhary points out across the range, is sufficiency rather than headline numbers: no sport mode, no paddle shifters, no turbo theatrics. The unit pulls cleanly in the city, overtakes on the highway without drama and stays refined thanks to the hybrid assist. Buyers craving the urgency of an X3 M40i or AMG GLC will be left cold, but for daily premium duty the powertrain feels perfectly judged.

Ride Quality & Handling

Ride quality is the XC40's strongest dynamic suit. The suspension absorbs broken Indian tarmac without thumping into the cabin, and high-speed composure is genuinely confidence-inspiring. The steering is light and accurate rather than weighty or communicative, which suits the car's relaxed brief, and the brakes are progressive. ADAS, however, divides opinion: on the newer Volvo EX30 the calibration feels mature and unobtrusive, while on the XC60 reviewers found the lane-keep and adaptive cruise interventions too aggressive for Indian traffic, often demanding manual override. The 18-inch wheels strike a sensible balance; at low speed over sharp edges the body can toss slightly, but loaded up the car settles into a proper highway glide.

Build Quality & Technology

Build quality is where Volvo earns its premium. Doors shut with a solid chunk, panel gaps are tight and even the recycled and plastic materials are blended without anything that rattles or feels out of place. The foam-cored bonnet, whiplash-protection headrests, pedestrian-aware nose and full ADAS suite, including blind-spot warning, cross-traffic alert and lane assist, make this one of the safest cars on sale at the price. Equipment is generous: panoramic sunroof, Harman Kardon-grade audio, four-zone climate with CleanZone air quality and humidity sensors, power tailgate, 360-camera and wireless charging. The digital cluster's lack of customisation and the touch-heavy interface are the only real weak spots in an otherwise meticulously assembled package.

Price & Value

At an expected price around ₹45-48 lakh ex-showroom, the XC40 sits below the BMW X1 and Mercedes-Benz GLA on badge prestige but matches or beats them on safety kit and build integrity. The bigger XC60 lands near ₹84 lakh and competes directly with the GLC, X3 and Q5. The honest pitch: you are paying for substance, not status. If road presence and resale strength matter most, the Germans still win. If you want the safest car in the segment, cruelty-free materials and a calm ownership experience, the Volvo justifies itself. Cross-shoppers should also weigh the brand's own XC60 and electric EX30, plus value-led EVs like the Hyundai Ioniq 5 and Kia EV6.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • Class-leading safety credentials with a full ADAS suite that most luxury rivals still skip
  • Solid Scandinavian build quality with a clean, minimalist cabin
  • Comfortable ride and easy, relaxed driving manners suited to Indian cities
  • Understated styling that appeals to buyers who want premium without showing off
  • 197 hp four-cylinder mild-hybrid petrol delivers adequate, not exciting, performance

Points of Disagreement

  • Touchscreen-led interior: praised for clean design by some, criticised as fiddly and not premium-looking by others
  • ADAS calibration: rated mature and subtle on the newer Volvo, called intrusive on the XC60 in Indian traffic

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Volvo XC40?
Yes, if safety, build quality and understated design matter more than badge prestige or outright performance. Test drive a BMW X1 too before deciding.
What is the Volvo XC40 price in India?
Reviewers expect the 2026 XC40 to be priced around ₹45 lakh ex-showroom. Official pricing was not announced at the time of the reviews.
What are the main problems with the Volvo XC40?
Tight rear-seat thigh support, a touchscreen-heavy interface that buries basic controls, a non-customisable digital cluster and weaker road presence than German rivals.
How is the Volvo XC40 mileage?
Around 12 km/l from the 2.0-litre four-cylinder mild-hybrid petrol with a 54-litre tank, per reviewer-quoted figures.
Is Volvo XC40 good for highway driving?
Yes. The 197 hp engine, composed suspension and full ADAS suite make it a relaxed, safe highway tourer, though it is not sporty.
How does Volvo XC40 compare to rivals?
Versus the BMW X1 and Mercedes GLA, the XC40 trails on badge value and steering feel but leads on safety kit, ADAS and build.
What is the boot space of Volvo XC40?
Boot space is adequate for a family of four with luggage, with a temporary spare wheel, parcel tray and underfloor storage.
Is Volvo XC40 safe?
Yes, it is among the safest cars in the segment, with a full ADAS suite, whiplash-protection seats, pedestrian-friendly bonnet and multiple radars.
