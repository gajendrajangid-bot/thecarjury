Jeep Compass: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
7.6
Jury Score / 10

A genuine driver's SUV with class-leading build, handling and highway ability; just accept the city compromises, service costs and dated 9-speed automatic.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 13 May 2026 Synthesis of 6 independent sources 1,429 words · 6 min read

The 2025 Jeep Compass remains one of India's most accomplished mid-size SUVs, blending genuine Jeep DNA with class-leading build quality, a punchy 2.0 Multijet diesel and benchmark handling. It is, however, an expensive car for its size, more at home on the highway than in city traffic, and Jeep's patchy after-sales network remains a real concern.

What Works

  • Premium cabin with extensive soft-touch materials and solid switchgear
  • Refined, torquey 170 hp diesel with effortless highway cruising
  • Sharp handling, planted high-speed stability and excellent ride on broken roads
  • Genuine 4x4 capability with Selec-Terrain modes on top variants
  • Robust safety kit: 6 airbags, ESP, TPMS and 360-degree camera on top trim

Watch Out For

  • Expensive to buy and service compared to Harrier, Safari and XUV700
  • Tight rear knee room, no rear sunshades, no seat-back recline
  • 9-speed automatic feels lazy in city driving; turning radius is large
  • Patchy Jeep after-sales network and reports of electrical niggles

Design

The Compass still looks unmistakably Jeep, with the seven-slot grille, trapezoidal wheel arches, sloping roofline and chrome strip running mirror-to-mirror. The facelift adds LED projector headlamps with DRLs, a redesigned front bumper, LED tail-lamps with smoked inserts and new alloy designs in 17 and 18-inch sizes. It measures around 4.4 metres long with roughly 178 mm of ground clearance, so it is smaller than a Harrier or XUV700 but more rugged-looking than a Seltos. As MotorBeam notes, the proportions are squat and aerodynamic rather than imposing. Colour options include black, white, grey, blue, red and silver. The shark fin antenna, roof rails and side cladding round off a design that has aged remarkably well in eight years.

Interior & Features

Inside is where the Compass justifies its price. The dashboard mixes soft-touch plastics, leatherette, brushed aluminium and (on base trims) a fabric insert, and the build feels a clear notch above the Harrier, Astor and even the XUV700 AX7. The new 10.1-inch Uconnect 5 touchscreen and 10.25-inch digital cluster are sharp, customisable and well integrated, though the UI can need a double-press for some inputs. Front seats are firm, supportive and ventilated with power adjust, lumbar control and memory. The rear bench is comfortable for two adults with decent knee and headroom, but a third passenger and anyone over six feet will find it cramped. There are no rear sunshades and no seat-back recline, and the panoramic sunroof eats some headroom.

Performance & Powertrain

India gets two engines: a 1.4-litre turbo-petrol making 163 hp paired with a 7-speed DCT or 6-speed manual, and the 2.0-litre Multijet II diesel producing 170 hp and 350 Nm, mated to a 9-speed automatic or 6-speed manual. The diesel is the one to have. It pulls cleanly from around 1,700 rpm, masks speed superbly on the highway and sits at roughly 1,600 rpm at 100 km/h in ninth gear. There is some clatter at idle and mild vibration past 3,000 rpm, but cabin insulation keeps it civilised. The 9-speed automatic is smooth at cruise but slow to kick down in town. Claimed mileage is 16 kmpl auto and 17 kmpl manual; real-world figures run 8 to 14 in the city and 16 to 20 on the highway.

Ride Quality & Handling

Ride and handling are the Compass's biggest party tricks. Frequency Selective Damping (a feature unique in this segment when launched) lets it soak up broken roads with composure no Harrier or XUV700 can match, while keeping body roll well contained. The chassis with independent rear suspension feels agile, planted at triple-digit speeds and genuinely fun on twisty roads. Faisal Khan rates the steering feel and feedback among the best in class, even if the brushed-aluminium wheel itself feels chunky. Low-speed ride is firm rather than plush, the turning radius is large, and the Bridgestone tyres get vocal in corners. On 4x4 variants, Selec-Terrain with Auto, Snow, Sand and Mud modes plus hill descent control makes the Compass genuinely capable off-road.

Build Quality & Technology

Step inside and the Compass feels engineered like a small tank: heavy doors that shut with a thud, dense plastics, a body that is 70 percent high-strength steel and clear attention to detail down to the rubber-lined cup holders. Top variants get six airbags, ESP, traction control, hill hold, electronic roll mitigation, TPMS, a 360-degree camera, front parking sensors, electric parking brake with auto-hold and a panoramic sunroof. Connected-car features via Jeep's app cover live tracking, vehicle health and remote disable. ADAS is not offered. The infotainment supports wireless Android Auto and Apple CarPlay, and the Alpine 9-speaker setup with subwoofer is genuinely good. A few testers, including MotorOctane, did note minor plastic rattles inside the cabin.

Price & Value

Value is where the Compass stumbles. Prices run roughly from Rs 20 lakh to over Rs 30 lakh ex-showroom, putting the top diesel 4x4 automatic 10 to 11 lakh above a Seltos and 5 to 6 lakh above a Harrier that shares the same engine. Rivals like the XUV700, Harrier, Safari and Scorpio N offer more space, more features and bigger road presence for less. Service costs of Rs 20,000 to 30,000 per visit at 15,000 km intervals are steep, and Jeep's thin dealer network is a real concern. As the TeamBHP owner concedes, the base manual is the value pick; higher automatic variants are hard to justify on a spreadsheet. You buy a Compass for the way it feels, not what it offers per rupee.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • Build quality and cabin materials are the best in the segment, with a tank-like feel.
  • The 2.0-litre Multijet diesel is strong and effortless on highways, especially past 1,700 rpm.
  • Handling, steering feedback and high-speed stability are class-leading, helped by Frequency Selective Damping.
  • Rear seat is comfortable for two but tight for three; six-footers will find knee room limited.
  • Pricing is steep versus rivals like Harrier, Safari and XUV700 that offer more space.

Points of Disagreement

  • City usability: some find it manageable, others call buying a Compass for city use a waste of the car.
  • The 9-speed automatic: praised for smoothness on highways but criticised as slow and laggy in town.

TeamBHP's Take

TeamBHP

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Jeep Compass?
Yes, if you value build quality, driving dynamics and highway ability over space and features. The diesel manual is the sweet spot; skip it if low service costs matter.
What is the Jeep Compass price in India?
The 2025 Compass is priced roughly from Rs 20 lakh to over Rs 30 lakh ex-showroom, depending on variant, with the top 4x4 diesel automatic being the most expensive.
What are the main problems with the Jeep Compass?
High service costs of Rs 20,000 to 30,000 per visit, a thin dealer network, lazy 9-speed automatic in city, tight rear seat for three and reports of electrical niggles.
How is the Jeep Compass mileage?
Real-world diesel mileage is 8 to 14 kmpl in the city and 16 to 20 kmpl on the highway. Claimed figures are 16 kmpl automatic and 17 kmpl manual.
Is Jeep Compass good for highway driving?
Yes, it is excellent. The 170 hp diesel, FSD suspension and planted chassis make it one of the best highway SUVs in its price range.
How does Jeep Compass compare to rivals?
It is smaller and pricier than the Harrier, Safari and XUV700 but beats them on build, materials, handling and ride quality. Rivals win on space and features per rupee.
What is the boot space of Jeep Compass?
Boot space is adequate for four passengers' luggage and slightly larger and taller than a Kia Seltos, with 60/40 split-folding rear seats for extra flexibility.
Is Jeep Compass safe?
Yes. Top variants get six airbags, ESP, traction control, hill hold, TPMS, 360-degree camera, electronic roll mitigation and a body that is 70 percent high-strength steel.
