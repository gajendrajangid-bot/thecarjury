Jeep Compass: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
7.6
Jury Score / 10

A premium, properly-built SUV that rewards highway and rough-road use, but its city manners and pricing demand careful consideration.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 13 May 2026 Synthesis of 6 independent sources 1,332 words · 6 min read

The 2025 Jeep Compass remains India's most authentically rugged premium SUV, with a tank-like build, genuine 4x4 hardware, and a chassis that thrives on highways and broken roads. The facelift adds richer materials, sharper screens and a 10.1-inch infotainment system, but tight city manners, a firm ride at low speeds and a steep price tag keep it a considered choice rather than an obvious one.

What Works

  • Solid, premium build quality with 70% high-strength steel body
  • Brilliant ride quality and planted highway behaviour thanks to FSD suspension
  • Strong 170 hp / 350 Nm diesel with refined cruising manners
  • Genuine off-road credentials with 4x4 Low and Selec-Terrain
  • Upgraded cabin with 10.25-inch infotainment, 10.1-inch digital cluster, ventilated seats and panoramic sunroof

Watch Out For

  • Cramped rear seat for three; six-footers struggle for knee and foot room
  • Engine clatter, vibrations and firm low-speed ride make city driving tiring
  • Pricey compared to Harrier and Seltos which share segment or engine
  • After-sales network remains a concern versus established rivals

Design

The Compass sticks to its proven formula and is better for it. The seven-slot grille, squared wheel arches, sloping roofline and chrome strip running between the mirrors remain unmistakably Jeep, while the facelift adds LED projector headlights, a redesigned bumper and a sharper LED tail-lamp signature. At 4.4 metres long with 170 mm of ground clearance, it is compact for the segment but visually substantial. MotorBeam notes the Compass is the most aerodynamic Jeep ever produced, and small touches like Jeep silhouettes hidden in the windshield, headlights and speaker surrounds reward a closer look. Seven exterior colours are offered, with Brilliant Black and Magnesio Grey new to this update. It looks rugged without trying too hard.

Interior & Features

The cabin is where the facelift pays off most clearly. A 10.25-inch touchscreen with wireless Android Auto and Apple CarPlay sits alongside a 10.1-inch digital instrument cluster, both crisp and customisable. Materials are genuinely premium: soft-touch surfaces dominate the upper dash and doors, the new flat-bottom steering feels expensive, and the leather seats are electrically adjustable, ventilated and shaped for long hauls. A panoramic sunroof, wireless charging, nine-speaker Alpine system with subwoofer and dual-zone climate control complete the kit. The rear bench seats only two comfortably; three adults find it narrow, and six-footers cannot tuck their feet under the front seats. Rear sunshades and seatback recline are missed. As MotoWagon points out, the memory seats and 360-degree camera lift the experience.

Performance & Powertrain

Two engines are offered: a 1.4-litre turbo-petrol with 163 hp paired to a six-speed manual or seven-speed DCT, and the 2.0-litre Multijet-II diesel with 170 hp and 350 Nm mated to a six-speed manual or nine-speed automatic. The diesel is the one to have. It pulls hard from the mid-range, masking speed so effectively that 120 km/h feels like 90, and settles at just 1,600 rpm in ninth gear at 100 km/h. There is turbo lag below 2,000 rpm and audible clatter at idle, with vibrations creeping in past 3,000 rpm. The nine-speed auto is smooth on a light throttle but slow to kick down. Fuel efficiency is 8-9 km/l in the city, climbing to 16-18 km/l on highways.

Ride Quality & Handling

This is where the Compass justifies its badge. Frequency Selective Damping, unique in the segment, lets it absorb broken roads at speed that would unsettle rivals, while body control through corners is genuinely impressive for a 1.6-tonne SUV. The independent rear suspension keeps it planted and agile, and the steering, though chunky and lacking ultimate feel, weighs up well at highway speeds. At low speeds it is a different story: the suspension is firm over potholes, the turning radius is large, and parking demands patience. The 4x4 variant adds Selec-Terrain with Auto, Snow, Sand and Mud modes plus 4WD Low. There is no low-range gearbox, but with 170 mm of clearance and Hill Descent Control, real off-road ability is intact.

Build Quality & Technology

Build quality is the Compass's single biggest strength and the reason existing owners forgive its quirks. The body uses 70 percent high-strength steel, doors shut with a reassuring thunk, and panel gaps are tight. Inside, plastics feel dense and switchgear operates with weight. Six airbags, ESP, traction control, Hill Hold, electronic roll mitigation, tyre-pressure monitoring and a 360-degree camera are available, though ADAS is absent and TPMS plus 360 cam are restricted to the top S variant. The Unknown Reviewer rightly flags that rubber floor mats and rugged touches signal a car built to be used hard. Some plastic rattles do creep into the cabin on rough roads, and insulation could be better, but the underlying structure feels indestructible.

Price & Value

Pricing remains the Compass's weakest argument. The range stretches from roughly Rs 20 lakh to over Rs 30 lakh ex-showroom for the top 4x4 diesel automatic, putting it five to six lakh above a Harrier that shares the same engine and around ten lakh above a comparable Seltos. The MotorOctane take is that buyers cross-shop the BMW X1 at this money, and the Compass holds its own on build but not on badge. What you pay for is genuine 4x4 hardware, FSD suspension, a cabin that punches above its segment and proven long-term durability. After-sales reach lags Hyundai and Toyota. For highway-heavy or rural buyers it makes sense; for pure city use, cheaper rivals do the job.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • Build quality and cabin feel are class-leading; it genuinely feels like a tank inside
  • The 2.0-litre Multijet diesel is strong on highways with effortless mid-range torque
  • Frequency Selective Damping suspension delivers exceptional ride and high-speed stability
  • The seven-slot grille, squared wheel arches and Jeep DNA still make it instantly recognisable
  • Genuine 4x4 capability with Selec-Terrain modes sets it apart from soft-roader rivals

Points of Disagreement

  • City usability: some reviewers call the steering manageable while others flag heavy low-speed feel, large turning radius and engine clatter as deal-breakers
  • Value for money: opinions split between calling it premium and well-equipped versus overpriced against the Harrier and Seltos

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Jeep Compass?
Yes, if you do regular highway runs, rough roads or need real 4x4. For pure city use, cheaper rivals like the Seltos make more sense.
What is the Jeep Compass price in India?
The 2025 Compass range starts around Rs 20 lakh ex-showroom and the top 4x4 diesel automatic crosses Rs 30 lakh ex-showroom.
What are the main problems with the Jeep Compass?
Tight rear seat for three, diesel clatter at idle, firm low-speed ride, large turning radius and a limited after-sales network compared to mainstream rivals.
How is the Jeep Compass mileage?
The 2.0 diesel automatic returns roughly 8-9 km/l in city traffic and 16-18 km/l on highways. The manual is slightly more efficient.
Is Jeep Compass good for highway driving?
Excellent. FSD suspension, strong diesel torque, low cruising rpm and planted high-speed stability make it one of the best highway SUVs in its price band.
How does Jeep Compass compare to rivals?
It out-builds the Seltos and matches the Harrier on engine, but costs five to ten lakh more, justified mainly by genuine 4x4 and superior ride.
What is the boot space of Jeep Compass?
The boot is slightly larger than a Kia Seltos on paper and taller in practice, swallowing three suitcases comfortably with 60:40 split-folding rear seats.
Is Jeep Compass safe?
Yes. Six airbags, ESP, traction control, Hill Hold, electronic roll mitigation, TPMS and a 360-degree camera are offered, with a robust high-strength steel body.
