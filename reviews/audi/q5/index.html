Audi Q5: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
7.7
Jury Score / 10

The new-generation Q5 is the most driver-focused, well-rounded package in the mid-luxury SUV segment, provided you can wait for the 2026 India launch.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 19 May 2026 Synthesis of 6 independent sources 1,300 words · 5 min read

The 2026 Audi Q5 arrives as the brand's first all-new SUV in years, sitting on a fresh platform with a 2.0-litre turbo-petrol, twin panoramic screens and optional air suspension. It targets the BMW X3 and Mercedes-Benz GLC with a sharper drive, better rear-seat comfort and an expected aggressive entry price.

What Works

  • Engaging dynamics with 2.0 TFSI delivering around 200-265 bhp depending on tune
  • Refined, well-isolated cabin with minimal NVH
  • Optional adaptive and air suspension setups
  • Comfortable, properly contoured rear bench with three-zone climate
  • Strong Bang & Olufsen 3D audio and crisp twin-screen interface

Watch Out For

  • India launch pushed to 2026, well after rivals have refreshed
  • Real-world fuel efficiency hovers around 10 km/l on the petrol
  • Touch-based steering controls and reset-on-restart settings irritate
  • Blind-spot monitoring and ventilated seats may be missed at this price
  • Central tunnel makes the rear middle seat impractical

Design

The new Q5 keeps the familiar Audi silhouette but sharpens almost every surface around it. The single-frame octagonal grille gets vertical slats and gloss-black detailing, the LED headlamps are slimmer, and the rear adopts a new full-width treatment that previews future Audi SUVs. Twin functional exhaust tips, a subtle spoiler and 18 to 21-inch wheel options (19 or 20-inch expected for India) give it more visual purpose than the outgoing car. At 4.7 metres long and roughly 1,900 kg kerb, it remains compact enough for narrow city streets yet planted on the highway. Against the BMW X3 and Mercedes-Benz GLC, MotorOctane rates it the most youthful and performance-led of the trio, and the road presence backs that up.

Interior & Features

Audi finally moves its SUV cabin into the screen era. A 14.5-inch central touchscreen sits beside an 11.9-inch digital cluster, with an optional 10.9-inch passenger display forming the panoramic layout. Material quality, knurled finishes and a flat-top, flat-bottom steering lift the ambience, while physical AC vents and a separate climate strip survive the digital push. Front seats are wide, well-bolstered and comfortable on long drives; the rear bench slides, reclines, and offers genuine knee and headroom for two adults, though the tall transmission tunnel rules out a third. Boot space is 520 litres, expanding to 1,520 with the seats folded. Niggles remain: touch-capacitive steering buttons, no ventilated seats on the current India car, and no head-up display.

Performance & Powertrain

The Q5 launches globally with a 2.0-litre TFSI turbo-petrol mated to a 7-speed dual-clutch and quattro all-wheel drive, producing roughly 200-265 bhp and 370 Nm depending on tune, with mild-hybrid assistance for the new generation. Zero to 100 km/h takes around 6.1 seconds and top speed is 240 km/h. As Namaste Car notes, the engine is tractable from low revs and rarely needs more than half-throttle on Indian roads. The gearbox is smooth and intuitive, with no jerkiness in traffic. Crucially, the Q5 stays the driver's pick of the segment: the BMW X3 has softened and the GLC prioritises comfort, leaving Audi as the one still chasing the sporty brief. A diesel TDI continues in some markets but is unlikely for India.

Ride Quality & Handling

Ride quality is where the new platform pays off. With the optional adaptive dampers or full air suspension, the Q5 settles quickly over expansion joints and feels properly planted at highway speeds. V3Cars found that low-speed ride over sharp speed breakers is firmer than expected and the suspension settles a touch too quickly, but 32 PSI tyre pressure transforms the experience. Body roll is well-controlled, brakes feel confident and the auto-hold engages and disengages intuitively. The electromechanical progressive steering is light at parking speeds and weights up cleanly on the move. Six drive modes (comfort, dynamic, individual, auto, efficiency, off-road) tailor the character. Compared with the Volvo XC60, the Q5 is sharper; against the GLC, it trades a sliver of plushness for engagement.

Build Quality & Technology

Build quality is a clear strong point. The cabin is exceptionally well-isolated: engine, road and ambient noise are all subdued enough that conversations happen at low volume even at highway speeds. Panel fit is tight, there is no rattling reported even on a 33,000 km media test car, and the 19-speaker, 755W Bang & Olufsen 3D sound system with Fraunhofer technology is a genuine highlight. Safety kit includes eight airbags, ESP, hill-hold, 360-degree camera, tyre-pressure monitoring and ISOFIX, with the outgoing car already holding an IIHS Top Safety Pick. Full European-spec ADAS, including lane-keep and adaptive cruise, is unlikely to arrive in India in complete form, with Audi expected to follow Mercedes and BMW in offering a reduced suite.

Price & Value

Pricing is where the Q5 currently wins. The outgoing car starts at around Rs 65.1 lakh ex-showroom, with the top Technology variant at roughly Rs 70.80 lakh, and discounts often close the gap to on-road pricing. For an SUV with this size, performance and equipment, that undercuts the BMW X3 and Mercedes-Benz GLC for what is arguably the more complete package. The catch: the all-new generation lands in India only in 2026, by which time rivals will have moved on. MotoWagon's view is that buyers wanting the latest platform should wait; those who want the current car at a sharp price should negotiate now. Within the Audi range, the smaller Q3 remains the value pick for buyers not needing Q5 space.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • Sharpest-driving SUV in its segment, retaining genuine performance character
  • All-new platform delivers noticeably better ride and refinement than the outgoing car
  • Twin-screen panoramic dashboard with Bang & Olufsen audio lifts cabin appeal
  • Rear-seat comfort is better than the X3 and GLC for two adults
  • Boot space is generous and rear suspension can be lowered via air suspension for loading

Points of Disagreement

  • Whether the firm low-speed ride is acceptable or a meaningful daily-use drawback
  • Whether ADAS in full European spec will or should arrive in India

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Audi Q5?
Yes, if you want the segment's sharpest drive and can wait for the 2026 all-new model; otherwise, the current car offers strong discounts now.
What is the Audi Q5 price in India?
The current Q5 starts at around Rs 65.1 lakh ex-showroom, with the top Technology variant at roughly Rs 70.80 lakh.
What are the main problems with the Audi Q5?
Firm low-speed ride, no ventilated seats, touch-capacitive steering controls, around 10 km/l real-world economy, and a 2026 India launch for the new model.
How is the Audi Q5 mileage?
Real-world economy is about 10 km/l on petrol over 2,000 km mixed driving; the outgoing TDI diesel returned around 12.5 km/l.
Is Audi Q5 good for highway driving?
Yes. The 2.0 TFSI's 370 Nm, quattro AWD, refined cabin and planted high-speed ride make it an excellent highway cruiser.
How does Audi Q5 compare to rivals?
Sharper to drive than the softened BMW X3, more engaging than the comfort-focused Mercedes GLC, with better rear-seat space than both.
What is the boot space of Audi Q5?
520 litres with the rear seats up, expanding to 1,520 litres when folded, with air suspension lowering the rear for easier loading.
Is Audi Q5 safe?
Yes. Eight airbags, ESP, 360-degree camera, hill-hold, ISOFIX and an IIHS Top Safety Pick rating for the outgoing generation.
