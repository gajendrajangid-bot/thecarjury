Audi Q7: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
7.8
Jury Score / 10

A polished, comfortable seven-seater luxury SUV with a strong V6 petrol and air suspension, held back only by the missing diesel and an ageing platform.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 20 May 2026 Synthesis of 5 independent sources 1,285 words · 5 min read

The Audi Q7 returns as a facelifted seven-seater luxury SUV with a 3.0-litre V6 TFSI petrol, mild-hybrid tech and adaptive air suspension. It blends serious highway pace with limousine-grade ride comfort, though the platform is no longer the freshest in its class.

What Works

  • Strong, refined V6 TFSI petrol with 48V mild-hybrid
  • Air suspension ride quality on Indian roads
  • Quattro AWD with seven drive modes including off-road
  • Spacious second row, 780-litre boot expanding to 1908 litres
  • Eight airbags, 360-degree camera and 5-star Euro NCAP rating

Watch Out For

  • No diesel option in a segment where rivals still offer one
  • Platform dates back to 2016, no full ADAS suite for India
  • Real-world fuel economy of 5-7 km/l
  • Third row tight for adults on longer journeys

Design

The facelifted Q7 keeps its familiar silhouette but tightens the details: a vertical-slat single-frame grille, slimmer Matrix LED headlamps with dynamic indicators, redesigned bumpers and fresh 19 or 20-inch alloys. At 5.07 metres long and just under 2 metres wide, it is one of the larger SUVs you can park in India, yet MotorBeam notes it does not project the sheer bulk of a GLS or X7. The clean surfacing and HD Matrix lighting signature give it understated authority rather than chest-thumping presence, which is very much the Audi way. Compared to the Volvo XC90's Scandinavian calm or the more aggressive BMW X5, the Q7 lands somewhere in the middle: classy, recognisable and ageing gracefully rather than shouting for attention.

Interior & Features

Inside, the Q7 finally feels modern. The dual-screen MMI layout pairs a 10.1-inch infotainment with a smaller climate display, backed by a 12.3-inch Virtual Cockpit Plus. Materials are genuinely rich on the lower dash and doors, with wood inserts, quattro badging and tight panel gaps. Four-zone climate, panoramic sunroof, ventilated front seats, wireless charging and a 19-speaker, 730-watt Bang & Olufsen 3D system are all standard on the Technology trim. Namaste Car points out the seven-seat layout offers 780 litres of boot space that expands to 1908 litres with the third row folded. The second row is genuinely comfortable for adults, but the third row is best reserved for children or short hops, a limitation the larger Mercedes GLS handles better.

Performance & Powertrain

Under the bonnet sits a 3.0-litre V6 TFSI petrol with 48V mild-hybrid tech, producing 335-340 bhp and 500 Nm, paired to a ZF 8-speed Tiptronic and quattro all-wheel drive. Audi claims 0-100 km/h in 5.6 seconds and a 250 km/h top speed. MotorBeam describes the engine as silky, meaty and genuinely eager to rev, with seven drive modes (Efficiency, Comfort, Auto, Dynamic, Individual, All-road, Off-road) reshaping throttle, gearbox and suspension behaviour. Downshifts are quick, paddle shifters add involvement, and refinement at idle is excellent. The trade-off is fuel: expect 5-6 km/l in the city and 7-8 km/l on the highway from the 85-litre tank. Buyers who want sharper engagement in this price band should also cross-shop the Porsche Macan.

Ride Quality & Handling

This is where the Q7 truly justifies its price. The adaptive air suspension soaks up broken tarmac, expansion joints and sharp-edged potholes with a composure few seven-seaters match, and the ride height can be lifted by up to 90 mm for rough patches or dropped at speed for stability. Body control in Dynamic mode is impressively flat for a 2100 kg SUV, and the steering, while light, weights up predictably. High-speed stability past 120 km/h is rock solid, helped by quattro AWD with a self-locking centre differential. Visibility from the driver's seat is excellent and the large mirrors make narrow Indian streets manageable. It is not as overtly sporty as a BMW X5, but as a long-distance highway cruiser it is hard to fault.

Build Quality & Technology

Fit and finish feel appropriately German. Doors shut with a heavy, vault-like thud, switchgear operates with consistent damping, and there are no obvious panel misalignments. Safety kit is comprehensive: eight airbags, ESP, 360-degree camera, park assist plus, lane departure warning, hold assist, tyre pressure monitoring and ISOFIX mounts. The Q7 also carries a 5-star Euro NCAP rating from earlier testing. The notable omission is a full ADAS suite, which MotorOctane attributes to Audi India deliberately holding it back because European calibration does not suit Indian traffic. The Virtual Cockpit, Matrix LEDs, four-zone climate and HD MMI touchscreens all work crisply, though buyers should note the head-up display offered abroad does not make it to Indian cars.

Price & Value

At an ex-showroom range of roughly Rs 88.66 lakh for the Premium Plus and Rs 97.81 lakh for the Technology, the Q7 undercuts the Mercedes GLS and sits close to the BMW X5 while offering three rows as standard, which the X5 does not. Gagan Choudhary highlights that pricing is the trickiest part of the conversation: petrol-only running costs and 5-7 km/l economy will sting. Service intervals are reasonable and Audi offers maintenance packages extending up to seven years. If you primarily need a refined, well-built seven-seater for highway-heavy family duty and can absorb the fuel bills, the Q7 is excellent value. If diesel mileage matters more, the segment offers better answers elsewhere.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • The 3.0-litre V6 petrol with 335-340 bhp and 500 Nm is effortlessly quick, 0-100 km/h in roughly 5.6-5.9 seconds.
  • Adaptive air suspension delivers excellent ride quality and lets you raise or lower the car by up to 90 mm.
  • Cabin quality, dual-screen MMI layout and Bang & Olufsen 3D audio feel suitably premium.
  • Petrol-only line-up hurts buyers who clocked big miles on the old diesel.
  • Real-world fuel economy sits at a thirsty 5-7 km/l in city and highway use.

Points of Disagreement

  • Road presence: some find the Q7 proportionate and elegant, others say it looks smaller and less imposing than the GLS or X7.
  • Third-row usability: rated decent for short trips by some, called strictly kids-only by others.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Audi Q7?
Yes, if you want a refined seven-seater petrol luxury SUV with strong highway manners. Skip it if you need diesel economy or a full ADAS suite.
What is the Audi Q7 price in India?
The facelift is priced from roughly Rs 88.66 lakh ex-showroom for Premium Plus and Rs 97.81 lakh for the Technology trim.
What are the main problems with the Audi Q7?
No diesel option, real-world fuel economy of 5-7 km/l, an ageing 2016 platform, tight third row for adults and limited ADAS for India.
How is the Audi Q7 mileage?
Expect 5-6 km/l in city traffic and 7-8 km/l on highways from the 3.0 V6 petrol, with an 85-litre fuel tank.
Is Audi Q7 good for highway driving?
Excellent. Air suspension, quattro AWD, 335 bhp V6 and a stable chassis make it one of the most composed highway SUVs in its class.
How does Audi Q7 compare to rivals?
More driver-focused than a Volvo XC90, three-row standard unlike the BMW X5, but less imposing than the larger Mercedes GLS.
What is the boot space of Audi Q7?
780 litres with all seats up, expanding to 1908 litres with the third row folded. Powered tailgate and rail-mounted load securing are standard.
Is Audi Q7 safe?
Yes. Eight airbags, ESP, 360-degree camera, park assist plus, lane departure warning and a 5-star Euro NCAP rating from earlier crash testing.
