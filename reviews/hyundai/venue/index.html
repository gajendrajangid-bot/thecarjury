Hyundai Venue: The Jury's Verdict

BUY

A comprehensively upgraded sub-4m SUV with class-leading powertrain choice and a genuinely premium cabin, though stiff ride and top-end pricing demand careful variant selection.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 6 May 2026 Synthesis of 8 independent sources 2,143 words · 9 min read

The 2025 Hyundai Venue is a ground-up second generation built on the new K1 platform, growing wider, taller and longer in wheelbase while staying under 4 metres. It is the most feature-rich and best-finished Venue yet, and the only sub-4m SUV to offer petrol manual, turbo-petrol DCT, diesel manual and now a diesel automatic. Pricing starts at Rs 7.89 lakh ex-showroom for the petrol and stretches into Creta territory at the top.

What Works

  • Only car in segment with diesel manual, diesel automatic, petrol manual and turbo-petrol DCT
  • Premium, well-finished cabin with physical climate buttons retained
  • 1.5 diesel manual delivers segment-best efficiency and effortless torque
  • Improved K1 platform with hot-stamped high-strength steel and 6 standard airbags
  • Strong, confidence-inspiring brakes and stable highway manners

Watch Out For

  • Suspension is on the stiffer side and transmits sharp bumps with audible thud
  • Steering goes lighter and more disconnected at highway speeds
  • No panoramic sunroof, no CNG option at launch and no rear radar even on top variant
  • Top HX10 variant pricing overlaps with base Hyundai Creta

Design

The second-generation Venue moves to Hyundai's K1 global platform and grows 20 mm wider, around 46 mm taller and gains roughly 20 mm of wheelbase, though overall length stays under the 4-metre tax bracket. The face borrows heavily from the Palisade and Alcazar with a connected DRL bar, quad-LED headlamps on top variants and a dark-chrome grille, while the rear gets a full-width LED lightbar with the Venue lettering integrated inside the housing rather than stuck on. Faisal Khan flags the 16-inch alloy design as not particularly sporty, and the squared wheel arch cladding makes the wheels look smaller than they are. In photographs the styling can feel busy, but in the metal it reads as a more grown-up, SUV-shaped car rather than a raised hatchback. The N Line, when ordered, sharpens the look further with twin exhaust tips, red accents and a dynamic swipe indicator at the rear. Six body colours are offered, with Hazel Blue and Atlas White available in dual-tone with a black roof.

Interior & Features

Step inside and the Venue feels like the start of a new Hyundai design generation. The dashboard is anchored by a curved housing for two 12.3-inch screens (top variant only), a leather-wrapped D-cut steering wheel with Morse-code 'H' branding, and a Razo-textured trim with embossed Venue lettering. The dark navy and dove-white theme looks genuinely upmarket, though detailers will be busy: the white touch points will soil quickly. The Nvidia-accelerated infotainment finally adds wireless Apple CarPlay and Android Auto, supports OTA updates and runs slickly. Crucially, Hyundai has retained physical climate and shortcut buttons. Front seats are broad, supportive and ventilated on top trims, with four-way powered adjustment for the driver, though height adjustment stays manual and there is no lumbar support. Rear bench space has improved thanks to scooped-out front seatbacks and reclining backrests, but width is still tight for three adults and the middle passenger gets no headrest. Boot space rises to 375 litres with a full-size steel spare. Niggles include halogen roof lamps and a small under-armrest cubby.

Performance & Powertrain

The Venue is the only car in its class to offer four powertrain combinations. The 1.2-litre naturally aspirated petrol with 83 PS and a 5-speed manual is the volume seller, adequate but uninspiring. The 1.0-litre turbo-GDI petrol makes 120 PS and 172 Nm and pairs with a 6-speed manual or a refined 7-speed DCT, which Faisal Khan clocked from 0-100 kmph in around 13.4 seconds. Hyundai claims a marginally retuned torque figure of 170 Nm. The star remains the 1.5-litre U2 CRDi diesel with 116 PS and 250 Nm, available with a 6-speed manual or, for the first time on the Venue, a 6-speed torque-converter automatic. The diesel manual is the enthusiast and high-mileage pick: light clutch, strong mid-range from 1,500 rpm and real-world efficiency around 17-20 kmpl. The diesel automatic adds genuine convenience for long-distance users but, as observed across the Creta and Syros, can feel slightly mismatched, dropping city efficiency to 12-12.5 kmpl and adding some NVH over time.

Ride Quality & Handling

Hyundai has retuned the suspension, retaining MacPherson struts up front and a coupled torsion-beam rear, and the low-speed ride is more compliant than before. However, the setup remains on the firmer side and the suspension is audibly noisy over potholes and sharp-edged joints, a trait Arun Panwar and other reviewers all noted. The diesel variants feel a touch more settled than the petrol because of the added engine weight over the front axle, while the N Line is mechanically slightly firmer, which actually helps it flow through corners with less roll. High-speed stability is a clear strength, and the brakes, with rear discs from HX8 onwards, are strong and progressive. Handling is neutral and grippy rather than playful: the electric steering is light and easy in the city but goes oddly distant at highway speeds, lacking the feedback to encourage spirited driving. Compared to the Mahindra XUV 3XO and Tata Nexon, the Venue trails in outright pothole absorption but matches or beats them in straight-line composure.

Build Quality & Technology

The K1 platform claims a high proportion of high-strength and ultra-high-strength steel with hot-stamped sections, and Hyundai is openly targeting strong Bharat NCAP scores, though no rating is published yet. Six airbags, ESC, vehicle stability management, hill-start assist, TPMS and ISOFIX are standard from the base HX2, and Level 2 ADAS with 16 features arrives on higher trims, although the absence of a rear radar (present only on the N Line) means rear collision warning and assist are missing. The cabin is mostly hard plastic but the textures, panel gaps and switchgear feel a clear notch above the outgoing Venue. Feature highlights include 360-degree camera with a driver rear-view monitor, electronic parking brake with auto hold, wireless charging, ventilated front seats, a Bose 8-speaker system with subwoofer and amplifier, three drive modes, three traction modes, OTA updates and over 70 connected-car features. MotorBeam noted the speakers have been upgraded to compensate for dropping the Arkamys tuning of the older car.

Price & Value

Pricing starts at Rs 7.89 lakh ex-showroom for the base petrol and the diesel HX2 manual is around Rs 9.69 lakh ex-showroom, translating to roughly Rs 11.5 lakh on-road. The diesel automatic is only available from HX5 upwards at around Rs 11.58 lakh ex-showroom, and the fully loaded HX10 sits at the top of the segment, the most expensive sub-4m SUV on sale. At that level the Venue overlaps with entry-level Hyundai Creta variants, which Team-BHP and others flag as a real internal threat: the base CVT Creta is roughly Rs 13.3 lakh ex-showroom and offers more space and presence, albeit with fewer features. Against direct rivals such as the Kia Sonet, Mahindra XUV 3XO, Maruti Brezza and Tata Nexon, the Venue's case rests on the breadth of powertrains, particularly the diesel manual for high-running buyers, and the perceived premiumness of the cabin. Lower variants offer the strongest value; the HX6 and HX8 are the sweet spots.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • All-new K1 platform with higher use of high-strength steel makes the body feel more substantial and improves cabin space
  • Cabin is a genuine generational leap, with twin 12.3-inch screens, an Nvidia-powered infotainment system and a tasteful navy-and-dove-white theme
  • The 1.5-litre diesel manual remains the powertrain hero with strong torque and real-world efficiency near 20 kmpl on highways
  • Ride is improved over the old car but still firm, with audible suspension noise on broken roads
  • Feature list is deep: ventilated seats, 360-degree camera, electronic parking brake, Level 2 ADAS, Bose 8-speaker audio and a Bose subwoofer in the boot

Points of Disagreement

  • Reviewers split on the new diesel automatic: some find it lively and useful for heavy users, others feel the 6-speed torque converter saps the engine's natural smoothness and drops city efficiency to 12-12.5 kmpl
  • Opinions diverge on the front design, with some calling it mature and Alcazar-like in the flesh while others find the grille and bumper detailing overdone

TeamBHP's Take

TeamBHP

Team-BHP's Utkarsh Negi frames the Venue as Hyundai doing what Hyundai does best: refining a proven idea rather than reinventing it. The forum's review highlights solid build quality without dramatic improvement over the old car, well-judged physical controls, strong straight-line stability and grabby brakes, but flags self-inflicted issues like white touch points that will age quickly, only-okay seat ventilation, halogen roof lights and an asterisk on ADAS due to the missing rear radar.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Hyundai Venue?
Yes, if you want a feature-rich sub-4m SUV with the widest powertrain choice in the segment. The diesel manual is ideal for high-running buyers, the turbo-petrol DCT for urban convenience. Skip the top HX10 if budget overlaps with the base Hyundai Creta.
What is the Hyundai Venue price in India?
The 2025 Venue starts at around Rs 7.89 lakh ex-showroom for the base petrol HX2. The diesel HX2 manual is approximately Rs 9.69 lakh ex-showroom, the diesel automatic starts from HX5 at around Rs 11.58 lakh, and the top HX10 is the most expensive variant in the sub-4m SUV segment.
What are the main problems with the Hyundai Venue?
Three consistent issues: the suspension is firm and audibly noisy over potholes, the steering feels disconnected at highway speeds, and the diesel automatic drops city efficiency to 12-12.5 kmpl. There is also no panoramic sunroof, no CNG, and no rear radar even on the top variant.
How is the Hyundai Venue mileage?
The 1.5 diesel manual is the efficiency leader, returning around 17-20 kmpl on highways and 14-16 kmpl in the city. The diesel automatic gives roughly 12-12.5 kmpl in town and 17 kmpl on highways. The 1.0 turbo-petrol DCT manages 10-12 kmpl in the city, while the 1.2 NA petrol is the most economical choice for low-running urban use.
Is Hyundai Venue good for highway driving?
Yes. High-speed stability is a strong point, the brakes are confidence-inspiring with rear discs from HX8 onwards, and the diesel's 250 Nm makes overtakes effortless. The ride remains firm but composed at speed, and Level 2 ADAS adds long-drive convenience.
How does Hyundai Venue compare to rivals?
Versus the Kia Sonet, the Venue feels fresher inside but the Sonet still has its diesel automatic and similar engines. The Mahindra XUV 3XO and Tata Nexon offer better pothole absorption and stronger crash ratings. The Maruti Brezza counters with CNG, which the Venue lacks at launch.
What is the boot space of Hyundai Venue?
The 2025 Venue offers 375 litres of boot space, up by 25 litres over the previous model. A full-size steel spare wheel (195/65 R15) is provided under the boot floor on most variants.
Is Hyundai Venue safe?
Hyundai claims an enhanced K1 platform with high-strength and hot-stamped steel. Six airbags, ESC, VSM, hill-start assist, TPMS and ISOFIX are standard from the base HX2. Level 2 ADAS with 16 features is offered on higher variants, though Bharat NCAP results were not available at launch.
What is the waiting period for Hyundai Venue?
Waiting periods vary by city and variant. Diesel automatic and top HX10 trims typically have longer waits at launch, while the volume 1.2 petrol HX2 and HX4 are more readily available. Check with your local Hyundai dealer for the latest delivery timelines.
Which variant of Hyundai Venue should I buy?
For value, the HX4 or HX6 petrol manual hits the sweet spot of features versus price. High-mileage users should pick the diesel manual in HX6 or HX8. Buyers wanting a fun car with a manual should look at the N Line. Avoid the HX10 if you can stretch to a base Hyundai Creta.
