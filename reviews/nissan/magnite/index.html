Nissan Magnite: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
7.5
Jury Score / 10

The 2025 Magnite delivers genuine sub-4m SUV value with strong safety, a 360-camera and turbo-petrol option, provided you can live with Nissan's smaller dealer network.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 13 May 2026 Synthesis of 7 independent sources 1,274 words · 5 min read

The 2025 Nissan Magnite is the sensible value pick in the sub-4m SUV segment, now wrapped in a more premium cabin with leatherette finishes, ambient lighting and a 9-inch touchscreen. With six airbags as standard, a Global NCAP 4-star rating, a 360-degree camera and a strong turbo-petrol CVT option, it punches well above its price tag. Refinement and dealer reach remain its weak spots.

What Works

  • Standard 360-degree camera, rare at this price
  • Six airbags and 4-star Global NCAP rating standard
  • Spacious cabin with genuine three-adult rear bench usability
  • Strong real-world fuel efficiency: 13-15 kmpl city, up to 21 kmpl highway
  • Affordable ownership and low service costs

Watch Out For

  • 1.0L naturally aspirated engine feels strained with a full load
  • Touchscreen looks dated and occasionally laggy
  • Heavy clutch and cramped footwell on the manual
  • Smaller dealer and service network than Hyundai, Maruti, Tata

Design

The Magnite retains its upright sub-4m SUV stance with 205mm ground clearance, a 1.6m height, 16-inch dual-tone diamond-cut alloys, LED projector headlamps with L-shaped DRLs, and a honeycomb grille. The 2024 facelift adds a heavier grille surround, revised bumper, new LED fog lamps and a floating skid plate. The body uses 67 percent high-tensile steel. Namaste Car notes the design is now exported to 65-plus countries, lending it global credibility. Panel gaps are large but consistent. Settled shades like Sunset Copper Orange and brown hide minor scratches well in Indian conditions. Opinions split on the facelift: the busier front is functional but the cleaner pre-facelift face arguably aged better.

Interior & Features

The cabin is the Magnite's biggest leap forward. Leatherette dashboard and door pads, orange contrast stitching, ambient lighting, a 9-inch floating touchscreen with wireless Android Auto and CarPlay, auto-dimming IRVM, and a 7-inch driver display lift perceived quality significantly. The 360-degree camera is the segment standout, useful for first-time SUV buyers in tight Indian parking. Storage is well thought out with large bottle holders, a deep armrest cubby and a usable glovebox. Weak spots remain: the touchscreen UI looks dated next to newer rivals, the camera feed is low-resolution, lower-dash plastics feel cheap, and there is no fuse puller in the toolkit. Rear AC vents, USB and ISOFIX mounts are present.

Performance & Powertrain

Two 1.0-litre three-cylinder petrol engines are offered. The naturally aspirated unit makes 71 hp and 96 Nm, paired only with a 5-speed manual. The turbo produces 98 hp with 160 Nm (manual) or 152 Nm (CVT). The NA engine is adequate solo in the city but struggles on inclines, with three or four occupants, or during overtakes; downshifts are mandatory above 2,500 rpm. Gagan Choudhary flags noticeable shudder at crawl speeds and a heavy clutch. The turbo CVT is the editorially recommended pick: smoother, more refined and far better suited to highway cruising and family duty. NVH has improved with better insulation, though three-cylinder vibrations are still felt.

Ride Quality & Handling

The Magnite rides on MacPherson struts up front and a twin-tube setup at the rear, with stabilizer bars at both ends. The facelift's softer tune handles 80 percent of Indian roads with composure, though sharp bumps at speed can produce a clunky thud. Ground clearance of 205mm and a 5m turning radius make it easy in city traffic and over speed breakers. The electric steering is light at low speeds but feels slow and disconnected when pushed. Brakes are described as mushy, lacking bite when you need confidence. Headlight throw and spread are genuinely strong for the segment, boosting night-driving confidence on highways. High-speed stability is acceptable rather than reassuring.

Build Quality & Technology

Build quality has tightened meaningfully with the facelift. MotorOctane's year-long test reports no rattles or major issues after 14,000 km. Plastic rattles that dotted the older cabin are largely gone, and engine sound is better isolated. Panel gaps, while wide, are consistent. Standard safety is segment-leading: six airbags, ABS with EBD, ESC, traction control, hill-start assist, TPMS, ISOFIX, and three-point seatbelts for all five occupants. Global and ASEAN NCAP have awarded four and five stars respectively across tests. Quality niggles persist: lower-dash plastics, parcel-tray hooks, wiper rubber and the defogger element finish feel built to a price. Nissan offers a 3-year/100,000 km warranty.

Price & Value

Value is where the Magnite is unmatched. The range starts around 6 lakh ex-showroom and tops out near 13.5 lakh for the Tekna Plus, undercutting every rival in the sub-4m SUV segment for equivalent kit. The second-from-base variant already packs a 9-inch touchscreen, wireless smartphone mirroring and a rear wiper under 8 lakh on-road. Service costs are exceptionally low: four free labour visits up to 20,000 km, with the 10,000 km service costing roughly 1,900 rupees inclusive of GST. Nissan claims a five-year running cost of 39 paise per kilometre. The trade-off is dealer reach, smaller than Hyundai, Maruti or Tata, and lingering uncertainty about Nissan's global alliance plans.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • Outstanding value-for-money in the sub-4m SUV segment with feature levels rivals charge much more for
  • Strong safety credentials: six airbags standard, 4-star Global NCAP, three-point seatbelts for all occupants
  • Cabin space, boot (336L) and rear three-passenger comfort are class-competitive
  • Turbo-petrol with CVT is the recommended powertrain; the 1.0L NA struggles with loads and inclines
  • Service costs are genuinely low with four free labour visits in the first 20,000 km

Points of Disagreement

  • Design: some find the 2024 facelift's busier grille and bumper a step back, others rate it as classy and modern
  • NA engine refinement: rated acceptable for solo city driving by some, dismissed as rough and underpowered by others

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Nissan Magnite?
Yes, if you want maximum value in a sub-4m SUV. Pick the turbo-petrol CVT, and verify service reach in your city.
What is the Nissan Magnite price in India?
The 2025 Magnite range starts around 6 lakh ex-showroom, with the top Tekna Plus variant priced around 13.5 lakh ex-showroom.
What are the main problems with the Nissan Magnite?
Weak 1.0L NA engine under load, dated touchscreen UI, heavy clutch, mushy brakes, and a smaller dealer network than rivals.
How is the Nissan Magnite mileage?
Real-world figures are 13-15 kmpl in city traffic and up to 21 kmpl on the highway at 80-85 kmph cruising speeds.
Is Nissan Magnite good for highway driving?
The turbo CVT cruises and overtakes easily. The NA engine manages solo highway runs but needs downshifts with passengers.
How does Nissan Magnite compare to rivals?
It undercuts Nexon, Sonet, Venue and Brezza significantly while matching them on safety, space and the rare 360-camera feature.
What is the boot space of Nissan Magnite?
The Magnite offers 336 litres of boot space, expanding to 690 litres with the 60:40 split rear seats folded down.
Is Nissan Magnite safe?
Yes. Six airbags, ESC, ABS and three-point seatbelts are standard, with a 4-star Global NCAP and 5-star ASEAN NCAP rating.
