Nissan Magnite: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
7.4
Jury Score / 10

The facelifted Magnite delivers genuine SUV space, strong safety credentials and 360-degree camera at a price no rival can match.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 13 May 2026 Synthesis of 7 independent sources 1,348 words · 6 min read

The 2025 Nissan Magnite is the value benchmark of the sub-4-metre SUV segment, now wrapped in a more premium cabin with six standard airbags and a 360-degree camera. It is honest, spacious and supremely affordable to run, though refinement and dealer footprint remain the trade-offs buyers must weigh.

What Works

  • Outstanding value for money in its segment
  • 360-degree camera, six airbags and three-point belts for all as standard
  • Genuine three-abreast rear seat and high ground clearance for Indian roads
  • LED headlamp throw is excellent, ride quality is now softer and more mature
  • Low running and service costs, claimed 39 paise per km maintenance

Watch Out For

  • Three-cylinder NA engine feels strained with load and on inclines
  • Touchscreen looks dated and feels slow, no ventilated seats
  • Cramped pedal box, slow steering and mushy brakes hurt driver involvement
  • Smaller dealer and service network than Hyundai, Tata or Maruti rivals

Design

Nissan's facelift adds a chunkier honeycomb grille, new LED DRLs, redesigned bumpers and fresh dual-tone diamond-cut alloys, while retaining the 1.6-metre height and a useful 205 mm ground clearance. The Sun Copper Orange and dual-tone Black roof options remain the visual highlights, and proportions still read as a proper compact SUV rather than a jacked-up hatch. Not everyone is convinced the update is an upgrade: one MotorInc view is that the older car's cleaner face looked classier and that the new surrounds appear busy. Detailing like the LED light-saber turn indicators, shark-fin antenna and 50 kg-rated functional roof rails lifts kerb appeal, though panel gaps, while consistent, remain wider than the segment best.

Interior & Features

Inside, the Magnite finally feels like it belongs in 2025. Leatherette dashboard and door pads, orange stitching, ambient lighting and an auto-dimming IRVM lift perceived quality, and an Arkamys six-speaker setup handles audio duties well. The 9-inch floating touchscreen supports wireless Android Auto and Apple CarPlay, but it looks dated next to rivals, the UI is sluggish, and daylight visibility could be better. Practicality is genuinely strong: large bottle holders in all four doors, a 336-litre boot expanding to 690 litres, rear AC vents, USB, 12V socket and a usable centre armrest. Namaste Car notes the seats use a UV-resistant heat-reflective coating, though the absence of ventilation is felt. Rear space comfortably seats three adults, a rarity here.

Performance & Powertrain

Two 1.0-litre three-cylinder petrols are on offer: a 71 hp naturally aspirated unit with a 5-speed manual, and a 98 hp turbo with manual or CVT, producing 160 Nm (152 Nm on CVT). The NA engine is fine for solo city running and returns 13-15 km/l in traffic, but Gagan Choudhary flags that it feels strained on inclines and with a full load, and the clutch is on the heavier side. The turbo-CVT is the sweet spot: smoother now after the retune, with minimal rubber-band effect, decent 90-to-100 km/h roll-on and a useful L mode for engine braking. Highway efficiency touches 21 km/l at 85 km/h on the NA. NVH has improved noticeably but the three-cylinder thrum is still audible.

Ride Quality & Handling

The revised suspension tune is softer and more absorbent, making the Magnite genuinely comfortable over broken city roads and expansion joints. Twin-tube telescopic dampers at the rear and a stiffer front spring rate, combined with 195/60 R16 rubber and 205 mm of ground clearance, let it shrug off potholes that would unsettle smaller rivals. Big sharp-edged hits can still produce a clunky thud, but it is a fair compromise for the everyday plushness gained. Handling is secure rather than engaging: the steering is slow, the brake pedal lacks initial bite, and the cramped pedal box discourages spirited driving. Vehicle Dynamic Control, traction control and hill-hold assist are standard on the turbo, and the 4.9-metre turning radius makes urban manoeuvring easy.

Build Quality & Technology

Build feels a clear step up over the pre-facelift car. Plastic rattles have been dialled down, window glass is thicker, and door shut quality has improved. Safety has moved from a strong point to a genuine USP: six airbags standard, three-point seatbelts for all, ISOFIX, ESP, hill-hold, TPMS and a 360-degree camera that no rival offers at this price. The Global NCAP 4-star adult and child rating remains. Feature niggles persist: no wireless charger from factory, no ventilated seats, no auto headlamps on lower variants, and the rear power windows operate slowly. Nissan's 3-year/1-lakh-km warranty and four free labour visits within the first two years keep the long-term equation friendly, with claimed maintenance of just 39 paise per km.

Price & Value

Value is where the Magnite is untouchable. The range opens at roughly 6 lakh ex-showroom and tops out around 13.5 lakh for the Tekna Plus, with the sweet-spot turbo-CVT landing well under what a comparably equipped Brezza, Sonet or Venue commands. MotorOctane's year-long ownership pegged the 10,000 km paid service at around Rs 1,900 all-in, and four free labour visits up to 20,000 km mean two years of running can be done for well under Rs 5,000. Resale and dealer reach trail Maruti and Hyundai, and Nissan's global restructuring adds a small dose of uncertainty, but the India operation has committed funding and an expanding network. For value-conscious first-time SUV buyers, nothing else comes close.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • Class-leading 360-degree camera and 4-star Global NCAP rating with six standard airbags
  • Spacious cabin that genuinely seats three at the rear, with 336-litre boot
  • Service costs are very low, with four free labour visits in the first two years
  • 1.0L naturally aspirated engine is adequate for city, the turbo-CVT is the one to buy

Points of Disagreement

  • Facelift styling: some find the new grille and bumper busier than the cleaner original, others welcome the freshness
  • NA engine refinement: opinion splits between 'acceptable for the price' and 'too coarse for city use with a full load'

TeamBHP's Take

TeamBHP

After 22,000 km and two years, the Magnite Turbo CVT delivers on value, safety and go-anywhere ground clearance, but rattles and small ergonomic misses persist.

What owners praise
  • 360 camera makes tight parking effortless: owner refuses to buy any future car without it
  • Suspension shrugged off multiple 60-90 km/h pothole hits with no damage to alloys or tyres
  • Cabin stays quiet up to 80-85 km/h on good tarmac, wind noise only audible past 90
  • Mechanical AC knobs for fan, temperature and circulation: tactile and far better than touch controls
What owners flag
  • Develops minor rattles over time and service centres have been useless at fixing them
  • LED headlamps become near-useless in rain or on wet roads, sealed unit means no bulb replacement
  • Cannot pause Spotify from the steering wheel, no auto door unlock or mirror retraction on engine off
  • Factory fog lamps are weak 4W units with non-standard shape, hard to find replacements

Long-term reality: Across 22,000 km including multiple Bangalore-Hyderabad and Bangalore-Mangalore runs, the seats held up for 5-hour stints without posture issues. The owner credits the TCS/ESP combo for saving a near-miss, and would buy again primarily for the 360 camera and ground clearance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Nissan Magnite?
Yes, if you want maximum SUV for the money with strong safety. Pick the turbo-CVT for the best all-round experience.
What is the Nissan Magnite price in India?
The 2025 Magnite range starts at roughly Rs 6 lakh ex-showroom and tops out around Rs 13.5 lakh for the Tekna Plus turbo-CVT.
What are the main problems with the Nissan Magnite?
Coarse NA engine under load, dated touchscreen, mushy brakes, smaller dealer network, and minor rattles developing after a year of use.
How is the Nissan Magnite mileage?
The NA petrol returns 13-15 km/l in city and up to 21 km/l on highway at 85 km/h. Turbo-CVT delivers around 17-19 km/l.
Is Nissan Magnite good for highway driving?
The turbo is a confident highway cruiser with strong 90-110 km/h roll-on. The NA copes but needs lower gears for overtakes with a full load.
How does Nissan Magnite compare to rivals?
It undercuts Brezza, Sonet, Venue and Nexon significantly while offering 360 camera, six airbags and 4-star NCAP as standard. Rivals beat it on dealer reach and refinement.
What is the boot space of Nissan Magnite?
The Magnite offers 336 litres of boot space, expanding to 690 litres with the 60:40 split rear seat folded down, plus a 14-inch spare wheel.
Is Nissan Magnite safe?
Yes, it scores 4 stars in Global NCAP for adult and child occupant safety, with six airbags, ESP, hill-hold, TPMS and three-point belts for all as standard.
