Nissan Magnite: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
7.4
Jury Score / 10

The facelifted Magnite blends genuine SUV practicality, 4-star safety and segment-best value with a meaningfully more premium cabin.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 13 May 2026 Synthesis of 7 independent sources 1,260 words · 5 min read

The 2025 Nissan Magnite is the sensible buyer's sub-4m SUV: spacious, safe, well-equipped and priced well below its rivals. The facelift adds a softer cabin, six airbags as standard and a 360-degree camera, while the turbo-CVT remains the pick of the range.

What Works

  • Strong value across the range, especially the turbo variants
  • Spacious cabin with practical 336L boot and 60:40 split rear
  • Four-star Global NCAP rating with six airbags standard
  • Affordable, transparent service experience with free labour for first four visits
  • Excellent LED headlight performance

Watch Out For

  • 1.0L naturally aspirated engine struggles with load and inclines
  • Touchscreen looks dated and occasionally hangs
  • Brakes feel mushy and steering is slow at low speeds
  • Nissan's smaller dealer network and global ownership uncertainty

Design

The Magnite still cuts a confident sub-4m SUV silhouette at 1.6m tall with 205mm of ground clearance and 16-inch dual-tone diamond-cut alloys on the top Tekna Plus. The facelift adds a new grille surround, redesigned bumper, L-shaped LED DRLs and updated tail-lamps with a 3D honeycomb gradient. Opinion splits here: the older car's clean lines had genuine appeal, and the new front end can look busy and heavy depending on the colour. The Sun Copper Orange and the more settled brown shade hide minor scratches well, a real-world plus in Indian traffic. Functional roof rails rated for 50kg, shark-fin antenna and integrated turn indicators on the electrically folding mirrors keep the package modern without going overboard.

Interior & Features

The cabin is where the facelift earns its money. Leatherette dash and door pads, orange stitching, ambient lighting and an auto-dimming IRVM lift perceived quality meaningfully, and plastic rattles that plagued the older car have largely been silenced. The 9-inch floating touchscreen gets wireless Android Auto and CarPlay but looks dated next to newer rivals, and the 360-degree camera feed is low-resolution. Space is a real strength: three adults fit across the rear, the floor hump is minimal, and rear AC vents, a USB port and ISOFIX mounts are all present. As MotorOctane notes, the Arkamys six-speaker audio punches above its price. Niggles remain: cruise control is offered but auto headlamps and ventilated seats are missed.

Performance & Powertrain

Two 1.0-litre three-cylinder petrols are on offer: a 71hp/96Nm naturally aspirated unit and a 98hp turbo making 160Nm with manual or 152Nm with CVT. Gagan Choudhary's verdict on the NA engine is blunt: it shudders at low revs, struggles on inclines with passengers aboard, and demands frequent downshifts above 2,500rpm to feel alive. City fuel economy sits at 12-15kmpl, with 21kmpl achievable on the highway at a steady 85kmph. The turbo-CVT is the pick: smoother, more sophisticated after a calmer retune, and genuinely fluid between 90 and 100kmph for overtakes. Real-world economy on the turbo is more modest at 9-11kmpl city and 16-18kmpl highway. NVH and refinement remain the engine family's weakest links.

Ride Quality & Handling

The facelifted suspension tune is softer and more absorbent than before, dealing well with broken Indian roads at city and moderate highway speeds. Large hits over expansion joints can still send a clunky thud into the cabin, but it is a fair compromise for the everyday comfort gained. The double-acting shock absorbers, MacPherson struts up front and twin-tube telescopic dampers at the rear keep body movements in check. The 5-metre turning radius is handy in tight urban parking. Where the Magnite still falls short is driver engagement: the steering is slow, the foot well is cramped for larger shoes, and the brakes feel mushy under firmer inputs, all of which dull confidence when hustling or threading through tight gaps.

Build Quality & Technology

Nissan has worked hardest under the skin. Better window sealing, improved sound insulation and tighter trim fits make the cabin noticeably quieter. The body uses 67 percent high-tensile steel, and the Magnite carries a 4-star Global NCAP rating with six airbags, ESC, hill-hold, traction control, TPMS and three-point belts for all five occupants as standard. Panel gaps are wide in places but consistent, and the leatherette surfaces feel durable. Niggles flagged by Namaste Car and others: no fuse-puller in the kit, plasticky parcel-tray hooks and a touchscreen that has hung at least once. The Arkamys 3D sound system, plasma-cluster auto AC, wireless charging accessory and PM2.5 cabin filter add genuine perceived value at this price.

Price & Value

Value is the Magnite's clearest argument. The range spans roughly Rs 6 lakh to Rs 11.5 lakh ex-showroom, with the second-from-base variant offering a 9-inch touchscreen, wireless smartphone mirroring and a rear wiper under Rs 8 lakh on-road. Nissan claims a 5-year running cost of 39 paise per kilometre, and MotorOctane's first service at 10,000km came to roughly Rs 1,900 all-in thanks to four free labour visits. Three-year/100,000km warranty is standard. The one caveat, flagged by the unknown reviewer, is Nissan's smaller dealer footprint and the uncertainty around the brand's global alliances; Nissan India insists India plans and funds are locked in, but it is a leap of faith some buyers will weigh carefully.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • Best value-for-money sub-4m SUV, undercuts rivals on equipment-for-rupee
  • 360-degree camera, six airbags and three-point belts standard add real safety credibility
  • Cabin is genuinely spacious, three adults fit in the rear with ease
  • LED headlight throw and spread is excellent, among the best in segment
  • Service costs are low, basic service comes in under Rs 2,000 with free labour

Points of Disagreement

  • Design: praised as classy and SUV-ish by some, called busy and heavy after the facelift by others
  • 1.0L NA engine: rated adequate for city use by some, called underpowered and rough in stop-go traffic by others

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Nissan Magnite?
Yes, if you want maximum SUV for the money with 4-star safety. Stretch for the turbo-CVT variant for the best all-round experience.
What is the Nissan Magnite price in India?
Roughly Rs 6 lakh to Rs 11.5 lakh ex-showroom, with the top Tekna Plus at around Rs 11.5 lakh ex-showroom.
What are the main problems with the Nissan Magnite?
Weak 1.0L NA engine, dated touchscreen that can hang, mushy brakes, slow steering and a smaller Nissan dealer network than rivals.
How is the Nissan Magnite mileage?
NA petrol returns 12-15kmpl city and up to 21kmpl highway. Turbo delivers 9-11kmpl city and 16-18kmpl highway in real driving.
Is Nissan Magnite good for highway driving?
The turbo-CVT cruises easily and overtakes fluidly between 90-100kmph. The NA engine works but feels strained with a full load.
How does Nissan Magnite compare to rivals?
It significantly undercuts Sonet, Venue, Brezza and Nexon while matching them on space and safety, but loses on refinement and brand network.
What is the boot space of Nissan Magnite?
336 litres with rear seats up, expanding to 690 litres with the 60:40 split folded. A 14-inch spare wheel sits below.
Is Nissan Magnite safe?
Yes. It holds a 4-star Global NCAP rating with six airbags, ESC, hill-hold, TPMS and three-point seatbelts for all five occupants as standard.
