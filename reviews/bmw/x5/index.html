BMW X5: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
8.2
Jury Score / 10

End-of-cycle X5 still leads its segment for driving feel, features and refinement, with dealer discounts sweetening the deal.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 20 May 2026 Synthesis of 5 independent sources 1,325 words · 6 min read

The 2026 BMW X5 is a facelifted G05 nearing the end of its lifecycle, but it remains the most driver-focused luxury SUV in its class. Priced from around Rs 94 lakh to Rs 1.37 crore on-road, it pairs a punchy 3.0-litre straight-six diesel or petrol with air suspension, a 14.9-inch curved display, and the largest features list in the segment. Niggles around fit-and-finish and UI complexity exist, but no rival blends comfort and dynamics this convincingly.

What Works

  • Brilliant 286 hp / 650 Nm diesel hits 0-100 km/h in claimed 6.1 seconds
  • Air suspension, xDrive and rear-biased setup deliver class-leading dynamics
  • Feature-loaded: 360 camera, HUD, 16-speaker Harman Kardon, four-zone climate, panoramic sunroof
  • Split tailgate, 650-litre boot and 40:20:40 folding rear seats
  • Real-world range close to 900 km on a single tank from the 80-litre fuel tank

Watch Out For

  • AC controls and key functions buried inside the touchscreen UI
  • 21-inch low-profile tyres make low-speed ride busy on Indian roads
  • Minor fit-and-finish lapses: loose dashboard panel, hanging steering thread
  • Rear seat backrest angle is not adjustable and feels upright

Design

The X5 has aged with grace. Faisal Khan notes the grille is correctly sized for the car and illuminates at night, which still looks special. The M Sport pack gets a sharper bumper, 21-inch Y-spoke alloys with staggered 275/40 and 315/35 rubber, and blue brake calipers. The split tailgate, quad-style exhaust tips and 3D LED tail lamps with welcome animation remain segment highlights. The Ruby Red paint on the Individual cars looks superb, though the fender vents are decorative. Compared to the Audi Q7 and Volvo XC90, the X5 looks the most athletic without descending into the polarising oversized-grille territory BMW has adopted elsewhere. At 4.9 m long with 214 mm ground clearance, road presence is exactly what buyers in this segment expect.

Interior & Features

The cabin mixes proper luxury with a few facelift missteps. The X5 branding on the passenger dash, crystal-cut iDrive knob, gear selector and start button feel suitably premium, and the 12.3-inch driver display plus 14.9-inch curved infotainment dominate the dashboard. BMW Individual leather, Alcantara headlining and ambient lighting in 15 colours lift the ambience. However, moving the AC controls into the touchscreen has made everyday operation fiddlier, the new key drops the older display-key functions, and small lapses like a hanging stitch on the steering and an ill-fitting dashboard panel are unbecoming at this price. Rear knee room is adequate but the backrest is upright and not adjustable. The 40:20:40 split folding seats and 650-litre boot remain genuinely practical.

Performance & Powertrain

This is where the X5 stays untouchable. The 3.0-litre B57 diesel produces 286 hp and 650 Nm, paired with a 48V mild-hybrid system adding 12 hp and 200 Nm of instant boost. Claimed 0-100 km/h is 6.1 seconds and real-world testing returns almost exactly that figure. The petrol xDrive40i makes 381 hp and dispatches the same run in 5.1 seconds. The ZF 8-speed torque converter shifts cleanly, the diesel revs all the way to 5,500 rpm with genuine eagerness, and turbo lag is well contained. Real-world fuel economy of 8-10 km/l combined with the 80-litre tank delivers close to 900 km of touring range. As Driven Plus notes on the global M60i, BMW's straight-six and V8 calibrations remain benchmarks for the segment.

Ride Quality & Handling

Air suspension with five ride-height modes is the X5's secret weapon on Indian roads, letting you lift the body over speed breakers and drop it for highway stability. Above 60 km/h the car automatically settles to level three. Handling is the standout: the steering is precise, body roll is controlled, and the rear-biased xDrive system makes the 2,400 kg SUV feel surprisingly agile, almost approaching Lamborghini Urus and Audi RSQ8 territory in commitment. The flip side is the 21-inch wheels with low-profile rubber, which let sharp expansion joints and broken patches into the cabin at low speeds. High-speed stability is exceptional, helped by the staggered, oversized tyres that border on supercar specification.

Build Quality & Technology

Equipment is comprehensive: adaptive LED headlights with welcome animation, heads-up display with AR navigation, 360-degree camera with drive recorder, eight airbags, ADAS with lane keep assist and AEB, automatic parking, gesture control, soft-close doors, four-zone climate, wireless charging, and a 464W 16-speaker Harman Kardon system. Gagan Choudhary points out that fit and finish has slipped slightly versus the pre-facelift car, with some plastics feeling thinner and the AC panel reduced to a small strip. The infotainment UI, while visually rich, is not as intuitive as it should be and key functions like rear sunshades have moved from physical switches into menus. For Indian buyers used to BMW's earlier polish, these are visible compromises.

Price & Value

Pricing runs from roughly Rs 94 lakh to Rs 1.08 crore ex-showroom, with the M Sport diesel landing near Rs 1.37 crore on-road in Mumbai. The M Sport commands roughly a Rs 16 lakh premium over the xLine but adds sportier bumpers, alloys and trim that most buyers will want. Against the Mercedes-Benz GLE, the X5 undercuts by a couple of lakhs and drives appreciably better; against the Audi Q7 and Volvo XC90 it asks a premium but offers stronger dynamics. With the G65 next-generation X5 due next year, dealers are now offering meaningful discounts, which strengthens the case considerably. For buyers wanting a fast, feature-loaded, driver-focused luxury SUV today, the value equation is finally working.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • 3.0-litre straight-six diesel is refined, fast-revving to 5,500 rpm and genuinely exciting
  • Steering and handling defy the X5's 2,400 kg kerb weight, making it the segment's driver's pick
  • Air suspension with five height modes is essential for Indian roads
  • 14.9-inch curved touchscreen looks premium but burying AC controls inside it hurts usability
  • Approaching end-of-life with the next-gen G65 due, so dealer discounts are now meaningful

Points of Disagreement

  • Whether the 21-inch wheels ruin low-speed ride quality or are a fair trade for grip and stance
  • Whether the new key, which loses the older display-key functions, is a downgrade or acceptable

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the BMW X5?
Yes, if you want the most driver-focused luxury SUV in the segment. With the next generation due in 2026, current dealer discounts make it especially attractive.
What is the BMW X5 price in India?
Ex-showroom prices range from around Rs 94 lakh to Rs 1.08 crore. The M Sport diesel costs about Rs 1.37 crore on-road in Mumbai.
What are the main problems with the BMW X5?
AC controls buried in touchscreen, minor fit-and-finish lapses on the facelift, busy low-speed ride on 21-inch wheels, and an upright non-adjustable rear backrest.
How is the BMW X5 mileage?
The 30d diesel returns 8-10 km/l in real driving against an ARAI claim of 12 km/l. The 80-litre tank gives around 900 km range.
Is BMW X5 good for highway driving?
Excellent. The straight-six diesel, air suspension, staggered 21-inch tyres and 243 km/h top speed make it exceptionally stable and quick on highways.
How does BMW X5 compare to rivals?
It drives better than the Mercedes GLE, Audi Q7 and Volvo XC90, undercuts the GLE on price, and matches them on features and luxury.
What is the boot space of BMW X5?
650 litres with rear seats up, expandable by folding the 40:20:40 split rear seats. Boot opens as an electric split tailgate.
Is BMW X5 safe?
Yes, with eight airbags, ABS, 360-degree camera, ADAS including lane keep assist and automatic emergency braking, and BMW's reinforced body structure.
