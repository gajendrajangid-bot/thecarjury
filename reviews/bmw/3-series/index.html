BMW 3 Series Gran Limousine: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
8.0
Jury Score / 10

A genuinely engaging driver's sedan with a long-wheelbase rear cabin that makes it practical for India, provided you can live with its quirks.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 19 May 2026 Synthesis of 6 independent sources 1,298 words · 5 min read

The BMW 3 Series Gran Limousine remains the segment's enthusiast pick, blending sharp rear-wheel-drive dynamics with a stretched cabin tailored for Indian rear-seat buyers. With 330i M Sport petrol, 320Ld diesel and the 374 hp M340i on offer, it covers everything from chauffeur duty to full-blooded performance.

What Works

  • 330i does 0-100 km/h in 5.8 seconds, M340i in 4.4 seconds
  • Long-wheelbase rear cabin with proper headroom and under-thigh support
  • ZF 8-speed gearbox picks the right ratio almost every time
  • Mild-hybrid system helps petrol return 14-15 km/l on highways
  • Three-zone climate control, wireless charging, gesture control and Harman Kardon audio

Watch Out For

  • No Android Auto, no passive keyless entry on doors
  • Wireless charging pad is too small for larger phones
  • Brake feedback is wooden and could be sharper
  • Centre tunnel makes the rear strictly a four-seater

Design

The Gran Limousine wears the G28 generation 3 Series design with a stretched 4.8-metre body and active kidney grille that opens based on cooling demand. The 330i M Sport adds a sportier front bumper, gloss black and chrome accents, M badging on the wheels and scuff plates, and 18-inch alloys wrapped in 255/40 R18 rubber, up from 17-inch on the 320Ld Luxury Line. Skyscraper Grey, Black, Blue and White are the colour options. The M340i is visually distinguished by its unique grille mesh, M-division wheels, a rear lip and a louder quad exhaust. Ground clearance sits at 165 mm on the 330i and 130 mm on the M340i, the latter still better than most sports sedans sold in India.

Interior & Features

The cabin mixes comfort with technology rather than chasing outright opulence. Up front, the 330i adds adjustable side bolsters, a thicker M Sport steering wheel and petrol-specific trim, while a 12.3-inch digital cluster and 14.9-inch infotainment screen anchor the dashboard. Three-zone climate, wireless charging, gesture control, Harman Kardon audio and ambient lighting are standard on higher trims. Rear seat space is the headline: under-thigh support and legroom are generous, and the scooped roofliner preserves headroom for six-footers, as Biturbo Media notes in the M340i. The drawbacks are real though: no 360-degree camera, no ventilated seats, no head-up display, a small wireless pad that rejects larger phones, and a centre tunnel that limits the rear bench to two adults.

Performance & Powertrain

Three powertrains are on sale. The 330i uses BMW's B48 2.0-litre twin-scroll turbo petrol with 258 hp and 400 Nm, paired with a mild-hybrid system, doing 0-100 km/h in 5.8 seconds. The 320Ld diesel makes 190 hp and clears the sprint in 7.6 seconds. The M340i sits at the top with a 3.0-litre straight-six producing 374 hp, all-wheel drive and a 4.4-second 0-100 time, with pops and bangs in Sport Plus. Across all three, the ZF 8-speed automatic is the unsung hero: Gagan Choudhary notes it picks the correct ratio almost every time. Turbo lag is minimal, throttle response is sharp, and the petrol still returns 8-9 km/l in city and 14-15 km/l on highways.

Ride Quality & Handling

India misses out on adaptive dampers, which is arguably a blessing. The fixed M Sport setup on the 330i is firm but never crashy, absorbing speed breakers and broken patches without complaint, helped by the taller-sidewall 18-inch rubber. The M340i's stiffer tune does transmit undulations even on good roads, though it remains usable daily, unlike harder-edged sports cars. Handling is where the 3 Series justifies its reputation: the electric steering weights up beautifully in Sport, the rear-driven 330i lets you place the nose precisely, and the all-wheel-drive M340i offers grip and composure that few cars under a crore can match. Brake feel is the weak link, lacking the bite enthusiasts expect from a driver's car.

Build Quality & Technology

Locally assembled at Chennai, the 3 Series feels solidly screwed together with consistent panel gaps and a doors-shut thunk that matches its German peers. Materials inside are a mix of Sensatec leatherette, Alcantara on M variants and soft-touch plastics on the upper dash. Safety includes six airbags, Dynamic Stability Control, Cornering Brake Control, ISOFIX, tyre pressure monitoring, attentiveness assistant and a reversing camera with parking assistant. The car carries a Euro NCAP five-star rating from its global testing. Service packages stretch up to 10 years or 2 lakh kilometres, and the resale value remains among the strongest in the segment, a point My Country My Ride flags as a real ownership advantage.

Price & Value

Pricing starts at roughly Rs 59 lakh ex-showroom for the 330i M Sport, Rs 60.5 lakh for the 320Ld Luxury Line and around Rs 75 lakh for the M340i, with BMW's financial services offering EMIs from about Rs 60,000 a month and bumper-to-bumper plans up to five years. Against the Mercedes-Benz E-Class, which leans chauffeur, and the larger BMW 5 Series LWB, the 3 Series Gran Limousine carves out the driver-focused niche. The Audi A6 in current form is less directly competitive. Within the BMW family, buyers cross-shopping SUVs will look at the X1 and X3. The omissions hurt at this price, but for driving enthusiasts the package remains compelling.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • ZF 8-speed automatic and turbocharged engines deliver near-flawless drivetrain behaviour
  • Long-wheelbase Gran Limousine format gives genuinely usable rear-seat legroom
  • Steering feel and chassis balance remain class-leading under Rs 1 crore
  • Ride quality on 18-inch wheels handles Indian roads without drama
  • NVH isolation is excellent on both petrol and diesel variants

Points of Disagreement

  • Whether the 330i's price premium over the 320Ld is justified given similar real-world usability
  • Whether the M340i's firmer suspension is acceptable for daily Indian commuting

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the BMW 3 Series Gran Limousine?
Yes, if you prioritise driving pleasure with rear-seat practicality. The 330i M Sport suits enthusiasts, the 320Ld suits chauffeur use, and the M340i suits performance buyers.
What is the BMW 3 Series Gran Limousine price in India?
The 330i M Sport starts at around Rs 59 lakh ex-showroom, the 320Ld Luxury Line at Rs 60.5 lakh, and the M340i at roughly Rs 75 lakh.
What are the main problems with the BMW 3 Series Gran Limousine?
No Android Auto, no passive keyless entry, a small wireless charging pad, wooden brake feedback, and a centre tunnel limiting rear seating to two.
How is the BMW 3 Series Gran Limousine mileage?
The 330i petrol returns 8-9 km/l in city and 14-15 km/l on highways thanks to mild-hybrid assistance. The 320Ld diesel delivers around 19 km/l.
Is BMW 3 Series Gran Limousine good for highway driving?
Yes, excellent. NVH isolation is strong, the ZF 8-speed cruises effortlessly, and handling stability at triple-digit speeds is among the best in the segment.
How does BMW 3 Series Gran Limousine compare to rivals?
It is sharper to drive than the Mercedes E-Class and offers more rear space than a standard 3 Series. The Audi A6 is less directly competitive currently.
What is the boot space of BMW 3 Series Gran Limousine?
The boot offers around 450 litres of usable space with good depth, sufficient for two large suitcases plus weekend luggage for four passengers.
Is BMW 3 Series Gran Limousine safe?
Yes. It carries a five-star Euro NCAP rating, with six airbags, Dynamic Stability Control, Cornering Brake Control, ISOFIX mounts and a reversing camera as standard.
