Mahindra Thar: The Jury's Verdict

BUY
8.0
Jury Score / 10

The Thar Roxx delivers genuine 4x4 hardware, SUV presence, and a feature-loaded cabin at a price no rival can match, provided you accept ladder-frame compromises.

By The Car Jury Editorial 4 July 2026 Synthesis of 7 independent sources 6 min read

The Mahindra Thar Roxx is the five-door, family-friendly answer to the three-door Thar's practicality problem. It keeps the rugged body-on-frame character and genuine 4x4 hardware while adding a panoramic sunroof, Level 2 ADAS, ventilated seats and a 10.25-inch dual-screen setup. Priced between roughly Rs 14.5 lakh and Rs 27 lakh on-road, it undercuts every rival with comparable off-road credentials.

What Works

  • Strong road presence and genuine SUV stance
  • Refined diesel and torque-converter automatic combination
  • Long features list including 360-degree camera, wireless charging and Harman Kardon audio
  • Real four-wheel drive with locking diff and Intelli Turn Assist
  • Aggressive pricing versus Scorpio N, Gurkha and imported rivals

Watch Out For

  • Wind noise post 80 km/h and continuous vertical movement over patchy roads
  • Front footwell is cramped for six-footers; no reach adjustment on steering
  • Door pockets too small for a standard one-litre bottle
  • Petrol returns single-digit fuel economy in the city

Design

The Thar Roxx retains the boxy, upright silhouette that made the three-door a cult product, but stretches to 4,428mm in length on a 2,850mm wheelbase. C-shaped LED DRLs, a six-slat grille (deliberately not seven, to keep Jeep's lawyers away), exposed hinges and Easter eggs like camels and cacti give it visual character. Faisal Khan notes the angled C-pillar cut and hidden rear door handles do not sit perfectly with the older body lines, and the 19-inch alloy design divides opinion. Ground clearance stays at 226mm, approach angle at 41.2 degrees, and the wading depth is a genuine 650mm. It looks down on a Scorpio N from the driver's seat, and that presence is the single biggest reason buyers will sign the cheque.

Interior & Features

The dual-tone black and ivory cabin borrows heavily from the XUV 3XO and XUV700: 10.25-inch touchscreen, matching digital cluster, circular AC vents and soft-touch dashboard with double-stitching. Ventilated leatherette seats, wireless charging, 65W USB-C, panoramic sunroof, electronic parking brake and a nine-speaker Harman Kardon system are all standard on the top variant. But the practicality misses are real: door pockets cannot hold a one-litre bottle, the driver seat lacks reach adjustment, front footwell is cramped for anyone above six feet, and the ivory upholstery shows dirt within days. Rear seats recline and slide, and knee room is decent for 5'8" passengers, but headroom and under-thigh support tighten quickly for taller occupants.

Build Quality & Technology

Safety: crash rating awaitedThis car has not been crash-tested by Bharat NCAP or Global NCAP as per our data.

Fit and finish is a genuine improvement for Mahindra: soft-touch dashboard plastics, consistent panel gaps, well-damped doors and leather with double-stitching feel a segment above what the brand delivered five years ago. Six airbags, ESP, hill descent, TPMS and a 360-degree camera are standard. Level 2 ADAS covers 10 features including adaptive cruise, forward collision warning, autonomous emergency braking and blind view monitor, but blind spot alert and leading vehicle departure warning are missing. The MotorInc review points out that door pocket ergonomics, the sliding-only asymmetric glovebox and the lack of a passive entry request sensor on early cars are the kind of niggles that reveal the price point. Overall build is Mahindra's best effort yet in this segment.

Performance & Powertrain

Two engines carry over from the Scorpio N and XUV700 family: a 2.2-litre diesel making 172hp/370Nm in top trim and a 2.0-litre mStallion turbo-petrol producing 175hp/380Nm. Both pair with a six-speed manual or six-speed torque-converter automatic. The Namaste Car walkaround confirms only the diesel gets four-wheel drive; the petrol is rear-wheel drive only. The diesel is impressively refined, almost petrol-like at idle, and pulls linearly rather than in a violent surge. The automatic is smooth but not quick, and paddle shifters are absent. Petrol buyers should expect 7-9 kmpl in the city and 11-13 kmpl on the highway; the diesel is meaningfully better at 8-10 kmpl city and 13-17 kmpl highway. Fuel tank is 57 litres.

Ride Quality & Handling

The new lighter ladder-frame, wider track, longer wheelbase and frequency-dependent damping deliver a clear step-up over the three-door Thar. At highway speeds the Roxx feels planted, the electric power steering is light and precise, and body roll is contained enough to hustle it through corners. However, this is still a body-on-frame SUV: sharp patchwork and expansion joints send vertical movement through the cabin, and long-term owner Gagan Choudhary reports CEAT tyres transmit sharper impacts than the alternative MRFs. Wind noise builds noticeably past 80 km/h thanks to the upright windshield. Brakes are strong but the pedal is soft and ABS calibration under hard braking could be sharper. It out-rides a Scorpio N over broken roads but cannot match an XUV700.

Price & Value

This is where the Thar Roxx demolishes the argument. The petrol RWD manual starts at roughly Rs 14.5-15 lakh on-road and the fully-loaded diesel 4x4 automatic tops out around Rs 27 lakh on-road in Mumbai. That undercuts a comparably-equipped Scorpio N, makes the Force Gurkha look under-specified, and puts a genuine Wrangler-style SUV within reach at less than a third of the imported car's price. V3Cars notes the on-road pricing varies significantly by city and top trims cross Rs 30 lakh in some states. Against siblings, buyers cross-shopping the Scorpio N and XUV700 will find the Roxx offers more character and off-road ability, though the XUV700 remains the more sensible family choice.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • Genuine 4x4 capability with electronic locking differential and 650mm water wading depth
  • Feature-loaded cabin punches well above its price with panoramic sunroof, ventilated seats and Level 2 ADAS
  • Ride quality is a big step up over the three-door Thar but still not as settled as a monocoque SUV
  • Both petrol and diesel engines are refined, smooth and well-suited to the automatic gearbox
  • Wind noise becomes intrusive above 80 km/h due to the boxy, upright profile

Points of Disagreement

  • Whether the exterior design improves on the three-door: some reviewers prefer the shorter car's proportions
  • Whether the 10 ADAS features are sufficient, given Hyundai and Kia offer up to 20 in this price band

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Mahindra Thar?
Yes, if you want genuine 4x4 ability, SUV road presence and a feature-loaded cabin. Skip it if you want monocoque-smooth ride quality.
What is the Mahindra Thar price in India?
The Thar Roxx ranges from roughly Rs 14.5 lakh on-road for the petrol base to about Rs 27 lakh on-road for the top diesel 4x4 automatic.
What are the main problems with the Mahindra Thar?
Wind noise above 80 km/h, cramped front footwell for tall drivers, small door pockets that cannot hold a one-litre bottle, and single-digit petrol economy in city.
How is the Mahindra Thar mileage?
Diesel returns 8-10 kmpl in city and 13-17 kmpl on highway. Petrol delivers 7-9 kmpl city and 11-13 kmpl highway.
Is Mahindra Thar good for highway driving?
Yes, the Roxx cruises confidently with light steering and stable composure, but wind noise past 80 km/h and vertical movement over patches remain.
How does Mahindra Thar compare to rivals?
It undercuts the Scorpio N on price, offers more character than the XUV700, and out-features the Force Gurkha while providing genuine 4x4 credentials.
What is the boot space of Mahindra Thar?
The Thar Roxx offers approximately 644 litres of boot space, significantly larger than the three-door and usable for family luggage.
Is Mahindra Thar safe?
The Roxx gets six airbags, ESP, ABS with EBD, 360-degree camera and Level 2 ADAS as standard. The three-door Thar is Global NCAP four-star rated.
