Mahindra XUV400 EV: The Jury's Verdict

WAIT
7.0
Jury Score / 10

Solid performance and low running costs are undermined by an unfinished cabin, soft suspension at speed, and Mahindra's newer EVs offering a more complete package.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 13 May 2026 Synthesis of 6 independent sources 1,344 words · 6 min read

The Mahindra XUV400 EV is the brand's first electric SUV, based on the XUV300 with a 39.4 kWh battery, 147 hp and a claimed 456 km range. It delivers strong performance and excellent running costs but feels rushed, with a dated cabin and soft suspension that betray its placeholder status in Mahindra's EV lineup.

What Works

  • Instant torque, 0-100 km/h in a claimed 8.3 seconds
  • Service cost effectively zero at 10,000 km, running cost around Rs 1-2/km
  • Larger 378-418 litre boot versus the XUV300
  • 50 kW DC fast charging takes battery to 80% in about 50 minutes
  • Six airbags, ESP and traction control now standard on long-range variant

Watch Out For

  • Cabin design and plastics feel dated and patchwork
  • No rear AC vents, no manual regen level selection
  • Suspension turns soft and bouncy above moderate speeds
  • Drive modes are poorly calibrated: base mode capped at 90 km/h, Fearless feels excessive

Design

The XUV400 EV is essentially a stretched, electrified XUV300 with copper accents replacing chrome and a closed-off grille for aerodynamics. It is 200 mm longer than the XUV300 and the widest car in its segment, wider even than the Hyundai Kona. New 16-inch diamond-cut alloys, copper Twin Peaks badging, a copper-finish roof option and revised LED tail lamps mark it out, though MotorBeam notes the headlight unit and overall silhouette are carried over wholesale. The result looks tidy but unmistakably last-generation, especially against Mahindra's own newer 3XO and BE6. The bronze highlights work best on the roof; the rest feels like a styling patch rather than a ground-up EV design.

Interior & Features

The cabin is where the XUV400 most clearly shows its rushed origins. The dashboard architecture is lifted from the XUV300, hard plastics dominate, and the mix of old switchgear with new dual 10.25-inch screens looks like a patchwork. The perforated leatherette seats with copper stitching feel genuinely premium, the steering is nicely wrapped, and the new shift-by-wire selector with P, R, N, D and Lively (single-pedal) modes is a neat touch. But there are no rear AC vents, no auto-dimming IRVM on lower trims, regen levels cannot be selected manually, and wireless smartphone connectivity is promised only as an update. Rear headroom is good but the low seat base limits long-distance comfort for adults.

Performance & Powertrain

The 39.4 kWh battery feeds a permanent magnet synchronous motor producing 147 hp and 310 Nm, with a claimed 0-100 km/h in 8.3 seconds and a 150 km/h top speed. On Mahindra's proving track the car genuinely felt that quick, with MotorBeam clocking 160 km/h indicated in Fearless mode. Power delivery is smooth and linear, NVH is excellent, and the Lively mode enables effective one-pedal driving. The catch is calibration: Fun mode is capped around 90 km/h forcing a jump to Fast for highways, while Fearless feels unnecessarily aggressive. Faisal Khan noted the steering's effortless lightness masks the roughly 1.5-tonne kerb weight well. A 34.5 kWh variant with the same outputs but 375 km claimed range is also offered.

Ride Quality & Handling

Ride and handling are the XUV400's weakest dynamic area. At city speeds the suspension absorbs potholes well and ground clearance remains SUV-like despite the floor-mounted battery. Push harder and the setup turns soft, with noticeable lurching, bouncing and body roll on undulating roads. The steering is exceptionally light, which suits parking and traffic but offers little feedback at highway speeds. Mahindra added ESP and traction control after launch to stop the original car spinning its front wheels under power, and that has made the XUV400 meaningfully safer to drive briskly. But the chassis was never re-engineered for the extra battery mass, and on unfamiliar roads it pays to stay calm with the throttle.

Build Quality & Technology

Build quality is mixed. Panel gaps are acceptable and the structure is crash-compliant with pyro switches for battery safety, IP67-rated motor and battery pack, and Mahindra claims 98.2% motor efficiency. The long-range variant gets six airbags, ABS with EBD, ISOFIX, TPMS, hill-hold assist and a 360-degree-style reverse camera. The new NXT connected car suite adds over 55 remote features including geo-fencing, find-my-car and nearest charging point alerts. Battery and motor carry an 8-year/160,000 km warranty. However, hard plastics dominate the cabin, the instrument cluster software felt buggy on test cars, and the patchwork of old and new switchgear suggests parts-bin engineering rather than a clean-sheet EV.

Price & Value

Value is the XUV400's strongest pitch. MotorOctane's 10,000 km service bill came to roughly Rs 700, mostly for an AC filter and wiper fluid, with running costs around Rs 1-2 per km against Rs 8-12 for petrol equivalents. Over six to seven months, savings of around Rs 1.1 lakh on fuel alone are realistic for high-mileage users. The 50 kW DC fast charging and 8-year battery warranty add peace of mind. Against the Tata Nexon EV Max and Hyundai Kona it offers more size for the money, though the MG ZS EV feels more premium inside. The bigger question is internal: Mahindra's newer BE6 and even a 3XO diesel make a stronger case today.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • Real-world range sits around 260-300 km in mixed city use, well below the 456 km claim
  • Acceleration is genuinely brisk and the drive is smooth, silent and effortless
  • Cabin design and quality feel dated, lifted largely from the XUV300
  • Running and maintenance costs are dramatically lower than petrol or diesel equivalents
  • Boot space at 378-418 litres is a clear improvement over the XUV300

Points of Disagreement

  • Reviewers split on highway suitability: some find the performance and fast charging adequate for long trips, others say the soft suspension and light steering undermine confidence at speed
  • Opinions diverge on whether the XUV400 is worth buying today or whether the newer BE6 and 3XO diesel make better sense

TeamBHP's Take

TeamBHP

TeamBHP owners echo the running-cost story, with real-world range settling around 260-300 km in city use. Common gripes mirror the press: dated cabin, missing rear vents, no manual regen control, and soft high-speed ride. Service experience and low maintenance are widely praised.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Mahindra XUV400 EV?
Only if running costs and size for money are your top priorities. Mahindra's newer BE6 and even a 3XO diesel are more complete packages today.
What is the Mahindra XUV400 EV price in India?
Prices were not confirmed in the source reviews; expect positioning against the Tata Nexon EV Max, with the long-range variant at a premium.
What are the main problems with the Mahindra XUV400 EV?
Dated cabin lifted from the XUV300, no rear AC vents, no manual regen selection, soft suspension at speed and poorly calibrated drive modes.
How is the Mahindra XUV400 EV mileage?
Claimed range is 456 km (39.4 kWh) or 375 km (34.5 kWh). Real-world figures settle around 260-300 km in mixed city driving.
Is Mahindra XUV400 EV good for highway driving?
Performance and 50 kW fast charging help, but soft suspension, light steering and limited real-world range mean it is better suited to city use.
How does Mahindra XUV400 EV compare to rivals?
Bigger and wider than the Tata Nexon EV and Hyundai Kona, with strong performance, but the MG ZS EV feels more premium inside.
What is the boot space of Mahindra XUV400 EV?
Boot space is 378-418 litres depending on source, a clear improvement over the XUV300 and competitive in the compact electric SUV segment.
Is Mahindra XUV400 EV safe?
Yes. The long-range variant gets six airbags, ESP, traction control, hill-hold, TPMS and an 8-year/160,000 km battery warranty with pyro safety switch.
