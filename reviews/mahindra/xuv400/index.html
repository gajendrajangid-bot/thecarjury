Mahindra XUV400 EV: The Jury's Verdict

WAIT
7.0
Jury Score / 10

A competent but unfinished first EV from Mahindra; the newer BE6 and XEV 9E are more complete propositions.

By The Car Jury Editorial Published 13 May 2026 Synthesis of 6 independent sources 1,350 words · 6 min read

The Mahindra XUV400 EV is the brand's first electric SUV, built on a stretched XUV300 platform with a 39.4 kWh battery, 147 hp and a claimed 456 km range. It delivers strong straight-line performance, low running costs and decent space, but feels rushed in cabin design, ride polish and feature integration.

What Works

  • Strong, refined electric performance with instant torque
  • Claimed 456 km ARAI range and 50 kW DC fast charging in 50 minutes
  • Larger 378-litre boot versus the XUV300 it is based on
  • Very low running cost at roughly Rs 1-2 per km
  • Eight-year/1,60,000 km battery and motor warranty

Watch Out For

  • Cabin design, plastics and screen integration feel unfinished
  • No rear AC vents, a glaring omission in this segment
  • Regen levels cannot be manually selected
  • Soft suspension causes lurch and bounce at higher speeds
  • Light, feel-less steering reduces highway confidence

Design

The XUV400 EV is essentially a stretched XUV300 with EV-specific cues: a closed grille, copper-finished Twin Peaks logo, copper accents on the bumper, door cladding and satin roof, plus 16-inch diamond-cut alloys wrapped in 205/65 R16 rubber. It is 200 mm longer than the donor car and is the widest in its segment, sitting wider than even the Hyundai Kona. The LED tail-lamps are larger but the overall silhouette looks dated next to Mahindra's newer 3XO. MotorBeam notes the absence of fog-lamps despite the XUV300 offering them. It is a functional makeover rather than a clean-sheet EV design, and against the bolder Mahindra BE6 it already looks a generation behind.

Interior & Features

Inside, the cabin layout mirrors the XUV300 with a black theme, perforated leather seats, blue stitching and copper accents on the AC vents and gear knob. The dual 10.25-inch screens for infotainment and instrumentation are sharp, and equipment now includes wireless charging, dual-zone climate, sunroof, auto-dimming IRVM and the AdrenoX connected suite with 55-plus features. However, hard plastics dominate, the mix of old and new buttons looks like a patchwork, and wireless Android Auto and Apple CarPlay are promised only via update. Rear AC vents are missing entirely. The rear bench is wide enough for three but the flat squab and reclined backrest hurt long-distance comfort, though headroom is excellent and the 378-litre boot is genuinely useful.

Performance & Powertrain

The 39.4 kWh lithium-ion pack drives a permanent-magnet synchronous motor producing 147 hp and 310 Nm, with a claimed 0-100 km/h of 8.3 seconds and a 150 km/h top speed. A smaller 34.5 kWh option offers the same outputs but only 375 km of range. Power delivery is smooth, silent and linear, with sound insulation good enough to keep the cabin calm. The three drive modes, Fun, Fast and Fearless, also raise the top-speed cap, with Fun limited to 90 km/h, which forces highway drivers into Fast. MotorBeam clocked 160 km/h indicated on a test track. The throttle calibration draws criticism for feeling dull initially then too urgent under deeper inputs, and regen levels change with the mode rather than via a manual selector.

Ride Quality & Handling

Ride and handling are where the XUV400 EV feels least resolved. The front MacPherson and rear twist-beam setup, carrying around 1.5 tonnes of battery-laden body, soaks up potholes well at city speeds and ground clearance is on par with the 3XO. Push harder and the suspension feels under-damped: the car lurches, bounces and gets unsettled over undulations taken at pace. The electric power steering is extremely light, weights up only mildly in Fast and Fearless modes, and offers little feedback, making fast driving on unfamiliar roads a wary affair. Body roll is evident through corners. ESP and traction control, added with the Pro update, have made the car safer and more predictable, but the smaller 3XO remains the more composed driver's tool.

Build Quality & Technology

Build quality is a mixed bag. Panel gaps are acceptable and the perforated leather upholstery and chunky grab handles feel solid, but hard plastics dominate the touchpoints and the cabin lacks the premium feel buyers in this price bracket increasingly expect. Safety is comprehensive on paper: six airbags (standard only on the long-range variant), ABS with EBD, ISOFIX, TPMS, hill-hold, a pyro switch for battery isolation, IP67-rated motor and pack, and a crash-compliant structure. The XUV300 donor scored five stars in Global NCAP. Mahindra backs the battery and motor with an eight-year or 1,60,000 km warranty and uses long-life transmission oil rated for 1,20,000 km. The infotainment software, however, still feels like a work in progress, with key connectivity features promised via later updates.

Price & Value

On running costs, the XUV400 EV is hard to argue with. MotorOctane logged 11,000 km in six to seven months at roughly Rs 1-2 per km, claiming Rs 1.1 lakh in fuel savings already, and their first paid service bill came to around Rs 700 covering only an AC filter, wiper fluid and a PPF repair. There is no engine oil, no oil filter and brake and tyre life roughly doubles thanks to regenerative braking. Against the Tata Nexon EV Max and Hyundai Kona, the XUV400 offers more length, width and boot space, and 50 kW DC fast charging takes the pack to 80 percent in 50 minutes. The catch is resale uncertainty, insurance for EVs and a product that still feels half a generation behind Mahindra's own BE6 and XEV 9E.

What India's Reviewers Agree On

Consensus

  • 39.4 kWh battery with a claimed 456 km range; real-world 260-280 km in city use
  • 147 hp and 310 Nm deliver effortless, smooth acceleration with a claimed 0-100 in 8.3 seconds
  • Running and maintenance costs are dramatically lower than petrol or diesel equivalents
  • Interior design and material quality feel dated and below segment expectations
  • Three drive modes, Fun, Fast and Fearless, alter steering, throttle and regen but cannot be set manually

Points of Disagreement

  • Real-world range estimates vary from 260 km to nearly 400 km depending on driving style
  • Whether the ride is acceptable or unsettled at speed splits opinion sharply

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I buy the Mahindra XUV400 EV?
Wait. It is competent and cheap to run, but the cabin, ride polish and Mahindra's own newer BE6 and XEV 9E make it hard to recommend today.
What is the Mahindra XUV400 EV price in India?
Pricing was not confirmed in the source reviews; expect it to sit close to the Tata Nexon EV Max bracket. Check Mahindra for current ex-showroom rates.
What are the main problems with the Mahindra XUV400 EV?
Dated interior with hard plastics, no rear AC vents, soft suspension at speed, light feel-less steering and regen levels that cannot be manually selected.
How is the Mahindra XUV400 EV mileage?
ARAI claims 456 km on the 39.4 kWh pack and 375 km on the 34.5 kWh. Real-world city range is around 260-280 km.
Is Mahindra XUV400 EV good for highway driving?
Performance is effortless and 50 kW fast charging helps, but light steering and unsettled high-speed ride mean it is best driven calmly on highways.
How does Mahindra XUV400 EV compare to rivals?
It is longer, wider and taller than the Tata Nexon EV with a bigger 378-litre boot, but trails the MG ZS EV on cabin polish.
What is the boot space of Mahindra XUV400 EV?
378 litres, significantly larger than the XUV300 it is based on, with a steel spare wheel and tool kit stored beneath the floor.
Is Mahindra XUV400 EV safe?
Yes. Six airbags on the long-range variant, ESP, traction control, ABS with EBD, hill-hold, TPMS, IP67-rated battery and a Global NCAP five-star donor platform.
